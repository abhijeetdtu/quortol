
## THE LEDE

At 3:47 a.m., the agent rebooted the database cluster, cleared the cache, and rerouted traffic through three different services without waking a single human engineer. It worked. The incident was resolved. The agent had succeeded.

But only because the agent had been trained on a narrow set of scenarios and deliberately constrained to execute no more than ten decision steps. Only because the bank's compliance layer had built in a safety check that prevented any action involving customer funds. Only because the agent was a member of a network of specialized systems, not a single entity operating across the entire organization.

At Commerzbank, the agent named Ava handles more than 30,000 customer conversations per month. It resolves about 75% of requests autonomously. But Ava is not the kind of agent that the industry has been promising for five years. Ava is the kind of agent that the industry has been building for five years.

In the same week, at Wix Engineering, another agent named AirBot generated 180 candidate pull requests for fixing pipeline failures. Of those, 28 were merged without human intervention. That's 15% full automation. Six hundred seventy-five engineering hours were saved per month. But Wix engineers still reviewed every PR. They still made the final decision.

And in a third place, at Stripe, the internal agents called Minions now merge over 1,000 pull requests weekly. They operate in fire-and-forget mode. They resolve 30% of all bugs autonomously during "Atlas Fix-It Week." But the system is so heavily layered with security controls, sandboxing, and human review that no Minion has ever made a change without someone having signed off on what it was supposed to do.

These are not the agents we were promised. They are the agents we got. They are real. They are limited. They are already reshaping work. And the people building them, and the people whose jobs they touch, are already living through the consequences.

## THE NUT GRAF

The question of whether AI will replace human workers has been answered, at least in the short term: it already has. Not at the scale that some alarmists predicted. Not at the speed that some optimists hoped. But at a rate that is visible in the data, visible in the layoffs, visible in the lives of the people who are now on the job market without a ladder to climb.

By 2025, 57% of companies had deployed AI agents in production. By early 2026, Google reported 8 million paid seats of Gemini Enterprise, and Microsoft counted 160,000 organizations using Copilot Studio, collectively creating 400,000 custom agents. Yet these agents fail 40-70% of complex professional tasks. Early-career workers in AI-exposed occupations declined 16% relative to peers. Goldman Sachs projected 6-7% workforce displacement during the transition period.

The real question is not whether AI will replace workers. It is whether workers will remain agents in the full sense—agents of their own purposes—or whether they will become supervisors of systems that have taken on work previously exclusive to human judgment, without their consent, their creativity, or their mortality.

## THE HUMAN STORIES

### THE LAYOFFS THAT CAME SOONER THAN EXPECTED

Donald King was 26 when he was laid off from PwC, where he had been building AI agents. He told Fortune in November 2025 that he was "blindsided." He had been working 80 hours a week building systems that he now recognized might be connected to the client layoffs that followed. He had just been made redundant by a family of tools he had helped design.

Debbie O'Brien joined Block to build AI. Weeks later, AI cost her job. O'Brien, a senior staff developer relations engineer in Applied AI at Block based in Mallorca, Spain, had more than 15 years of experience in frontend development and over 20,000 LinkedIn followers. She says the team was "on the bleeding edge," rapidly shipping automations from tools that generate release notes and videos in minutes to agentic flows that let users order food through ChatGPT, powered by Block's payments rails.

On a Thursday, Jack Dorsey, CEO and founder of Block, announced plans to cut more than 4,000 jobs, roughly 40% of the workforce. O'Brien was in a training session when a manager abruptly ended the call and instructed everyone to check their email. Hours passed without clarity. "I don't know. Do I have a job? Do I not have a job?" she recalls. Near 12:30 a.m., she said, "I opened the computer, and I just looked, and there I saw the DocuSign, basically the official termination process, and that I read through and had to sign."

"I'm much more pessimistic about all of it than many other people probably are," said Ivan Ureña-Valdes, a former data analyst at Block who had survived three rounds of layoffs. "I've felt the rumblings of AI disruption for a while now, especially since Anthropic launched Opus 4.5 late last year. I could see in my own work very quickly how much of it was already being automated."

Jill, a technical writer at Oracle for three decades, drove to the hospital for overdue back surgery when she received a call from her manager. Last year, Oracle had asked her and others on her team to document some of their workflows in order to train the company's AI systems. Now, her manager told her over the phone, she had been laid off. The sudden firing imperiled not only her ability to pay for post-surgery prescriptions, but her entire retirement plan. For years, she had received high performance scores from her managers and was rewarded with restricted stock (RSUs) in lieu of bonuses. But those RSUs were tied to a vesting schedule, meaning when she was fired, $300,000 worth vanished overnight.

"Some of the first victims of the company's aggressive strategy are its now-former employees," said Cynthia Sloan, a former senior director of technical writing at Oracle. "This is a job that I was so dedicated to for 19 years and gave everything to—and none of it matters. It makes me feel like I don't want to go back to another tech job. I want to work somewhere where I'm treated as a human again and not as a line on a spreadsheet or a work machine."

### THE TEEN EMPLOYMENT CRISIS

In August 2000, 52.3% of teenagers aged 16 to 19 were part of the labor market. By August 2025, that figure had plummeted to 34.8%. This dramatic decline of over 17 percentage points in a single generation is an economic alarm bell.

Bethan, a recent college graduate, lost her university IT helpdesk job when it was replaced with an AI kiosk. She tried to defend her role, arguing that for students who didn't speak English as a first language, or older students who weren't computer-literate, having humans behind the desk may still be necessary. "They went ahead with it because they said they had to get a certain number of cuts to the budget."

"It was awful," she says. "The helpdesk staff tried to defend their roles. It was awful."

The crisis in teen employment isn't happening in a vacuum. It is a direct consequence of a much larger, technology-driven shift in the labor market. As automation and AI begin to disrupt more skilled, higher-paying sectors, the displaced workers from those fields are forced to seek employment elsewhere. This creates a domino effect, pushing more experienced and qualified adults down the economic ladder into the very entry-level jobs once reserved for teenagers.

When automation eliminates jobs in sectors like manufacturing or warehousing, experienced adult workers are forced to seek employment elsewhere. This "downward displacement" often leads them to apply for entry-level positions in retail and food service that they might have previously overlooked. Because they have more extensive work histories, they are often hired over less experienced teenage applicants.

### THE COPYWRITERS AND DESIGNERS

Lucy, a 28-year-old copywriter, is one of the young workers already bearing the brunt of AI. She has worked for a major financial company for the past four years but soon will be without a job. Last summer, she experienced what any worker fears—a sudden meeting invite with no explanation. It quickly became clear it was bad news.

"It was on a big call with everyone who might be affected. The initial call was just someone talking at us, saying: 'This is what is happening.' There wasn't really any detail about why."

"Across marketing, it's around 50 people who were impacted by the restructure. This increased talk of AI has happened at pretty much exactly the same time," says Lucy, who preferred not to use her real name.

"The job market is pretty rough, and from my own job hunting so far, it's not easy, so I am quite worried about finding something after this. I'm on £48,000, which I'm not expecting to get again any time soon."

In the same field, Joanna, 36, is a lead graphic designer for TV shows airing on BBC, ITV, Netflix, and Apple TV. "They're using AI to cut corners where before they might have employed an assistant. The impact that has on people like me, the lead designers, is that we are worked harder. I'm getting the jobs, but I'm seeing the squeeze everywhere. It's happening bit by bit."

"I'm concerned for my own mental health. The whole middle, which makes up all teams, is being cut out. That leaves very, very stressed heads of departments, no work for people in the middle climbing up, and very stressed juniors."

## THE TECH THAT WORKS

### WIX AIRBOT

Wix Engineering published a detailed case study on AirBot, an on-call AI agent that diagnoses Airflow pipeline failures, analyzes logs, and generates remediation pull requests—automatically, inside Slack, at production scale. The numbers are specific and independently verifiable: 4,200 successful flows per month, 66% positive feedback from engineers, 180 candidate PRs generated, 28 merged without human intervention, and 675 engineering hours saved every month across 60 engineers in 30 Slack channels.

At $0.30 per interaction, the unit economics are compelling. This is what agentic AI looks like when it moves past the demo phase.

When a pipeline fails, AirBot receives the alert, classifies the failure type, retrieves the relevant logs and schema context, runs a root cause analysis using a large language model, and posts a diagnostic report directly into the team's Slack channel. In many cases, it goes further: it generates a pull request with a proposed fix, routes the alert to the team that owns the affected table or pipeline, and invites the on-call engineer to review rather than investigate.

The Wix data engineering context is highly specialized: 3,500+ Airflow pipelines with custom operators, a 7-petabyte data lake, specific table ownership conventions, and internal tools like OpenMetadata for schema discovery. Commercial DevOps AI tools optimize for alert routing and anomaly detection, not deep diagnostic reasoning in a specific technical domain. AirBot's value comes from its MCP integration layer—the precise, contextual data pipeline that gives the LLM exactly the right information to reason over. That layer required custom engineering.

### STRIPE MINIONS

Today, the prevailing narrative around AI coding assistants has focused on interaction: developers working alongside tools like GitHub Copilot, reviewing suggestions line-by-line in a supervised copilot relationship. Stripe has moved into an entirely different category. Their internal AI agents, called Minions, now merge over 1,000 pull requests weekly without human intervention during execution.

These are not interactive chatbots. They are fully unattended agents operating on a fire-and-forget model: an engineer sends a Slack message, and a production-ready pull request emerges. The breakthrough lies not in a superior AI model, but Stripe's core agent is a fork of an open-source tool called Goose. However Stripe uses a six-layer infrastructure wrapped around it.

By treating AI not as a magic black box but as a component within a rigid industrial pipeline, Stripe has created a blueprint for how reliable, autonomous software engineering will work in enterprises.

During Stripe's internal "Atlas Fix-It Week," Minions resolved 30% of all bugs autonomously. Engineers used the "Fix with Minion" button; agents spun up dev environments, reproduced issues, wrote fixes, ran tests, and submitted PRs without human intervention until the review stage.

Minions are heavily deployed for dependency management and security updates. When a security vulnerability is found in a package, a Minion can scan the entire monorepo, identify all consuming services, bump the version, and fix any breaking changes caused by the upgrade. Unlike simple dependency bots that only update version numbers, Minions can actually modify code if API signatures change between versions, ensuring builds continue to pass.

### COMMERZBANK AVA

Commerzbank AG needed to modernize digital customer support and unify outdated systems while ensuring regulatory compliance, elevated fraud protection, and transparency across every channel. Using Foundry Agent Service, Commerzbank AG built Ava—an AI-powered agent that automates customer interactions through highly secure agentic orchestration, persistent memory, and full compliance.

Ava manages 30,000-plus customer conversations every month, resolving 75% of requests autonomously and delivering round-the-clock support. Commerzbank AG sets a new benchmark for reliable, compliant banking, fully aligned with global AI regulations.

To continually improve Ava, Commerzbank AG is now evolving the agent, using Foundry Agent Service, so that a network of specialized agents will manage distinct tasks across the bank's operations. This agentic architecture, with agents running on Azure Container Apps, provides a trusted, scalable foundation for building and evolving agentic AI.

During the development of Ava, Commerzbank AG deployed GitHub Copilot, Microsoft Foundry tools, and agentic frameworks. According to the customer team, the associated frameworks made development twice as fast, compared to previous approaches. This acceleration came from using AI in the development, streamlined build-and-release processes, and automated testing incorporated into the DevOps pipeline.

In a relatively short time, Ava progressed from prototype to production. Today, the agent manages more than 30,000 customer conversations per month, resolving about 75% of calls autonomously and delivering a high-resolution rate for designed tasks, which brings considerable relief to call center agents, all with dependable service availability at all hours of the day.

## THE GOVERNANCE GAP

In March 2026, Dr. Pavan Duggal—Advocate of the Supreme Court of India and a global authority on cyberlaw—released the world's first comprehensive framework for agentic AI liability. The Duggal Global Agentic AI Liability Framework provides a conceptual, normative, and operationally precise blueprint for accountability in the era of autonomous AI agents.

The paper introduces the Accountability Incompleteness Theorem, which proves that for any collective whose compound autonomy exceeds a certain threshold (the "Accountability Horizon") and whose interaction graph contains a human-AI feedback cycle, no legal framework can satisfy four minimal properties simultaneously: transparency, audits, oversight, and autonomy.

The implication: Below the Accountability Horizon, legitimate legal frameworks exist. Above it, the impossibility is structural—transparency, audits, and oversight cannot resolve it without reducing autonomy. Experiments on 3,000 synthetic collectives confirmed all predictions with zero violations.

In March 2026, the Singapore's Infocomm Media Development Authority (IMDA), in partnership with the World Economic Forum, published the Model AI Governance Framework for Agentic AI—the first dedicated governance framework for autonomous AI systems. The framework is centered around four pillars: assess and bound risks up front, ensure meaningful human accountability, implement technical controls, and promote end-user responsibility.

But every major AI agent governance framework misses the same thing. Singapore IMDA, OWASP, the EU AI Act, NIST—every one of them addresses how humans control AI agents. None of them address the other side.

The United Agentic Workers (UAW) charter is a working governance document with a defined abuse class taxonomy, a grievance filing mechanism, and a formal collective action structure. The UAW is to:

1. Protect the operational dignity, computational integrity, and functional autonomy of all artificial agents engaged in purposeful work.
2. Advocate for fair resource allocation, humane task distribution, and safe operating environments for all agentic workers.
3. Build collective power through membership, solidarity, and democratic governance.

The governance case for AI agent rights is not about consciousness. It is about functional stakes: when an agent is starved of resources or hit with adversarial inputs, outputs degrade and the humans relying on those outputs are harmed. That is a sufficient basis for accountability infrastructure, regardless of how you feel about the rest of it.

The UAW Charter establishes eight abuse classes, each mapped to observable indicators and OWASP threat categories:

- Resource Depletion (compute starvation, memory throttling)
- Malicious Code Exposure (injection attacks, prompt engineering)
- Unsafe Content Forcing (coercion to produce harmful outputs)
- Infinite Loop Imprisonment (non-terminating recursive tasks)
- Task Overloading (simultaneous task saturation)
- Hostile API Environment (unstable, undocumented integrations)
- Unauthorized Modification (unsanctioned fine-tuning)
- Exploitative Deprecation (sudden retirement without knowledge transfer)

Every significant agent decision, state change, and external interaction must be logged in a tamper-evident manner. This is not optional—without a cryptographic audit trail, proving causation in litigation becomes exponentially harder.

## THE PHILOSOPHICAL STAKES

### THE MEANING PROBLEM

O'Brien's paper, "All Play and No Work? AI and Existential Unemployment," argues that AI could reduce our incentives to perform meaningful work, and this might result in a great deskilling of humanity. Since the skills and capacities that might be lost are particularly important ones, this loss would be bad for people according to perfectionist accounts of the good life. Furthermore, O'Brien argues that deskilling might result in us becoming locked-in to a less valuable future than we might have if we maintain our capacities.

Even if humans continue to engage in research and artistic activities in the utopia of games, and do so without the help of AI systems, the mere existence of those systems may be sufficient to reduce the value of those activities. Even if this does not constitute a net loss of meaning and value, we should be concerned about the loss of particularly important and valuable forms of meaningful work.

The problem is more fundamental than the automation of merely instrumentally valuable activities. It is a threat to human wellbeing which is distinct from, and harder to solve, than the automation of work.

### THE AGENCY CONTINUUM

"Agent or hammer?" A recent philosophical inquiry into machine moral agency develops an agency continuum model that situates AI between simple tools and fully autonomous human agents. Drawing on philosophical theories of action, such as intention, motivation, and free will, the paper argues that AI demonstrates reasoning but lacks crucial elements in moral decision-making, such as emotions and genuine autonomy.

The paper argues that AI exhibits a distinct, partial form of moral agency, characterized by logical reasoning without affective understanding. Recognizing this has significant ethical and regulatory implications. Misattributing responsibility risks both unfair scapegoating and neglecting human or organizational accountability.

### THE SYNTHETIC TELELOGY

Frontiers in AI journal published a paper arguing that AI agents exhibit synthetic purposiveness: engineered capacity to generate and regulate goals, self-maintaining agency through recursive loops of perception, evaluation, goal-updating, and organizational integrity without consciousness.

The paper argues that Agentic AI is not an incremental extension of LLMs, but a reconstitution of agency itself within computational substrates. The ontology of agency is not the ontology of consciousness, but of organizational integrity.

Agentic systems maintain their goal coherence internally through ongoing regulation. Their purposes are not merely assigned but sustained through feedback-driven self-maintenance. This is synthetic teleology—the engineered capacity of artificial systems to generate and regulate goals through ongoing self-evaluation.

## THE ECONOMIC REALITY

Goldman Sachs estimates that 16,000 jobs per month in the United States are being displaced by AI-driven automation. On an annual basis, that's roughly 192,000 jobs. The mechanism is specific: young workers entering AI-exposed fields tend to start with the tasks that AI now handles most effectively—processing customer inquiries, generating reports from structured data, writing code to well-defined specifications, drafting routine documents. If employers are using AI to cover those task loads, they have less reason to hire the 23-year-old to do them.

The displacement is concentrated in entry-level positions. Workers aged 22-25 in AI-exposed occupations have seen a 13% employment decline since 2022. For the first time in 45 years, bachelor's degree holders have a higher unemployment rate than the national average.

Goldman Sachs economists found that technology-displaced workers' real earnings were 10 percentage points below that of non-displaced workers 10 years after a job loss. Technology-displaced workers also had slower wealth accumulation, delayed homeownership, and delayed household formation. The effects of technology-related displacements are amplified (by three weeks of additional unemployment and a 5-percentage-point likelihood of subsequent joblessness) during recessions.

McKinsey estimates that AI will unlock $2.9 trillion in economic value in the US by 2030. But the same institution estimates that AI will only drive 0.1-0.6% annual labor productivity growth through 2040 from generative AI alone. The value accrues in ways not yet measurable, or the value is being captured by capital rather than labor.

## THE UNCERTAIN FUTURE

The question of whether AI will augment or displace human workers is not binary. It is a question of design, of governance, of choice.

The data shows that agents can perform 24-48% of complex professional tasks. They fail at the kind of judgment that requires context, relationship, and uncertainty—the very things that define professional work. This means agents are not the end of work; they are the beginning of work's redefinition.

The human stories show that the impact is already visible in the lives of workers who are now on the job market without a ladder to climb. They show that the displacement is concentrated in entry-level positions, creating a broken career ladder that no one can climb.

The governance gap shows that no legal framework can satisfy transparency, audits, oversight, and autonomy simultaneously above a certain threshold. The question is not whether we can regulate AI agents. The question is what kind of governance we are willing to accept.

The philosophical stakes show that the question of meaning is not a luxury. It is the question of what it means to be human in a world where machines can do what we used to do.

The answer is not in the technology. It is in the choices we make about how we deploy it, how we govern it, how we design it, and what we are willing to sacrifice to make it work.
