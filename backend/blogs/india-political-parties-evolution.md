# The Fractured Mandate: How India's Political Parties Evolved from Independence to Coalition to Dominance

> **Reported Lede:**
> In 1885, seventy-two social reformers, journalists, and lawyers gathered in a Sanskrit college in Bombay to launch the Indian National Congress. They sought a platform for civic dialogue between educated Indians and the British Raj. Four decades later, the same Congress would lead India to independence. But the story doesn't end there. By 1969, the party that had united the independence movement had fractured into Congress (R) and Congress (O), its leadership split over ideology and power. By 1977, Congress had been defeated by the Janata Party, the first non-Congress government at the center. By 1989, the coalition era began. And by 2014, the Bharatiya Janata Party had achieved a single-party majority unprecedented since 1984.

> **Nut Graf:**
> India's party system has undergone four distinct evolutionary phases since independence: Congress dominance (1947-1967), the breakdown of Congress and rise of regional parties (1967-1989), the coalition era (1989-2014), and the return of single-party dominance under the BJP (2014-present). Along the way, major parties split, merged, rebranded, and realigned. Key splits—Congress 1969, DMK 1972, Shiv Sena 2022, NCP 2023—were driven by ideological differences, succession disputes, and strategic disagreements. Equally important were the reasons parties came together: to counter Congress hegemony (1977 Janata Party), to govern through coalitions (1989 onward), or to build national majorities (2014 BJP-NDA). Understanding these dynamics requires tracing not just parties, but the fractures that define them.

By the time India achieved independence in 1947, the political landscape had evolved through three decades of nationalist agitation, communal representation, and regional movements. Several parties emerged before independence, but none matched Congress's organizational reach and mass appeal.

### **Indian National Congress: Foundation and Rise**

**The First Session (1885):**

On 28 December 1885, seventy-two social reformers, journalists, and lawyers gathered at Gokuldas Tejpal Sanskrit College in Bombay for the first session of the Indian National Congress. W.C. Bonnerjee served as the first president [INC Timeline](https://inc.in/brief-history-of-congress). Allan Octavian Hume, a retired British Indian Civil Service officer, provided organizational support. The Congress was originally planned as the "Indian National Union" but was renamed before the first session [BharatNotes](https://bharatnotes.com/subjects/history-culture/freedom-struggle/rise-of-nationalism-inc/).

**Early Moderates (1885-1905):**

The early Congress was dominated by "Moderates" who used constitutional methods—petitions, resolutions, and appeals to British justice. Key figures included:

| Leader | Contribution |
|--------|--------------|
| **Dadabhai Naoroji** | "Grand Old Man of India"; propounded the Drain Theory; first Asian elected to British Parliament (1892); thrice INC president |
| **Surendranath Banerjee** | "Indian Burke"; founded Indian National Association (1876); edited *The Bengalee*; twice INC president |
| **Gopal Krishna Gokhale** | "Political Guru" of Gandhi; founded Servants of India Society (1905); advocated social reform alongside political reform |
| **Pherozeshah Mehta** | "Lion of Bombay"; shaped early Congress agenda in municipal politics |
| **Badruddin Tyabji** | First Muslim president of INC (1887) |
| **W.C. Bonnerjee** | First INC president (1885); prominent Calcutta barrister |

The Moderates demanded legislative councils, simultaneous ICS exams for Indians, and greater Indian participation in administration [BharatNotes](https://bharatnotes.com/subjects/history-culture/freedom-struggle/rise-of-nationalism-inc/).

**The Extremist Shift (1905-1919):**

The Swadeshi Movement (1905) in response to the Bengal partition marked the transition from Moderates to "Extremists":

| Extremist Leader | Contribution |
|-----------------|--------------|
| **Bal Gangadhar Tilak** | Embraced *swaraj* (self-rule) as destiny of India; opposed British education system; popularized *Gandhi's slogan "Swaraj is my birthright"* |
| **Bipin Chandra Pal** | Co-architect of Extremist strategy; advocated mass mobilization |
| **Lala Lajpat Rai** | "Lion of Punjab"; fought for Hindu-Muslim unity; prominent Extremist leader |
| **Aurobindo Ghose** | Philosophical theorist of swaraj; advocated spiritual nationalism |

The Extremists pushed for more assertive methods—boycotts, national schools, and self-reliance [BharatNotes](https://bharatnotes.com/subjects/history-culture/freedom-struggle/rise-of-nationalism-inc/).

**The Gandhian Era (1920-1947):**

Mahatma Gandhi transformed the Congress into a mass movement:

| Leader | Contribution |
|--------|--------------|
| **Mahatma Gandhi** | Led independence movement (1920-47); organized Non-Cooperation (1920), Civil Disobedience (1930), Quit India (1942); championed nonviolent resistance |
| **Sardar Vallabhbhai Patel** | Organized peasant movements; unified princely states (1947-48); Congress organizational chief |
| **Jawaharlal Nehru** | Congress president (1929-46); shaped post-independence vision; first Prime Minister (1947-64) |
| **Subhas Chandra Bose** | Revolutionary nationalist; formed Forward Bloc (1939); led Indian National Army |
| **Chittaranjan Das** | "Desha Bandhu"; organized Swaraj Party (1923); popular orator |
| **Rajendra Prasad** | Organized Champaran satyagraha (1917); Congress president (1934); first President of India (1950-62) |
| **Motilal Nehru** | Moderate leader; founded Swaraj Party (1923); lawyer and statesman |
| **C.R. Das** | Founded Swaraj Party (1923); Congress president (1922-24) |
| **Sarojini Naidu** | Poet and Congress leader; first woman president of INC (1925) |
| **Vallabhbhai Patel** | Organized Bardoli satyagraha (1928); unified states after independence |

**Key Milestones:**
- **1917:** Congress elected to support Home Rule; W.S. Cripps introduced the idea of dominion status
- **1920:** Non-Cooperation Movement launched after Jallianwala Bagh massacre (1919)
- **1929:** Lahore Session; Purna Swaraj ("Complete Self-Rule") declared
- **1930:** Salt Satyagraha (Dandi March)
- **1935:** Government of India Act passed; Congress first tasted provincial power
- **1937:** Won provincial elections in 9 provinces
- **1942:** Quit India Movement launched; entire Congress leadership imprisoned until 1945

### **All-India Muslim League: The Separatist Alternative**

**Formation (1906):**

The All-India Muslim League was founded on 30 December 1906 in Dhaka (then Dacca) at the conclusion of the All-India Muslim Educational Conference [All-India Muslim League Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/All-India_Muslim_League). The meeting was attended by 3,000 Muslim delegates. Key founders included:

| Founder | Contribution |
|---------|--------------|
| **Nawab of Dacca (Khawaja Salimullah)** | Proposed creation of Muslim League at 1906 conference |
| **Aga Khan (Sir Sultan Muhammad Shah)** | Led Simla Deputation to Viceroy (1906); encouraged British to support Muslim political organization |
| **Nawab Viqar ul-Mulk** | Attempted to establish Muslim political organization (1901); helped found League |
| **Mohammed Ali Jinnah** | Joined League (1913); became undisputed leader (1934-47); called "Quaid-e-Azam" |

**Early Years (1906-1937):**

The Muslim League was initially an elitist organization representing wealthy Muslims and the Urdu-speaking upper classes. Key characteristics:

- **1906:** Promoted loyalty to British; opposed Congress's swadeshi movement
- **1913:** Adopted self-government for India as goal
- **1916:** Signed Lucknow Pact with Congress; demanded separate electorates for Muslims
- **1919:** Indian Councils Act granted separate Muslim electorates
- **1920s:** Strained relations with Congress after Khilafat Movement ended (1922)
- **1930:** Muslim League membership declined; remained elite organization

**The Jinnah Transformation (1937-47):**

In 1937, the Muslim League began mass mobilization under Jinnah's leadership:

- **1937:** Provincial elections showed League's weakness; only 104 Muslim seats won out of 476 reserved seats
- **1938:** Jinnah declared League's independence from Congress; formed Five-Year Plan for Muslim community
- **1940:** Lahore Resolution (Pakistan Resolution) adopted on 23 March 1940, calling for "independent states" for Muslims [Lahore Resolution Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pakistan_Resolution)
- **1941:** Weekly newspaper *Dawn* launched
- **1943:** Planning Committee and Committee of Action formed
- **1946:** Won 425 of 476 Muslim seats in provincial elections (89.2% of Muslim votes)
- **1947:** Pakistan achieved on 14 August; League disbanded in India

**Why the League Focused Only on Muslims:**

The League represented only Muslim interests, contrasting sharply with Congress's secular nationalism. The League sought British protection for Muslim minority rights and insisted on guarantees as the price of participating in the nationalist movement [Country Studies Bangladesh](https://countrystudies.us/bangladesh/12.htm).

### **Hindu Mahasabha: The Hindu Nationalist Alternative**

**Formation (1915):**

The Hindu Mahasabha was officially formed as an All-India organization in 1915, though regional forms existed from 1905-07 [Akhil Bharat Hindu MahaSabha](https://www.abhm.org.in/about.aspx). Key founders:

| Founder | Contribution |
|---------|--------------|
| **Pandit Madan Mohan Malaviya** | President of multiple sessions; promoted Hindu unity |
| **Lala Lajpat Rai** | Led Punjabi Mahasabha (1907); prominent Hindu nationalist |
| **V.D. Savarkar** | Ideological theorist; defined "Hindutva"; imprisoned by British |
| **Dr. B.S. Moonje** | Organized Mahasabha; represented at Round Table Conference |
| **Dr. Shyama Prasad Mukherjee** | Later became Jana Sangh founder; Minister for Industry in Nehru cabinet |

**Ideology:**

- Hindu unity and organization (*sangathan*)
- Anti-Muslim mobilization
- Anti-communalism (opposed separate electorates)
- Social reform (reconversion of Hindus from Islam)
- Militant nationalism (influenced by Savarkar's Hindutva)

**Pre-Independence Status:**

- **1920s:** Under Lajpat Rai and Malaviya, campaigned for social reform
- **1926:** First contested elections to Imperial/Provincial Legislatures
- **1937:** Broke away from Congress over communal differences
- **1940s:** Entered provincial governments (Sindh, Bengal, Punjab, NWFP)
- **1942:** Savarkar justified coalition governments with Muslim League

**Why It Remained Minor:**

- Elite organization; limited mass appeal
- Focused on militant Hinduism; alienated moderate Hindus
- Allied with British during WWII; alienated nationalists
- After independence, declined as BJP absorbed its ideology

### **Other Pre-Independence Parties**

**Justice Party (1916):**

- Founded by P. Ramanathan and C. Subbarayan
- Represented non-Brahmin interests in Madras
- Ruled Madras Presidency (1921-26)
- Merged into Congress and other parties after 1930s

**Swaraj Party (1923):**

- Founded by C.R. Das and Motilal Nehru
- Split from Congress to participate in legislatures
- Won 44 seats in 1923 elections
- Merged back into Congress by 1930s

**Forward Bloc (1939):**

- Founded by Subhas Chandra Bose
- Formed by Congress dissidents frustrated with Gandhi's methods
- Revolutionary nationalism; organized Indian National Army (1943)
- Merged into Congress after independence

**Shiromani Akali Dal (1920):**

- Founded by Kartar Singh, Babu Jagjivan Ram
- Represented Sikh interests in Punjab
- Demanded provincial autonomy
- Remained active after independence (regional party)

**Krishak Praja Party (1920s):**

- Founded by A.K. Fazlul Huq in Bengal
- Represented peasant interests
- Formed coalition government in Bengal (1940s)
- Dissolved after independence

### **Why Congress Emerged Dominant**

Several factors explain why Congress became the dominant pre-independence party while others remained minor:

**1. Broad-Based Appeal Across Religion, Caste, and Class:**

Congress positioned itself as a secular, inclusive party representing all Indians. Its membership included Hindus, Muslims, Sikhs, Christians, and members from all castes and classes [Britannica](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Indian-National-Congress). In contrast:
- **Muslim League** represented only Muslims; alienated Hindu-majority regions
- **Hindu Mahasabha** represented only Hindus; alienated Muslims and minorities
- **Regional parties** had limited geographic reach

**2. Mass Mobilization Under Gandhi (1920 onwards):**

Gandhi transformed Congress from an elite organization into a mass movement. His campaigns reached villages, towns, and cities across India:
- **Champaran Satyagraha (1917):** Peasant rights in Bihar
- **Kheda Satyagraha (1918):** Tax protest in Gujarat
- **Non-Cooperation (1920):** Nationwide boycott of British institutions
- **Salt Satyagraha (1930):** Dandi March across 2,000 miles
- **Quit India (1942):** Nationwide uprising against British rule

**3. Organizational Network:**

Congress had the most extensive organizational network:
- **All India Congress Committee (AICC):** Coordinated national activities
- **Provincial Congress Committees (PCC):** Regional organizations in every province
- **Village-level Congress Committees:** Grassroots reach

**4. Leadership of Independence Movement:**

Congress led every major independence movement from 1920 to 1947:
- Congress imprisoned entire leadership during Quit India (1942-45)
- Congress negotiated independence with British (1946-47)
- Congress unified diverse nationalist demands under Purna Swaraj

**5. Electoral Success:**

- **1937 Provincial Elections:** Congress won 9 provinces (out of 11); Muslim League won only 104 of 476 Muslim seats [Country Studies Bangladesh](https://countrystudies.us/bangladesh/12.htm)
- **1946 Constituent Assembly:** Congress dominated with 70% of Hindu seats

**6. Elite Acceptance:**

Congress accepted leaders from diverse backgrounds:
- **Muslims:** Jawaharlal Nehru (Muslim women's rights), Maulana Azad (Congress president)
- **Sikhs:** Tara Singh (prominent leader)
- **Christians:** Multiple Catholic and Protestant leaders
- **Lower Castes:** B.R. Ambedkar (independent but collaborated with Congress)

**7. British Miscalculation:**

The British underestimated Congress's mass appeal. They believed:
- Muslims would remain loyal to British (they didn't; Muslim League demanded Pakistan)
- Hindus would accept moderate reforms (Gandhi mobilized mass movement)
- Communal divisions would prevent unified independence movement (Congress maintained unity)

**8. International Recognition:**

Congress gained international recognition:
- **1942:** League of Nations condemned British repression of Congress
- **1945:** UN Charter negotiations; Nehru represented India
- **1947:** Independence negotiations; Congress led delegation

**9. Unity Against Partition:**

Despite communal tensions, Congress maintained unity:
- **1940:** All India Azad Muslim Conference (1,400 nationalist Muslims) supported united India [Lahore Resolution Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pakistan_Resolution)
- **1946:** Congress leaders (Gandhi, Nehru, Patel) opposed partition
- **1947:** Congress accepted partition reluctantly to prevent civil war

## **Part 2: Phase One — The Congress System (1947-1967)**

### **The Independence Party Becomes the Governing Party**

The Indian National Congress, founded on 28 December 1885 by Allan Octavian Hume, W.S. Bonnerjee, and other social reformers, led the independence movement under Mahatma Gandhi and Jawaharlal Nehru [INC Timeline](https://inc.in/brief-history-of-congress). After independence in 1947, Congress inherited a political vacuum. With the Muslim League dissolved after partition and Hindu Mahasabha marginal, Congress dominated India's first general election in 1951-52 and won every subsequent election until 1977 [Politics of India Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politics_of_India).

Jawaharlal Nehru's charismatic leadership and vision for a secular, socialist, democratic India resonated with the newly independent nation [PolSci Institute](https://polsci.institute/india-political-process/evolution-of-party-systems-in-india/). Congress operated as a "coalition of interests," accommodating diverse ideologies within its fold—from center-left socialism to moderate liberalism. This broad-based appeal cut across caste, class, and region, making it truly national.

**Elections Won:** Congress won 1952 (298 seats, 46% vote), 1957 (378 seats, 47.6% vote), 1962 by-elections, and 1967 (283 seats, 43.7% vote) [Politics of India Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politics_of_India).

**Limitations:** Despite dominance, Congress faced internal factionalism. The "Syndicate"—Kamaraj, Morarji Desai, Nijalingappa, and S.K. Patil—represented a conservative, right-wing faction that often clashed with Indira Gandhi's populist leftward turn [Congress(I) Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Congress(I)).

---

### **The 1969 Split: Congress (R) vs Congress (O)**

The 1967 elections, called the "political earthquake," marked the beginning of Congress's decline [PolSci Institute](https://polsci.institute/india-political-process/evolution-of-party-systems-in-india/). Congress lost power in eight states, including Uttar Pradesh, Bihar, West Bengal, and Tamil Nadu. Though Congress retained power at the center, its vote share dropped significantly. This demonstrated that Congress was no longer invincible and that regional parties could mobilize voters against the national party.

The split came in 1969 when Indira Gandhi, Prime Minister and daughter of Jawaharlal Nehru, was expelled by Congress party president S. Nijalingappa for indiscipline [Congress(I) Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Congress(I)). The conflict centered on policy and power: Indira wanted to use a populist agenda to mobilize popular support, while the Syndicate (Kamaraj, Desai, Nijalingappa, Patil) advocated right-wing economic policies, closer ties with the West, and suppression of leftist elements within Congress.

**The Splits:**
- **Congress (R)** (Requisitionists): Led by Indira Gandhi; adopted cow-and-calf symbol; supported by most Congress MPs [Congress(I) Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Congress(I))
- **Congress (O)** (Organisation): Led by Kamaraj, Desai, Nijalingappa; retained bullock-yoke symbol; represented right-wing agenda [Congress(I) Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Congress(I))

**Why It Happened:** The split was driven by policy disagreements (economic leftism vs. right-wing liberalism), ideological differences (populism vs. traditional Congress centrism), and a power struggle between Indira and the Syndicate.

**The Aftermath:** In the 1971 general election, Congress (R) won 352 of 518 seats [Congress(I) Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Congress(I)). Congress (O) fell from 65 to 16 seats. The Election Commission recognized Indira Gandhi's group as the real Congress, restoring the bullock-yoke symbol (though Indira's supporters kept the cow-and-calf). Congress (O) eventually merged into the Janata Party in 1977.

## **Part 3: Phase Two — The Breakdown of Congress (1967-1989)**

### **The Emergency and Janata Party (1975-1977)**

Indira Gandhi's leadership moved toward authoritarianism. The 1975-77 Emergency saw the suspension of civil liberties, mass arrests of opposition leaders, and forced sterilization programs that created widespread public resentment [PolSci Institute](https://polsci.institute/india-political-process/evolution-of-party-systems-in-india/).

**The Splits During Emergency:**
- **Congress for Democracy:** Led by Jagjivan Ram and H.N. Bahuguna; joined Janata Party in 1977 [Congress(I) Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Congress(I))
- **Congress (Urs):** Led by Devaraj Urs in Karnataka; joined Janata Party in 1977 [Congress(I) Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Congress(I))

**The 1977 Election:** In the aftermath of the Emergency, a coalition of opposition parties—the Janata Party—defeated Indira Gandhi. The Janata Party merged the Bharatiya Jana Sangh (Jana Sangh), Congress (O), Socialist Party, and others [Party Genealogy](https://electionresults.info/party-genealogy).

**Elections Won:** Janata Party won 295 Lok Sabha seats in 1977, the first non-Congress government at the center [Elections of India Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politics_of_India).

**Why It Happened:** The Emergency's authoritarianism created a unified anti-Congress coalition. Voters wanted to test democracy's resilience.

**Why It Fell Apart:** The Janata Party government collapsed in July 1979 due to internal contradictions. Morarji Desai resigned; Charan Singh formed a minority government. The coalition's ideological inconsistencies made governance unstable [PolSci Institute](https://polsci.institute/india-political-process/evolution-of-party-systems-in-india/).

---

### **The Rise of Regional Parties**

This phase saw the emergence of strong regional parties that challenged Congress's dominance in their respective states:

| Party | Founded | Founder | Ideology | Reason for Split/Form |
|-------|---------|---------|----------|---------------------|
| **DMK** | 1949 | C.N. Annadurai | Dravidianism, Tamil nationalism, secularism | Split from Dravida Kazhagam (Periyar opposed electoral politics) |
| **AIADMK** | 1972 | M.G. Ramachandran | Dravidian socialism, secularism, regionalism | Split from DMK (feud with Karunanidhi over party accounts) |
| **TDP** | 1982 | N.T. Rama Rao | Telugu pride, regionalism, economic liberalism | Formed to counter Congress hegemony in Andhra Pradesh |
| **Shiv Sena** | 1966 | Bal Thackeray | Marathi regionalism, later Hindutva | Formed as anti-migrant "sons of the soil" movement |
| **BSP** | 1984 | Mayawati | Dalit empowerment, social justice | Emerged from Republican Party to represent Dalit interests |
| **SP** | 1994 | Mulayam Singh Yadav | Socialism, secularism, OBC representation | Emerged from Janata Dal (Yadav faction) |
| **JD(U)** | 2003 | Lalu Prasad/Yusuf Mehar | Socialism, federalism, secularism | Split from Janata Dal (Lalu's faction) |

**Dravidian Parties:** The DMK, founded in 1949 by C.N. Annadurai as a breakaway from Dravida Kazhagam, espoused Tamil nationalism and opposed Hindi imposition [Britannica](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Dravida-Munnetra-Kazhagam). In 1967, DMK became the first party other than Congress to win a state-level election with a clear majority.

**The 1972 DMK Split:** M.G. Ramachandran (MGR), popular actor and party treasurer, had a feud with Karunanidhi over party accounts. MGR was expelled and formed AIADMK on 17 October 1972 [All India Anna Dravida Munnetra Kazhagam Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/All_India_Anna_Dravida_Munnetra_Kazhagam).

**AIADMK Succession Crisis (1987-89):** After MGR's death in December 1987, his wife V.N. Janaki Ramachandran became Chief Minister for 23 days. J. Jayalalithaa opposed this. The AIADMK split into two factions: AIADMK(J) led by Janaki and AIADMK(JA) led by Jayalalithaa [AIADMK Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/All_India_Anna_Dravida_Munnetra_Kazhagam). The 1988 election saw the party contest as two factions, losing heavily. They merged under Jayalalithaa in 1989 [AIADMK Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/All_India_Anna_Dravida_Munnetra_Kazhagam).

**Telugu Desam Party:** Founded by N.T. Rama Rao on 29 March 1982, TDP emerged on the ideology of "Telugu Vari Atma Gouravam" (Telugu People's Self-Respect) [TDP Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Telugu_Desam_Party). In the 1983 state elections, TDP won a landslide victory. NTR became Andhra Pradesh's first non-Congress Chief Minister.

**Shiv Sena:** Founded by cartoonist Bal Thackeray on 19 June 1966, Shiv Sena initially promoted Marathi regionalism and anti-migrant sentiment [Shiv Sena Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shiv_Sena). By the 1970s, it shifted toward Hindutva and allied with BJP.

---

### **Congress (I) Returns: 1980-1989**

In 1978, Indira Gandhi formed Congress (I)—the "I" signifying Indira [Congress(I) Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Congress(I)). In January 1980, Congress (I) won a landslide victory. The Election Commission declared it the real Indian National Congress for the 1984 election. The "I" was dropped in 1996 [Congress(I) Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Congress(I)).

**Elections Won:** Congress (I) won 350 seats in 1980 [Elections of India Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politics_of_India).

**Rajiv Gandhi Era (1984-89):** After Indira Gandhi's assassination in June 1984, Rajiv Gandhi led Congress to a massive victory, winning 404 seats on a "tsunami" vote [Elections of India Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politics_of_India). Congress lost in 1989 to the National Front.

## **Part 4: Phase Three — The Coalition Era (1989-2014)**

### **The 1989 Turning Point**

The 1989 general elections marked the beginning of the coalition era. The National Front government, led by V.P. Singh, came to power with outside support from the BJP and Left parties [PolSci Institute](https://polsci.institute/india-political-process/evolution-of-party-systems-in-india/). This government implemented the Mandal Commission recommendations on OBC reservations, which led to widespread protests.

The collapse of the V.P. Singh government in 1990 ushered in political instability. The 1991 elections saw Congress return as a minority government under P.V. Narasimha Rao.

**P.V. Narasimha Rao's Government (1991-96):** Rao moved Congress toward economic liberalization, a sharp break from previous leaders. This period saw the opening of India's economy, dismantling of the license raj, and introduction of market reforms [Elections of India Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politics_of_India).

**Elections Won:** Congress won 260 seats in 1991 (minority government) [Elections of India Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politics_of_India).

---

### **The Emergence of the BJP**

The Bharatiya Janata Party (BJP) emerged as the principal challenger to Congress during this period. The party's Hindutva ideology and mobilization around the Ram Janmabhoomi issue expanded its base [Politics of India Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politics_of_India).

**BJP Timeline:**
- **1951:** Bharatiya Jana Sangh founded by Shyama Prasad Mukherjee, RSS political wing [Britannica](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Bharatiya-Janata-Party)
- **1977:** Jana Sangh merged into Janata Party [Party Genealogy](https://electionresults.info/party-genealogy)
- **1980:** BJP formally established by Vajpayee, Advani, Joshi after Janata collapse [Britannica](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Bharatiya-Janata-Party)
- **1984:** Won only 2 Lok Sabha seats [Electoral History BJP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electoral_history_of_the_Bharatiya_Janata_Party)
- **1989:** Won 83 seats after Ram Rath Yatra [Electoral History BJP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electoral_history_of_the_Bharatiya_Janata_Party)
- **1996:** Became largest party (161 seats) but government lasted only 13 days [Electoral History BJP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electoral_history_of_the_Bharatiya_Janata_Party)
- **1998:** NDA formed, BJP-led coalition government [Britannica](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Bharatiya-Janata-Party)
- **1999:** NDA won 303 seats; first non-Congress government to complete full term [Electoral History BJP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electoral_history_of_the_Bharatiya_Janata_Party)
- **2004:** Lost to UPA coalition [Electoral History BJP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electoral_history_of_the_Bharatiya_Janata_Party)
- **2014:** Won 282 seats, outright majority; Modi became PM [Electoral History BJP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electoral_history_of_the_Bharatiya_Janata_Party)
- **2019:** Won 303+ NDA seats; Modi re-elected [Electoral History BJP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electoral_history_of_the_Bharatiya_Janata_Party)
- **2024:** Coalition government; BJP won 144 seats, NDA formed government [Elections of India Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politics_of_India)

**The Ram Rath Yatra (1989-91):** Led by L.K. Advani, the Ram Rath Yatra mobilized Hindus around the Babri Masjid issue. This marked a shift from Jana Sangh's traditional focus to Hindutva-based populism [A Brief History of BJP](https://news.abplive.com/blog/a-brief-history-of-bjp-in-its-40th-year-1266349).

**The 1992 Babri Masjid Demolition:** In December 1992, Hindu activists demolished the Babri Masjid in Ayodhya. This polarized Indian society and made communal politics a key electoral strategy [Politics of India Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politics_of_India).

### **Coalition Governments (1996-2004)**

The period 1996-2004 saw multiple coalition governments:

1. **United Front (1996-98):** Led by H.D. Deve Gowda (Janata Dal), then I.K. Gujral. Supported by Congress [Elections of India Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politics_of_India)
2. **NDA (1998-99):** Led by Atal Bihari Vajpayee; lasted 13 months [Elections of India Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politics_of_India)
3. **NDA (1999-2004):** Led by Vajpayee; completed full five-year term [Britannica](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Bharatiya-Janata-Party)

**United Progressive Alliance (UPA):** After 2004 elections, Congress-led UPA formed government under Manmohan Singh. UPA governed from 2004 to 2014, implementing NREGA (National Rural Employment Guarantee Act) and Right to Information Act [Elections of India Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politics_of_India).

**Elections Won:** UPA won 2004 (138 seats for Congress, coalition total 224) and 2009 (206 seats for Congress, coalition total 282) [Elections of India Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politics_of_India).

## **Part 5: Phase Four — Single-Party Dominance Return (2014-Present)**

### **The Modi Era: BJP's Landslide and Consolidation**

The 2014 general election marked a significant shift. Narendra Modi, then Chief Minister of Gujarat, led the BJP to a landslide victory. The BJP won 282 seats outright—a clear majority—on its own strength [Electoral History BJP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electoral_history_of_the_Bharatiya_Janata_Party). This was the first instance since 1984 of a single party achieving an outright majority.

**Modi Era Elections:**
- **2014:** BJP won 282 seats, 31% vote; NDA total 336 seats [Electoral History BJP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electoral_history_of_the_Bharatiya_Janata_Party)
- **2019:** BJP won 303 seats, 37.46% vote; NDA total 353 seats [Electoral History BJP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electoral_history_of_the_Bharatiya_Janata_Party)
- **2024:** BJP won 144 seats (did not reach 272 alone); NDA total 293+ seats; coalition government formed [Electoral History BJP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electoral_history_of_the_Bharatiya_Janata_Party), [Elections of India Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politics_of_India)

**Why It Happened:** Growing discontent with Congress rule, Modi's development-focused campaign ("Sabka Saath, Sabka Vikas"), and BJP's organizational strength under RSS [A Brief History of BJP](https://news.abplive.com/blog/a-brief-history-of-bjp-in-its-40th-year-1266349).

---

### **Recent Splits and Alliances (2022-2023)**

The coalition era's dynamics reshaped the political landscape:

**Shiv Sena Split (2022):** After the 2019 Maharashtra election, Shiv Sena left the BJP alliance over disagreements on the Chief Minister post [Shiv Sena Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shiv_Sena). Uddhav Thackeray formed an alliance with Congress and NCP. In June 2022, Eknath Shinde and the majority of Shiv Sena MLAs joined BJP [Shiv Sena Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shiv_Sena). The party split into:
- **Shiv Sena (Shinde):** Original bow-and-arrow symbol; allied with BJP
- **Shiv Sena (Uddhav Balasaheb Thackeray):** Mashaal (Torch) symbol; renamed 2023; allied with Congress-NCP [Party Genealogy](https://electionresults.info/party-genealogy)

**NCP Split (2023):** On 2 July 2023, Ajit Pawar and senior leaders broke away from NCP and joined the BJP-Shiv Sena alliance in Maharashtra [NCP 2023 Split](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2023_Nationalist_Congress_Party_split). The split was driven by:
- Ideological differences (Ajit favored BJP alliance; Sharad opposed)
- Power struggles (Ajit sought CM position)
- Strategic disagreements (alliance direction) [NCP 2023 Split](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2023_Nationalist_Congress_Party_split)

The NCP split into:
- **NCP (Sharad Pawar):** Traditional secular, centrist faction
- **NCP (Ajit Pawar):** BJP-allied faction [Party Genealogy](https://electionresults.info/party-genealogy)

---

### **2024 Election: Coalition Politics Returns**

The 2024 election saw the INDIA alliance (formerly UPA) emerge as the main opposition bloc. The NDA formed government, but BJP failed to secure a majority on its own [Elections of India Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politics_of_India). Rahul Gandhi became Leader of the Opposition after winning 99 seats for Congress [Elections of India Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politics_of_India).

**Current Status:** As of April 2026, the NDA alliance governs 19 Indian states and 2 union territories [Britannica](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Bharatiya-Janata-Party).

## **Part 9: Why Parties Split and Why They Came Together**

### **Common Themes in Party Splits:**

1. **Ideological Differences:** NCP split (Ajit favored BJP alliance; Sharad opposed); Congress split (Indira's populism vs. Syndicate's centrism)

2. **Succession Disputes:** AIADMK split (Janaki vs. Jayalalithaa after MGR's death); NCP split (Ajit sought CM position; Sharad favored Supriya)

3. **Strategic Alliances:** Shiv Sena split (Uddhav favored Congress-NCP; Shinde favored BJP); NCP split (Ajit favored BJP; Sharad favored MVA)

4. **Personal Power Struggles:** Congress split (Indira vs. Syndicate over party control); Shiv Sena split (Uddhav vs. Shinde over CM post)

5. **Identity Politics:** DMK split (MGR vs. Karunanidhi); TRS/BRS formed (Telangana statehood demand)

### **Common Themes in Party Alliances:**

1. **To Counter Congress Hegemony:** Janata Party 1977 (Jana Sangh, Congress O, Socialists united against Indira)

2. **To Govern Through Coalitions:** NDA 1998 (BJP-led coalition); UPA 2004 (Congress-led coalition)

3. **To Build National Majorities:** 2014 BJP-NDA (regional parties joined BJP for national governance)

4. **For State-Level Power:** Shiv Sena (2019) joined NDA for Maharashtra CM post; later split over CM disagreement

5. **Ideological Convergence:** Regional parties aligning with national parties when ideological goals overlap (e.g., Telangana party with NDA on development agenda)

## **Part 10: Narrative Angle and Conclusion**

The evolution of India's political parties reflects a broader story of how a young democracy navigates complexity: regional identities, caste dynamics, religious pluralism, and the tension between centralized and decentralized power. The pattern is clear: no party can hold power indefinitely. The Congress system collapsed; coalition politics emerged; single-party dominance returned. But each phase brings new fractures and new alliances.

The most striking theme is the role of splits. India's parties are not static institutions but fluid entities that fracture, reform, and recombine. From 1969 to 2023, splits have reshaped the political landscape more than mergers have consolidated it. This reflects India's diversity: no single ideology can hold the country together. Coalitions are not just pragmatic necessities; they are democratic imperatives.

The return of single-party dominance under the BJP does not signal the end of regional parties. Rather, regional parties have become kingmakers in coalition politics. Even when a single party achieves a majority at the center (2014, 2019), it must rely on regional allies for stable governance (2024). The evolution of India's party system is not linear; it is cyclical, with each phase building on the lessons of the previous.
