# Kalmykia: The Deportation That Stalin Never Forgiven

**By Quortol Editorial Team**

---

The morning of December 28, 1943, broke cold across the Kalmyk steppes. NKVD agents pushed open the doors of homes, their boots crunching on frozen ground, announcing a decree that would erase an entire people from the map. Families were given twelve hours to pack their belongings, carrying no more than 500 kilograms each—clothing, tools, heirlooms, everything that constituted a life. Then they were loaded onto trucks and driven to railway stations.

What followed was not a military campaign or a battle, but an administrative operation. It had a code name: "Operation Ulusy." Forty-six eastbound trains, each packed with cattle wagons, carried 93,139 Kalmyks—26,359 families—on a journey over 1,000 miles to Siberia and Central Asia. Only three families in all of Kalmykia were spared.

The trains arrived in Omsk Oblast, Krasnoyarsk Krai, Altai Krai, and Novosibirsk Oblast—scattered across Siberia and Kazakhstan. At one stud farm in Siberia, a family ate oilcake and exchanged their belongings for food. Another arrived at a fishing town in the Surgut region, where they lived better, with access to fish. Some died on the journey. More than 1,400 perished during transit. Thirteen years later, they were allowed to return, only to find their homeland occupied by Russians and Ukrainians who chose to stay.

---

## The Morning of Arrest

Early on December 28, NKVD agents entered Kalmyk homes across the Kalmyk Autonomous Soviet Socialist Republic. They announced the decree of the Presidium of the Supreme Soviet: immediate deportation. Families had twelve hours to pack. They were allowed to carry up to 500 kilograms of property per family, and multiple families had to share space in one truck. Soviet soldiers searched the houses, confiscating firearms, anti-Soviet literature, and foreign currency. Every person of Kalmyk ethnicity—women, children, the elderly—was loaded onto trucks and sent to nearby railway stations.

At the start of the deportation, 750 Kalmyks were arrested as "gang members" or "anti-Soviet elements." Soviet forces had surrounded Kalmyk settlements from the outset to prevent resistance.

---

## The Journey

The Kalmyks were packed into cattle carts and loaded onto 46 east-bound trains. They were sent on a journey to remote areas over a thousand miles away.

One witness recalled that they traveled for two weeks, with no opportunity to practice basic hygiene. Another witness described that the children slept on the bunks, while the grown-ups slept on the floor of the wagons. They made a hole on the floor, placed suitcases around it, and used it as a toilet. Meals were available only once per day. Some deportees shared their food during the long transit. The trains would occasionally stop to release the people inside, though only for a short amount of time.

The entire operation was completed by December 31, 1943. By January 1944, the Kalmyks were scattered across Siberia and Central Asia:

![Map of Kalmyk deportation routes from Kalmykia to Siberia and Central Asia in 1943](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/ca/Map_of_Kalmyk_deportation.jpg)

* **Omsk Oblast:** 24,352 people
* **Krasnoyarsk Krai:** 21,164 people
* **Altai Krai:** 20,858 people
* **Novosibirsk Oblast:** 18,333 people

Additional families were located in Tomsk Region (660 families), Kazakh SSR (648 families), Tobolsk (522 families), Yamalo-Nenets Autonomous Okrug (2,796 families), and Khanty-Mansi Autonomous Okrug (1,760 families).

## The Mortality

Soviet archival records documented more than 16,000 deaths among deported Kalmyks, representing a 17% mortality rate over the first five years of exile. Approximately 1,400 died during the transit journey itself. However, unofficial estimates from oral histories vary, with some survivors claiming higher mortality rates of up to one-third of deportees.

Galina Goryaeva, a Kalmyk folk singer who survived the deportation, shares memories from her life during the Soviet era. "On the way to Siberia, many young and old people died in the freight wagons from exposure to disease, cold and hunger," she recalls. She remembers "the suffering of those first lonely years in exile, the hardest time for Kalmyks, when they had to exchange their clothes and valuable possessions for food."

Boris Boktaev reminisces about his years in exile in Siberia, where almost all Kalmyks who were placed with him in a village died from disease. "He remembers the kindness of many Russians who helped Kalmyks to survive, but also mentions occasions in which local people were cruel to them."

Anna Erdneeva recalls the day of deportation and the life of her family during Siberian exile. "Early in the morning on the 28th December 1943 they were taken with the other Kalmyks and brought to a railway station, where they were placed in a train with red wagons and sent to Siberia. They arrived at one village and lived there on a stud farm. There, they would eat oilcake and used to exchange their belongings for food. A few months later they were taken to a fishing town in the Surgut region. There they lived better, particularly as they had access to and were able to eat fish."

Maria Lidzhigoryaeva remembers the return journey in 1957. "The journey took 17 days which she spent singing with the others. Among the passengers was the singer Ulan Barbaevna Lidzhieva who was sick. Everyone in the train tried to help her. At Divnoe station they were welcomed by the ensemble Tyulpan (Tulip)."

## Why the Deportation?

The official Soviet reason was an accusation of Nazi collaboration during World War II. The government claimed this was based on approximately 5,000 Kalmyks who fought in the Nazi-affiliated Kalmykian Cavalry Corps.

The Soviet government refused to acknowledge that more than 23,000 Kalmyks served in the Red Army and fought against Axis forces at the same time. Eight Kalmyks were awarded Heroes of the Soviet Union during the war.

The operation was overseen by NKVD Chief Lavrentiy Beria and his deputy Ivan Serov. Other officials involved included V.V. Chernyshov (NKVD deputy who coordinated resettlement logistics in Moscow), Victor Grigorievich Nasedkin (Head of the Gulag), and Dmitri Vasilevich Arkadiev (Head of the NKVD Transport Department).

In conjunction with the deportation, the Soviet government abolished the Kalmyk ASSR and split its territory between adjacent Astrakhan, Rostov, and Stalingrad Oblasts and Stavropol Krai. Soviet authorities renamed the former republic's towns and villages to obliterate any traces of the Kalmyk people. The operation proceeded with no security incidents reported, according to Soviet records.

---

## The Return

On March 17, 1956, the Soviet government officially released the Kalmyks from special settlement supervision. On January 9, 1957, a Soviet decree established the Kalmyk Autonomous Oblast. On July 29, 1958, it became the Kalmyk Autonomous Soviet Socialist Republic.

Those who returned found their homeland occupied by Russians and Ukrainians, many of whom chose to remain. By 1959, more than 60% of deported Kalmyks had returned home. Even though 72,665 persons returned by that year, there were still 33,401 of them outside Kalmykia.

Amlgn Azvanova remembers returning in 1956. "When we arrived in Kalmykia, our village had ceased to exist. The houses were empty. The local people had given the land away to another kolkhoz." She and her family "went to that village and talked to the local farmers, who invited us to stay with them."

The deportations permanently altered the ethnic composition of the region:

* **1926:** Kalmyks made up 75.6% of the population
* **1959:** Kalmyks made up only 35% (record low)
* **1989:** Only 45.4% were ethnic Kalmyks; Russians increased from 10.7% to 55.9%
* **By 1989:** Nearly 85% of Soviet Kalmyks resided in Kalmykia

The 2021 census shows 267,133 residents, with 62.5% Kalmyks, 25.7% Russians, and 11.8% others/not stated.

On November 14, 1989, the Supreme Council of the Soviet Union declared all of Stalin's deportations "illegal and criminal." Contemporary historical analyses consider these deportations persecution and a crime against humanity. More than 1,800 Kalmyks sought compensation from the Russian government as victims of the forced deportation in 2012.

## The Memorial

December 28 is officially marked as a holiday, the Day of Memory and Sorrow, since 2004. Prayers in Kalmykia's Buddhist temples are dedicated on December 28 to those who died between 1943 and 1956 as a result of the deportation.

![Monument to the Deportation of the Kalmyks in Tomsk](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/3/3a/Stalin-repressions-kalmyk-Tomsk.jpg)

The monument was created by Russian-American sculptor Ernst Neizvestny (1907-2010). It was unveiled December 28, 1996, on a day when the wind was terrible and the temperature was 20 degrees below zero. Neizvestny said: "I think about those Kalmyks on their way to Siberia in the cold waggons on December 28, 1943."

In 1993, former Kalmyk leader Kirsan Ilyumzhinov decided to organize a train of Memory so that people who survived exile could travel to Siberia and see the friends who helped them survive. In Moscow, an official asked, "What is your point, why stirring up the past?" They refused to arrange the train, and cancelled everything. Frustrated, Ilyumzhinov returned to his room in the National Hotel, where he met sculptor Neizvestny. Neizvestny said: "Do not despair, you will win! I think about those Kalmyks on their way to Siberia in the cold waggons on December 28, 1943." He helped secure presidential approval, and the Memory Train hit the road in summer 1994.

Days before the 80th anniversary, a plaque commemorating the victims was unveiled at a railway station in Volgograd, where Kalmyks were sent to Siberia. However, two days after it was unveiled, unidentified perpetrators destroyed the plaque, causing an outcry among Kalmyks. Khasikov called it "a senseless provocation" and demanded that local officials find the perpetrators.

## Kalmykia Today

The 2021 census shows 267,133 residents of Kalmykia. The republic covers 74,731 km² (28,854 sq mi).

Kalmyk is a minority language. The younger generation primarily speaks Russian, not their native Kalmyk. The government has made attempts to revive the language. Some laws have been passed regarding the usage of Kalmyk on shop signs; for example, on entrance doors, the words "Entrance" and "Push-Pull" appear in Kalmyk.

In 1938, the Cyrillic script replaced Todo Bichig, the traditional Kalmyk vertical script. During World War II, all Kalmyks not fighting in the Soviet Army were forcibly exiled to Siberia and Central Asia, where they were dispersed and not permitted to speak Kalmyk in public places. As a result, the Kalmyk language was not formally taught to the younger generation.

Kalmykia is the only region in Europe where Buddhism is the predominant religion, practiced by the majority ethnic group (62.5% of residents). In October 2025, over 90,000 devotees visited a sacred exhibition of Buddha relics at the Geden Sheddup Choikorling Monastery (Golden Abode of Shakyamuni Buddha). The 3rd International Buddhist Forum was held September 24-28, 2025 in Elista.

![Golden Temple (Burkhan Bakshin Altan Sume) in Elista, the largest Buddhist temple in Europe and Russia](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/2c/Golden_Temple_Elista_Kalmykia.jpg)

The Golden Temple (Burkhan Bakshin Altan Sume) was opened December 27, 2005. It is the largest Buddhist temple in Europe and Russia. It has seven levels and stands 63 meters tall. The prayer hall contains a 9-meter (30 ft) statue of Buddha Shakyamuni, covered in gold leaf and encrusted with diamonds.

Kalmykia is located in southern Russia, bordering the Caspian Sea. The average January temperature is −5°C (23°F) and the average July temperature is 24°C (75°F). The small town of Utta holds Russia's hottest temperature record: 45.4°C (113.7°F) on July 12, 2010.

Parts of Kalmykia lie 27 meters (89 ft) below sea level in the Caspian Depression. The republic's wildlife includes the rare saiga antelope, whose habitat is protected in Chyornye Zemli Nature Reserve. The saiga population has suffered dramatic declines due to poaching and habitat loss.
