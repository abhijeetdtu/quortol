# The Last Laugh: Inside the Vanishing Profession of Political Cartoonists

**By Quortol Editorial Team**

---

## **When Three Pulitzer Winners Were Let Go in One Day**

At 4:05 a.m. on a Tuesday in July 2023, Jack Ohman, president of the Association of American Editorial Cartoonists and winner of the 2016 Pulitzer Prize for editorial cartooning, received a call from his editor at the Sacramento Bee. His job was ending. The paper would no longer publish opinion cartoons. Just blocks away, in Lexington, Kentucky, Joel Pett, another Pulitzer winner whose 2000 prize had recognized decades of political commentary, learned the same fate. And 1,500 miles south, in Charlotte, North Carolina, Kevin Siers—36 years as cartoonist at the Observer, 2014 Pulitzer recipient—read a memo stating his position would be eliminated. Three cartoonists. One day. No other layoffs in the newspaper industry that week carried the same symbolic weight. [Washington Post]

"This is just another brick in the wall," Ohman told The Post. "But we haven't had three Pulitzer [cartoon] winners go out the door on the same day before." [Washington Post]

---

## **A Profession Near Extinction**

The political cartoon—the sharp, visual commentary that once held power to shape public opinion, skewer presidents, and document the absurdities of American politics—faces an extinction event. At the start of the 20th century, there were approximately 2,000 editorial cartoonists employed by newspapers in the United States. Today, fewer than 40 staff positions remain, and that number continues shrinking. The profession that traced its lineage back to Benjamin Franklin's 1754 "Join, or Die" cartoon—arguably the first editorial cartoon published in America—has been systematically dismantled by an industry in retreat. [Herb Block Foundation; Columbia Journalism Review]

---

## **The Golden Age That Never Came Back**

Herbert L. Block, known to the world as "Herblock," was 19 years old when his career began in 1929 for the Chicago Daily News. By the time he joined the Washington Post in 1946, political cartoons were already an established part of American journalism, their lineage stretching back to Benjamin Franklin's 1754 "Join, or Die"—a segmented snake representing the colonies, with the caption calling for unity against Native Americans and the French. [Herb Block Foundation; Library of Congress]

The mid-20th century represented what industry experts call the "golden age" of editorial cartooning. By 1957, when the Association of American Editorial Cartoonists (AAEC) was formed, there were 275 staff cartoonists employed nationwide. Block himself would go on to win three Pulitzer Prizes (1942, 1954, 1979) and share a fourth for coverage of the Watergate scandal in 1973. His career spanned 72 years, covering everything from the Great Depression to the end of the Cold War. [Library of Congress; Herb Block Foundation]

Thomas Nast's 19th-century work for Harper's Weekly popularized the Democratic donkey and Uncle Sam, while Block's own drawings influenced public opinion enough to aggravate politicians including Senator Joseph McCarthy, who publicly denounced him. Block once broke ranks with his publishers on specific issues, voicing his own opinion in every cartoon he drew. [Library of Congress]

The profession's reach was extraordinary: at the start of the 20th century, there were approximately 2,000 editorial cartoonists employed by American newspapers. The art form was so culturally central that newspapers often placed cartoons on their front pages, not buried in the opinion section. [Herb Block Foundation]

## **The Brick by Brick Destruction**

By 2007, things were already deteriorating. Matt Bors, then 24, attended the AAEC's 50th annual convention at Washington's Mayflower Hotel, the youngest person invited to a gathering that had once been a true professional association. He'd recently signed a syndication deal with United Media—making him the youngest syndicated cartoonist in the country at the time. [Columbia Journalism Review]

"I'd been cartooning professionally for four years," Bors recalled. "I had nearly full-time employment drawing three editorial cartoons a week, and I filled every other waking hour with freelance illustration and work on a graphic novel." [Columbia Journalism Review]

The convention, he later realized, was a "last hurrah." It was a financial disaster for the AAEC, leaving the organization mired in debt. Over the next decade-plus, venues became smaller and humbler, attendance dwindled and aged. His syndication deal never paid him more than $20,000 annually. He wasn't the future of editorial cartoons; he was one of the last people making a go at it. [Columbia Journalism Review]

Then came the cascade of layoffs. In 2020, Pulitzer Prize winners Signe Wilkinson and Tom Toles retired from the Philadelphia Daily News and Washington Post—respectively—without their jobs being filled. In 2023, the McClatchy newspaper chain, which owns 30 U.S. newspapers, let go of three Pulitzer-winning cartoonists on a single Tuesday. [Columbia Journalism Review; Washington Post]

McClatchy's national opinion editor Peter St. Onge explained the decision as part of a "continuing evolution" driven by "changing reader habits and our relentless focus on providing the communities we serve with local news and information they can't get elsewhere." [Washington Post]

In other words: the paper chain decided cartoons were no longer essential to the local news mission. They'd be found online.

---

## **The Economics of Opinion**

The economics behind this decision are stark. Newspaper profits have been declining for years, with circulation dropping and the traditional advertising base evaporated. By 2023, total TV station revenue was anticipated to drop more than 9%. Editorial content—including opinion sections—is seen as non-essential compared to hard news and sports. [The Conversation; CNN]

Gannett, the nation's largest newspaper chain with more than 250 dailies, began in 2022 to rethink its opinion sections. Opinion pages would only appear a couple days a week. Executives reasoned these pages were not heavily read, and surveys showed readers did not want to be "lectured to." That also meant less room for cartoons. [The Washington Post]

The reasoning is there are plenty of places to find opinion online, particularly on national issues. Political endorsements are more infrequent at newspapers. In 2020, only 54 of the nation's top 100 newspapers endorsed a presidential candidate, down from 92 in 2008, according to the American Presidency Project at UC Santa Barbara. [The Washington Post]

This decline in endorsements correlates with the decline in opinion content. In 2020, the Pulitzer Prize board declined to issue an award for editorial cartooning—the first time since 1973, and in one of the most politically tumultuous years in American history. The AAEC issued a fiery statement to "urge radical structural reform of the award to evaluate modern opinion cartoons by 21st century standards." [Columbia Journalism Review]

## **The Human Stories**

Kevin Siers worked at the Charlotte Observer from 1987 to 2023, a 36-year run. His 2014 Pulitzer recognized "his thought-provoking cartoons drawn with a sharp wit and bold artistic style." His work skewered national and local politicians of both major parties. [Axios Charlotte]

"I'm thankful for the great support I've had over the years from my editors, my Observer colleagues, and from our readership," Siers wrote on Facebook after his layoff. He plans to "continue to cartoon in some capacity" and will continue to have his national work syndicated. [Axios Charlotte]

Jack Ohman, president of the AAEC, was "stunningly blindsided" when informed of his firing. His Facebook post announcing the layoff received over 1,500 reactions in a few hours and hundreds of positive comments. California Governor Gavin Newsom sent him a message offering support. [The Washington Post]

"I don't know what to make of it," Ohman added, noting that he was the only Bee staffer to be laid off. [The Boston Globe]

Joel Pett, who won the 2000 Pulitzer Prize, saw his freelance contract end. He had been involved in a public battle with Kentucky Attorney General Daniel Cameron, a Republican candidate for governor, who accused Pett of being a race-baiter in his cartoons and called for his firing—not knowing that hours earlier, his wish had been granted. [The Washington Post]

## **The Social Media Paradox**

The paradox of the modern era is this: political cartoons are more widely shared than ever, yet no one is paying the cartoonists for them. "Still, artists and cartoonists are thriving on social media," Heywood Reynolds wrote on The Daily Cartoonist. "By 'thriving,' I mean their work is widely shared—but almost no one is paying them." [The Daily Cartoonist]

Bors, who quit political cartooning at 37 after drawing about 1,600 cartoons over 18 years, notes the financial reality: "while the market for political cartoons is shrinking, graphic novels are booming. Last year, graphic novels and comic books accounted for $1.28 billion in sales in North America. (The revenue from political cartooning, meanwhile, must be the combined income of the last 30 people doing it.)" [Columbia Journalism Review]

Some cartoonists have moved to independent platforms. Bors runs *The Nib*, an online magazine funded by subscribers. Many contributors don't draw traditional political cartoons but rather "memoir and essay" comics that comment on the state of the world. [Columbia Journalism Review]

Kendra Wells, a 30-year-old *Nib* contributor, views political cartoons as "stuffy old guys whose one-panel strips get into newspapers or *Time* magazine." [Columbia Journalism Review]

## **The Cultural Cost**

Editorial cartoons served a specific democratic function: they provided a visual commentary on power, holding officials accountable while reaching audiences who might not read a full story. The art form was so important that Block's publisher would never fire him when news broke that he'd won a Pulitzer—he was already immune to being fired. [Library of Congress]

The decline represents more than lost jobs. It's the loss of a particular kind of cultural commentary that could be sharp, specific, and immediate. As Ohman told The Washington Post, "At the end of the day, I think people like cartoons. But it's hard for a cartoon to be ecumenical." [The Washington Post]

The profession has been winnowed further by buyouts, layoffs, and age. Fewer than 1,400 daily newspapers remain today, several thousand below the 1913 peak. Since then, the nation's population has more than tripled to 311 million. [Herb Block Foundation]

---

## **What Remains**

Despite the decline, political cartoons haven't disappeared entirely. They've migrated to digital platforms, independent websites, and social media. But the ecosystem that supported salaried cartoonists—newspaper chains, syndicates, editors who valued opinion content—has collapsed.

The profession that once employed 2,000 people and influenced presidents, that could aggravate McCarthy and document the Watergate scandal, that could draw millions of readers every month through newspapers, is now a niche practice. It survives in the margins.

Bors left at what he considers the "right moment." He might dip into political cartoons again someday. Or maybe he's done for good. Maybe *The Nib* will collapse. Or maybe his stress-induced chest pains will kill him. "All I know is," he writes, "that, even if political cartoons are disappearing, comics with politics in them are everywhere. I've stopped caring what people call them." [Columbia Journalism Review]

**Final Word**

The story of the editorial cartoonist's decline is not one of failure. It's a story of an art form that proved its value for over two centuries, that influenced presidents and held power to account, that became so culturally central that newspapers placed it on front pages. And then, brick by brick, it was dismantled—not by authoritarian censorship but by economics, by indifference, by the slow erosion of what once seemed like permanent institutions.

The last laughs are being told online, where no one pays the tellers. The profession survives in the margins, like many other forms of journalism in an era where opinion is less valued and editors are afraid of offending readers. The cartoonists themselves are moving on, finding new ways to comment on the state of the world.

But the institution that once employed 2,000 people, that could reach millions of readers daily through newspapers, is gone. The laughter that once came from the opinion pages of American newspapers—the sharp, specific, immediate commentary on power—has been silenced. Not with a shout, but with a memo, an email, a layoff notice.

And somewhere in Washington, the Library of Congress preserves the drawings of a man named Herbert Block, who spent 72 years drawing them, who once said he'd rather "be a critic than a cheerleader." His collection of 14,000 original ink and graphite drawings—spanning from 1929 to 2001—now sits in the Prints & Photographs Division, preserved for posterity, a monument to a profession that's disappearing even as it speaks. [Library of Congress]

The cartoonists are gone. The laughter is faint. And the country is poorer for it.
