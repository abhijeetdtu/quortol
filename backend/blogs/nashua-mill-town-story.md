## At the River That Powered a City

At Mine Falls, where the Nashua River drops sharply toward its confluence with the Merrimack, the water once turned a three-mile-long granite canal that fed the looms of New England's textile empire. By 1836, three cotton mills and their 710 looms had produced more than 9.3 million yards of cloth annually on the river's south bank. The river did not stop working. By the 1860s, six railroad lines crossed the mill town, with 56 trains arriving and departing daily. Workers lived within walking distance of the mills, and the company owned the largest contiguous portion of property, encompassing 400 acres. When the Nashua Manufacturing Company closed its doors in 1948, nearly 2,000 workers—roughly one-third of the city's population—found themselves without employment. [NPS.gov, Wikipedia, Britannica]

---

## Why Nashua's Mill Era Still Matters

Nashua's identity as New Hampshire's "Gate City" emerged from a 125-year industrial arc. From its 1823 incorporation to its 1948 closure, the Nashua Manufacturing Company served as the city's largest employer, economic engine, and social architect. The mill town transformed from a rural settlement into a manufacturing center that rivaled Lowell and Manchester along the Merrimack River. The 1948 closure was not merely an economic event but a civic trauma that reshaped the city's trajectory, forcing a post-industrial reinvention that continues today through the Millyard district's redevelopment. [Nashua.gov, UnionLeader.com, NPS.gov]

## What Happened: Industrial Development, 1823–1938

### Water Power as the Engine of Transformation

Long before European settlers ventured into the Merrimack River Valley, Indigenous peoples of the Algonquian language group lived there. They fished in the rivers and streams, hunted in the heavily wooded forests, and harvested from the sandy soil of the plain. The Algonquin name "Nashua" means "beautiful stream with a pebbly bottom," with an alternative interpretation of "land between two rivers." [City-data.com, Wikipedia, Britannica]

The Massachusetts Bay Colony issued land grants during the 1650s. About 1666, Judge Edward Tyng arrived in the region, purchasing land in what would become Tyngsboro, Massachusetts, and seeing the Nashua River area as a future economic stronghold. The town of Dunstable was chartered by the Massachusetts General Court on October 26, 1673, though settlement may have occurred earlier during the 1650s-1660s. [Britannica, Rootsweb, City-data.com]

The 1741 boundary dispute between Massachusetts and New Hampshire placed the northern section of Dunstable in New Hampshire five years later, when it was incorporated as Dunstable, New Hampshire. In 1836, the New Hampshire legislature renamed the town Nashua after the Nashua River. The town split in 1842 over the location of the town hall—northern "Nashville" and southern "Nashua"—for 11 years before being reunited as the City of Nashua in 1853. [Britannica, Wikipedia, Rootsweb]

Then came the industrial transformation. Daniel Abbott, considered the "father of Nashua," chartered the Nashua Manufacturing Company on June 18, 1823, on the initiative to buy up land from the banks of the Merrimack along the Nashua River up to Mine Falls. The company hired Asher Benjamin to design churches, while engineer James F. Baldwin served as chief engineer for the mill buildings and the three-mile-long power canal. After the canal was complete, the company built more mill buildings and hired more labor, helping the town's population rise from 1,142 to 2,417 in the years 1820-1830. [Wikipedia, NPS.gov]

The company's first task was to harness and control the available water power by building a 30-foot dam at Mine Falls and the three-mile-long power canal to channel the water to the new mill site. Actual cotton production began in 1826. The lower water rights were sold in 1826 at which time the competing Indian Head Mills began operations downstream. [NPS.gov, Wikipedia]

By 1836, the Nashua Manufacturing Company employed approximately 1,000 people and produced 13 million yards of cloth annually. Earlier estimates from 1836 showed the Nashua Corporation with three mills and 710 looms producing approximately 9.3 million yards of cloth annually. It employed mostly female workers who lived within easy walking distance of the complex. The company owned forty tenements on adjacent blocks and built two churches to hire enough workers to man the machinery. [NPS.gov, Wikipedia]

In 1835, one of the founders founded the Nashua Bank. The Indian Head National Bank was established as "The Indian Head National Bank of Nashua" in 1851 as a separate institution. The relationship with the Nashua Bank is not clearly documented. The creation of a new railroad line to Nashua from Lowell, Massachusetts, in 1838 gave the local economy a further boost, and the Nashua Iron Company opened, specializing in locomotive parts. [Wikipedia, NPS.gov]

### Six Railroads, Five Mills, and One River

Six railroad lines crossed the mill town—Boston, Lowell and Nashua; Worcester and Nashua; Nashua and Acton; Nashua and Wilton; Concord and Nashua; and Rochester railroads—with 56 trains arriving and departing daily in the years before the Civil War. These various railroads led to all sections of the country, north, east, south, and west. [Wikipedia, Rootsweb]

The Nashua Manufacturing Company bought several other textile manufacturers over the 20th century, including Jackson Company (1916), Indian Head Mills (1924), and Tremont and Suffolk Mills (1926). The company owned the largest contiguous portion of these properties encompassing 400 acres and is now protected as Mine Falls Park and the Nashua Manufacturing Company Historic District. [Wikipedia, NPS.gov]

By 1860, Nashua had a population of 12,000 and was the second largest commercial center in Hillsboro County. The former Nashua Manufacturing Company mill building (dating to 1823/1830) was historically restored in the 1980s into Clocktower Place Apartments, now housing 326 residential units. [Wikipedia, UnionLeader.com]

The Nashua Manufacturing Company Historic District was listed on the National Register of Historic Places in 1987. It encompasses an area just west of downtown Nashua, roughly located along the southern bank of the Nashua River, bordered on the west side by Mine Falls Park, on the south side by the Nashua River canal, up to Ledge Street, and from the east side by Factory, Pine and Water streets, up to the Main Street bridge. [NPS.gov, Wikipedia]

The district displays a dramatic contrast between 19th-century architecture and the bucolic Nashua River. The 3-mile-long, granite-faced power canal remains intact, and the major buildings range from exuberant Italianate mills to more modest early 20th-century industrial mills and warehouses. [NPS.gov, Nashua.gov]

### Life in the Mill Town

Life in the textile manufacturing business, from the necktie-wearing, well-compensated bosses down to the production line laborers, wasn't always bright and sunny. The steady work attracted first Irish and then French-Canadian immigrants. Over time, the Nashua Manufacturing Company laid out the city's streets, built its churches, encouraged business enterprises, and erected homes and boarding houses for its workers. [City-data.com, Rootsweb, Wikipedia]

The Civil War followed, and the Nashua mills produced thousands of suits of cotton underwear for the Union Army soldiers. During the 1870s, the Jackson Manufacturing Company employed hundreds of workers. [City-data.com, Wikipedia]

In 1836, Nashua was the third largest city of New Hampshire after Dover and Portsmouth. The company built forty tenements on adjacent blocks and two churches to hire enough workers. In 1870, the Nashua Historical Society was founded as a volunteer-run, independent, non-profit organization dedicated to "Preserving the Past for the Future." [Wikipedia, N Magazine]

## The 1948 Closure and Post-Industrial Transition

The dramatic burst of industrial development in the two subsequent decades was due to two factors — the combined presence of water transportation and the availability of water power to drive new mills. These events were triggered by the incorporation of the Nashua Manufacturing Company in 1823. [NPS.gov]

The historical significance of separate buildings within the district pertains to their roles within the textile manufacturing production process. Each building's design and siting reflect the specific use for which it was constructed. The original four mills were located directly off the power canal, and as the company grew, additional mills, warehouses, bleacheries, and dye houses were built close by. [NPS.gov]

The most game-changing event in the mills' history occurred in 1948, when news broke that the mills were shutting down. Nearly two years leading up to the closure, Textron Inc. had acquired the Nashua Manufacturing Company in 1945 and liquidated the mill in 1947, throwing some 2,000 people out of work when the city had a population of about 25,000. [Wikipedia, Nashua.gov, UnionLeader.com]

The closure was not merely an economic event but a civic trauma. The city had grown up around the mills, with workers living within walking distance of the complex. The company had built the city's streets, churches, and bank. When the mills closed, nearly one-third of the city's population lost their jobs. [Nashua.gov, Wikipedia]

Thanks to a group of forward-thinking Nashuans, the dark clouds of the 1948 shutdown began to dissipate over the next few years. The Nashua New Hampshire Foundation, established in 1941, worked to attract companies to the area by offering competitive rental and leasing rates. [UnionLeader.com]

## What Happened: Post-Industrial Transformation

In 1945, Textron Inc. acquired the Nashua Manufacturing Company and its mill facilities. In 1948, after only three years, Textron shut down the Nashua mills. Following the collapse of the traditional mill businesses in the 1940s, these buildings saw new uses—residential, artist spaces, churches, and light manufacturing. [Nashua.gov]

The Millyard district has always been home to local manufacturing and industrial businesses. Since 1823, the district housed small and medium-sized industrial uses. These buildings have kept their traditional charm and property owners have allowed the interior spaces to be transformed for tenant needs. With low vacancy and low business turnover in the area, it is clear that there is demand to be located in the Millyard District. [Nashua.gov]

The Millyard District is poised to be reborn as a thriving mixed-use destination. Listed on the National Register of Historic Places, the district displays a dramatic contrast between 19th-century architecture and the bucolic Nashua River. Recent public and private investments promise to unleash the potential of this district, located just steps from the river. [Nashua.gov]

Clocktower Place, the former cotton textile mill, was historically restored in the 1980s (1987) into a thriving residential community. The building now houses 326 luxury apartment units. [Wikipedia, UnionLeader.com]

## Contradictions or Gaps

Several minor contradictions appear in sources:

1. **Settlement date:** Some sources say 1654, others say 1665, and one says 1673 as the permanent settlement. The 1654 date appears in the context of a fur trading town, while 1673 refers to the formal charter. [Wikipedia, Britannica]

2. **Town name:** One source says "Dunstable" was changed to "Nashua" on December 31, 1836, effective January 1, 1837, while another says the name change became effective in 1836. [Wikipedia, Rootsweb]

3. **Population in 1948:** Some sources say 25,000, others say about 25,000. [Wikipedia, Nashua.gov]

4. **Mills production:** One source says 9.3 million yards of cloth annually on 710 looms, another says 13 million yards. The discrepancy likely reflects different years or different company components. [Wikipedia, NPS.gov]

**Missing information:**
- Exact date of 1938 hurricane damage photos (only "September 1938" confirmed)
- Number of workers in Jackson Manufacturing Company (sources say "hundreds")
- Exact population when mills closed (25,000 cited but not precisely verified)
- Whether any mills reopened after 1948 closure (no documentation found)
- Detailed worker wages and living conditions (no specific figures found)

## Narrative Angle

The mill town story is strongest when framed around water power as both the engine and the limitation of Nashua's industrial era. The Nashua River's flow powered mills for 125 years, but when the mills closed, the river remained—now a park, a historic landmark, and a symbol of rebirth. The 1948 closure was not merely an economic event but a civic trauma that reshaped the city's identity.

The narrative arc follows:
1. **Water power as opportunity** (1823-1830s)
2. **Mill town at peak** (1860s-1930s)
3. **Closure and crisis** (1945-1948)
4. **Rebirth and reinvention** (1950s-present)

This framing emphasizes both the triumphs and limitations of industrial New England, showing how Nashua's identity emerged from the tension between water power and economic diversification.
