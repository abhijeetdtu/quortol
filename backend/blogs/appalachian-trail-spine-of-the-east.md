# The Spine of the East: Walking the Appalachian Trail Between Ecstasy and Ruin

*The Appalachian Trail is 2,197.9 miles of dirt, rock, root, and revelation — a footpath conceived as utopian socialism, built by lawyers and Depression-era laborers, invaded by insects smaller than a grain of rice, and attempted every year by 3,000 people. About one in four finishes. The other three acquire something far more complicated than a finish line.*

---

It begins, for most, in the heat of a Georgia spring, on a rounded wooded summit called Springer Mountain, 3,782 feet above sea level, where a small plaque marks mile zero. There is no grand archway, no ribbon-cutting apparatus. Just trees, rocks, a metal sign, and the sudden vertiginous fact that you must walk back down this mountain before climbing the next one — and the next, and the next, for 2,197.9 miles, through 14 states, 464,500 feet of cumulative elevation gain — the equivalent of climbing Everest 16 times — and roughly five million steps.

One hiker, arriving at Springer with an 85-pound backpack, a laptop, and a solar panel, wrote that he was "exhausted and I was just getting started." He sent a photo to his mother. "I'm here at the top of the world mom," he typed. "My Journey Begins…"

But the trail is not the top of the world. It is, in every way that matters, the opposite: a long, slow descent into the real.

---

## I. The Dream

The Appalachian Trail began not as a hiking path but as a utopian prescription for the sickness of modern life — which is to say, it began as the kind of idea that sounds noble in a lecture hall and improbable on a mountainside.

In 1921, a professional forester and regional planner named Benton MacKaye published an essay in the *Journal of the American Institute of Architects* titled "An Appalachian Trail: A Project in Regional Planning." Its opening lines read:

"Something has been going on in this country during the past few strenuous years which, in the din of war and general upheaval, has been somewhat lost from the public mind. It is the slow quiet development of a special type of community — the recreation camp. It is something neither urban nor rural. It escapes the hecticness of the one, the loneliness of the other. And it escapes also the common curse of both — the high powered tension of the economic scramble."

MacKaye had suffered a devastating personal loss: his wife Betty died by suicide in April of that year. According to biographer Larry Anderson, the tragedy "was the turning point of a lifetime, the event that set [MacKaye] on a path to his greatest social contributions." Within weeks, MacKaye began writing his proposal, channeling grief into a plan for communal salvation through wilderness — as if the forest could absorb what the city had failed to heal.

The trail MacKaye envisioned was never merely recreational. He saw it as a "flank attack on the problems of social readjustment" — a cooperative antidote to capitalism's "mutual fleecing." The path would link a chain of "community camps" where urban workers could rediscover self-sufficiency, escape industrial exploitation, and live, as he put it, "on a nonprofit basis." He was a member of the Harvard Socialist Club; his editors consciously scrubbed the more radical language from the proposal, replacing it with the safer word "recreation." One suspects they understood that America will tolerate almost any idea as long as it is called a hobby.

What the trail became was different from what MacKaye imagined — less commune, more footpath. But the core insight survived: that walking the length of the Appalachians could strip away the unnecessary and return a person to something essential. The commune died; the corridor lived.

## II. The Builders

MacKaye was the prophet. Myron H. Avery was the general — and generals, as a rule, are more useful than prophets when there is actual work to be done.

Avery, a Maine-born lawyer, took over leadership of the fledgling Appalachian Trail Conference in 1931 and accelerated construction with military efficiency. His method: organize a trail club for each section; determine the best route; clear, mark, and maintain it; collect data and publish guidebooks — which he initially printed with his own money. It is a law of human nature that the people who care most about an idea are always the ones who end up paying for it.

The early volunteers worked with Boy Scout axes and pruning shears. The workweek ended Saturday noon. Dirt roads were terrible for driving. "It was difficult to persuade people to do all that work for nothing," one early member recalled. "They said nobody would ever use the trails anyway."

They built it anyway. There is something magnificent about people who persist in constructing something they have been assured nobody wants.

Between 1928 and 1932, with the help of the Civilian Conservation Corps — the Depression-era federal work program — crews constructed and marked about 260 miles of trail from the Susquehanna River to Rockfish Gap in Shenandoah National Park. By 1934, clubs reported completion of 1,937 miles. The CCC built the trail's iconic three-sided Adirondack shelters, following design guidelines published by the National Park Service in 1938.

The final link was completed without fanfare on August 14, 1937, by a six-man CCC crew in the Maine woods. They then moved on to build more shelters — a scene so unceremonious it might have been designed to frustrate anyone who enjoys ribbon-cuttings and speeches.

Avery, upon completion, told the conference: "Rather than a sense of exultation, this situation brings a fuller realization of our responsibilities. To say that the Trail is completed would be a complete misnomer. Those of us, who have physically worked on the Trail, know that the Trail, as such, will never be completed."

He was prescient. In 1938, a hurricane broke the trail's continuity for years. The Blue Ridge Parkway displaced nearly 120 miles of the original route — what Avery called "the major catastrophe in Appalachian Trail history." The path was not fully reconnected until 1951.

Avery died in 1952. By then, he had walked every mile of the trail — in sections — and become the first officially recognized "2,000-miler." He had earned the right to say the trail would never be finished by being the man who, more than anyone else, had finished it.

## III. The Numbers

![The Spine of the East: Elevation Gain and Loss by State](/api/blog/images/appalachian_elevation_profile.png)
*Cumulative climb: 526,664 feet — equivalent to climbing Everest nearly 18 times. Climb in green, descent in brown, ordered from Georgia (top) to Maine (bottom).*

By the numbers, the Appalachian Trail defies easy comprehension — which is, of course, exactly the point of numbers that large:

- **Length**: 2,197.9 miles (varies slightly each year due to relocations)
- **Elevation gain/loss**: 464,500 feet cumulative
- **States traversed**: 14
- **Steps required**: approximately 5 million
- **Annual visitors**: 16.9 million recreation visits in 2025
- **Annual thru-hike attempts**: approximately 3,000
- **Completion rate**: about 25-33%
- **Total 2,000-milers reported**: more than 20,000 since 1937
- **Volunteer hours (2024)**: 176,504 hours from 5,000+ volunteers
- **Average pack weight at start (2025)**: 30.4 pounds
- **Average daily mileage for thru-hikers**: 13.8 miles
- **Average duration of a thru-hike**: 152 days

These are abstractions. On the ground, the numbers become something else — something that involves your knees.

"The average pack weight was 30.4 lbs.," the ATC reported in 2025, "which is roughly equivalent to carrying a medium-sized bag of dog food or a microwave on your back." The comparison is absurd and precise, like everything about the trail. It is also the sort of fact that makes you realize how rarely we describe our own lives in terms of dog food.

The states follow a predictable difficulty curve. Maryland and West Virginia are easiest — the trail's flat country. New Hampshire and Maine are hardest. The USGS elevation data breaks down starkly: New Hampshire alone demands 53,182 feet of climbing and 50,615 feet of descent; Maine demands 67,280 feet up and 65,000 down. Georgia requires "only" 20,407 feet of climbing. The quotation marks around "only" are doing a great deal of work.

## IV. The Walk

A hiker named Willy "Catchup" set out in 2022 imagining himself a "solitary poet monk, wandering through endless woods in blissful silence a la Henry David Thoreau." By his first night at Stover Creek Shelter in Georgia, he had already found people he would hike with for the next two months. "We played cards, we laughed, we might've even sang together," he wrote. "It was the first night of my thru-hike but it was already abundantly clear to me that the types of people who're attracted to something as gargantuan and difficult as the Appalachian Trail are my people."

The trail, it turns out, has a terrible sense of dramatic irony. You arrive seeking solitude; it gives you community. You arrive seeking community; it gives you solitude. It gives you, in short, whatever you least expected and most needed.

Catchup eventually forced a silent meditation in the 100-Mile Wilderness of Maine — eight days of not speaking — and found that "by forcing myself to stay silent and still, the sights, sounds, and smells were heightened to a level I didn't think possible." He had to walk a thousand miles to discover what any good librarian could have told him, but there is a difference between being told something and earning the right to believe it.

Bill Bryson captured the strange cognitive shift in *A Walk in the Woods*:

"Distance changes utterly when you take the world on foot. A mile becomes a long way, two miles literally considerable, ten miles whopping, fifty miles at the very limits of conception. The world, you realize, is enormous in a way that only you and a small community of fellow hikers know."

"Life takes on a neat simplicity, too. Time ceases to have any meaning. When it is dark, you go to bed, and when it is light again you get up, and everything in between is just in between."

"All that is required of you is a willingness to trudge."

"There is no point in hurrying," Bryson continued, "because you are not actually going anywhere. However far or long you plod, you are always in the same place: in the woods."

This is the romantic version. It is true, and it is not the whole truth. It omits what happens when trudging ceases to feel philosophical and begins to feel like a decision you are starting to regret.

## V. The Breaking

The same trail that produces Bryson's "low-level ecstasy" also produces days of screaming. It is a testimony to the trail's complexity that it can accommodate both.

One hiker, keeping a journal under the pseudonym "Wormwood" during a 2025 thru-hike, wrote an entry titled "Walk With Me In Hell":

"It's the first day on trail that I've broken down and started screaming into the forest. It's been the first day that I've dropped to one knee and struggled to rise again, and it's been the first day that I've completely fallen to the ground and just laid there for several minutes before even making an attempt to get back up."

"I hated everything about the Appalachian Trail today. I hated the trail, the people who made it, the people who walk it, my own choice to be here, and my excitement to share the hike via these journals."

The cause was not a single catastrophe but an accumulation of miseries: 92-degree heat, 87% humidity, a heat index around 105 degrees. The hiker described rings of sweat-water wrung from a microfiber towel, bugs drowning in the layer of wetness on his skin, a black fly lodging in the back of his throat. "I sweat so much that I became dizzy." There is a peculiar honesty in admitting that your body has become a habitat for other creatures' deaths.

Later, he wrote of "The Ugly on the Outside":

"This trail has reduced me in so many ways. Down to the things that I needed, stripping away and degrading the things that didn't serve me. By the end, all that's left is mostly who you are."

"My beard has grown scraggly, my hair scraggly, matted, and longer than it's ever been in my life. My skin has lost the tan it had at the beginning. My upper body has shriveled. My legs have broken out in folliculitis."

He considered the exchange worthwhile: ugliness on the outside for clarity within. This is the sort of bargain that civilization spends a great deal of energy trying to avoid, and that the trail insists upon.

The 2024 Trek survey confirmed that 8% of hikers who exited the trail early did so because of weather — before Hurricane Helene made that figure even more stark. In 2025, the percentage of unplanned exits caused by injury rose nearly 5% from the prior year. Nature, it seems, is not merely indifferent to human ambition; it is actively competitive with it.

## VI. The Grim Arithmetic

![Causes of Death on the Appalachian Trail](/api/blog/images/appalachian_fatalities.png)
*Approximately 200 fatalities since 1930. Accidents account for 60% of deaths; overexertion for 20%; medical conditions for 10%.*

The Appalachian Trail kills people. This is not said to be dramatic; it is said because it is true, and because the truth deserves the same directness as the statistics that follow.

According to data compiled from NPS Morning Reports, approximately 200 fatalities have occurred on or near the AT since 1930. The leading causes: accidents (60%), including falls, vehicle collisions, and trespasser injuries; overexertion (20%), including heart attacks, heat stroke, and dehydration; medical conditions (10%); wildlife encounters (5%); and weather-related events (5%).

Males account for approximately 70% of fatalities. The 50-64 age demographic is disproportionately represented at 23% of deaths. It appears that middle-aged men, having spent decades mastering the art of ignoring their own limitations, are the most eager to test them against a mountain.

In September 2024, a trio of trail runners near Tinker Cliffs in Botetourt County, Virginia, found a hiker lying face-down on the side of the trail, still wearing his backpack. He was cold to the touch. The man was identified as Thomas Vanetten, 67, of Schenectady, New York. There was "no obvious trauma or signs of a fall." Sometimes the trail does not offer a story, only an end.

In July 2023, a 67-year-old backpacker was last heard from at the Inn at the Long Trail in Killington, Vermont. Days later, searchers recovered his body 1.5 miles downstream from where the trail crosses Stony Brook — a flashflood victim. The last thing he did was check into an inn named after the trail itself.

In March 2024, the NPS announced a norovirus outbreak among hikers in North Carolina, Georgia, and Tennessee. In May 2024, someone scattered dog treats stuffed with fish hooks along the trail near the George W. Outerbridge shelter in Pennsylvania. Humanity, it turns out, does not improve merely because one has entered the woods.

Garmin, whose inReach satellite devices have become standard equipment, reported in its 2025 Year in Review that it now sees more than 3,000 SOS incidents annually across its network. Injury is the top reason for activation. About one-third of rescues requiring outside help involved helicopter extraction.

And yet — the ATC emphasizes that "the Appalachian Trail is a relatively safe place to visit." The qualification matters. The word "relatively" is doing the work of an entire legal department.

## VII. The Weather Above the Trees

![Mount Washington: The Worst Weather on Earth](/api/blog/images/appalachian_mt_washington.png)
*Average annual temperature: 28.0 degrees F. Average annual snowfall: 281.8 inches. Record wind gust: 231 mph. Record wind chill: -108 degrees F (2023).*

The most dangerous part of the Appalachian Trail, climatically speaking, is not in the deep woods. It is above treeline in New Hampshire's Presidential Range, where the trail crosses the flanks of Mount Washington — a mountain that seems to have taken the name "Presidential" as a justification for behaving like a despot.

Mount Washington's weather observatory, in operation since 1932, holds records that sound like a dare: an average annual temperature of 28.0 degrees; 281.8 inches of snow per year; a record low of -47 degrees (January 1934); a record wind gust of 231 mph (April 1934) — the highest surface wind speed ever measured by instruments anywhere on Earth until 1999.

In February 2023, the wind chill dropped to -108 degrees, probably the lowest ever recorded in the United States. The summit was colder than the average temperature on Mars that week. One hesitates to say that Mars is more hospitable than New Hampshire, but the numbers do not leave much room for local pride.

For thru-hikers, this is not a curiosity but a gauntlet: 100 miles of exposed, technical terrain, above treeline for miles at a time, where a sunny afternoon can turn into a hypothermia emergency within minutes. It is the sort of place that reminds you that weather is not a backdrop but an adversary, and one that does not observe weekends.

## VIII. The Forest That Is Dying

The trail's forests are transforming in slow motion, and the hiker averaging 13.8 miles per day may not notice. But the change is ecosystemic, and it is accelerating. The view does not change; the view is changing, and we are too close to the ground to see it happen.

The hemlock woolly adelgid (HWA) is an aphid-like insect smaller than 1.5 millimeters — a grain of sand with legs. First detected near Richmond, Virginia, in the early 1950s, it has since spread across 19 states, from Georgia to southern Maine. It feeds by piercing the base of hemlock needles with its mouthparts, depleting the tree's nutrient reserves. Infested trees seldom recover. The most destructive force in the southern Appalachian forest is something you could mistake for dust.

The consequences are not subtle. According to ecological research, the HWA has "reset successional sequences, homogenized biological diversity at landscape scales, altered hydrological dynamics, and changed forest stands from carbon sinks into carbon sources." This is scientific language for: the forest is being rewritten by an insect smaller than a fingernail.

US Forest Service modeling projects a "near complete loss of the hemlock forest type within 20 years of adelgid establishment" in the central Appalachians, with conversion to hardwood forests and "significant alterations to terrestrial and aquatic wildlife habitat."

The emerald ash borer has destroyed hundreds of millions of ash trees across North America, including thousands along the AT. It kills within two years. An infected tree has less than a 1% chance of survival. Treating a single tree with pesticide costs $120. The arithmetic is simple: there are more trees than there is money.

Marian Orlousky, the ATC's Director of Science and Stewardship, puts it plainly: "Second only to climate change, non-native invasive species are the most urgent threat to the Appalachian Trail's 2,000 miles and almost 400,000 acres of land."

The hiker walking through southern Virginia passes through hemlock groves that will not exist in their current form within two decades. The cool, dark cathedral of the southern Appalachian forest is being replaced in real time, tree by tree, by an insect you need a magnifying glass to see. The largest things are often destroyed by the smallest.

## IX. The Volunteers

No one is paid to maintain the Appalachian Trail. It is maintained entirely by the sort of people who believe that a thing worth having is worth working for without expectation of payment — a concept so foreign to modern economic thinking that it might as well be a form of rebellion.

In the 2024 federal fiscal year, more than 5,000 individual volunteers contributed 176,504 hours to the trail's upkeep. In 2023, 4,730 volunteers logged 170,147 hours — the equivalent of 19.4 years of continuous labor. The ATC estimates that the whole trail requires about 200,000 volunteer hours in a normal year.

To understand what those hours mean on the ground, consider one club. The Tennessee Eastman Hiking & Canoeing Club — one of 30 trail-maintaining clubs — logged 10,756 hours in 2024 across 442 events involving 236 people. That included 3,464 adopt-a-trail hours and 7,757 hours on special projects.

These volunteers do not just clip brush and clean water bars. They build shelters, install footbridges, construct privies, remove invasive species, inventory natural resources, and pilot bear-canister loan programs. After Hurricane Helene devastated sections of the trail in Georgia, Tennessee, North Carolina, and southwest Virginia in 2024, they rebuilt the treadway by hand. When nature breaks what we have made, we make it again — and we do not send an invoice.

The trail is managed through a unique public-private partnership: the National Park Service, the U.S. Forest Service, the ATC, and 30 trail clubs. The ATC's 2024 operating budget was $14.2 million — less than the cost of a single mile of urban highway. The entire Appalachian Trail, the spine of the eastern United States, costs less to run than a mile of asphalt.

Every shelter, every white blaze painted on a tree, every switchback that prevents erosion, every bear box, every privy — all of it is the work of unpaid hands. The trail exists because enough people decided that their time was worth less than the thing they were building.

## X. The People Who Walked Through

The history of the trail is the history of people who had reasons to walk — and the reasons, when examined, are rarely simple.

Earl Shaffer (1948) was a World War II veteran who served in the South Pacific. He walked the entire trail in 124 days to, as he put it, "walk the war out of my system." He carried a diary that is now in the Smithsonian's National Museum of American History — a battered notebook showing "wear and tear of being carried and used for 124 days in extreme temperatures and often rainy weather."

At the time, experts believed a thru-hike was impossible. Shaffer had no guidebooks, no advice, no previous footsteps to follow. When blisters threatened his hike, he put sand in his boots and wore no socks until his feet toughened. "The Indian ways are usually best in the woods," he wrote. He treated his body like terrain to be conquered rather than a system to be preserved — which, given what he had survived, was probably the only approach he knew.

Emma "Grandma" Gatewood (1955) was 67 years old, mother of 11, grandmother of 23, and survivor of three decades of domestic violence at the hands of her husband. She read an article in National Geographic about the Appalachian Trail and decided to hike it. She wore Keds sneakers, carried a denim sack slung over her shoulder, and packed a shower curtain for shelter. Her gear weighed perhaps 15 pounds.

She broke her glasses and got lost on her first attempt in 1954. She tried again in 1955, starting from Georgia on May 3. She walked 146 days, wearing out seven pairs of sneakers, falling and injuring herself repeatedly, dealing with snakes and bears. When she reached the summit of Katahdin on September 25, she sang "America the Beautiful," signed the register, and said: "I did it. I said I'd do it and I've done it."

She became the first person to hike the entire AT three times. Asked why, she answered: "Because I wanted to." She also admitted: "I thought it would be a nice lark. It wasn't." There is perhaps no better summary of the Appalachian Trail than that sentence: it seemed like a good idea, and it was not one, and she did it anyway, three times.

## XI. The Psychology of Five Million Steps

![One in Four: The AT Thru-Hiker Funnel](/api/blog/images/appalachian_thru_hiker_funnel.png)
*Of approximately 3,000 who register each year, roughly 25 to 33 percent complete the full 2,197.9 miles. The funnel narrows sharply between Springer Mountain and Harpers Ferry.*

Academic research on what happens to the human mind over 2,200 miles is thin but revealing — as thin, perhaps, as the margin between those who finish and those who do not.

A 2024 study in the Journal of Outdoor Recreation, Education, and Leadership surveyed 128 AT thru-hikers and found statistically significant differences in mental toughness between those who completed the trail and those who did not — the first empirical evidence that finishing the AT requires a specific psychological quality beyond mere motivation or resilience. The study also found that social support facilitated success; the lone wolves tended to drop out. It turns out that the romantic image of the solitary hiker is exactly that — a romance. The people who finish are the ones who let other people help.

A gender-differences study of 211 hikers found that women were more worried about crime and harassment, and more worried about managing natural elements. Men were less confident about maintaining health and hygiene. But after the hike, both groups viewed themselves equally capable — and women reported a significantly greater sense of empowerment. The trail, it seems, is a great equalizer, though it takes a different toll on the way up than on the way down.

A physiological case study followed one hiker across 139 days: 2,151 miles, 326,322 feet of elevation gain. Resting heart rate and heart rate variability regressed over the course of the trek. Fatigue accumulated until the end and did not improve despite extensive rest. The declining markers had no impact on event completion — the body can perform far beyond what feels sustainable. The body, it turns out, has read more Stoic philosophy than the mind has.

A 2025 study of thru-hikers found that prolonged hiking reduced evening serum cortisol concentrations. The stress hormone dropped. The researchers concluded that thru-hiking "could be used as an additional tool to help individuals mitigate health conditions caused by elevated evening cortisol concentrations, such as anxiety, depression, panic attacks, and PTSD."

This is the trail's paradox made scientific: it destroys the body and heals the mind, often simultaneously. It is the most inefficient antidepressant ever devised, and it works precisely because of its inefficiency.

## XII. The Detachment

Bryson again — and this may be the truest thing anyone has written about the trail:

"Life takes on a neat simplicity. You have no engagements, commitments, obligations, or duties; no special ambitions and only the smallest, least complicated of wants; you exist in a tranquil tedium, serenely beyond the reach of exasperation."

"At times, you become almost certain that you slabbed this hillside three days ago, crossed this stream yesterday, clambered over this fallen tree at least twice today already. But most of the time you don't think. No point. Instead, you exist in a kind of mobile Zen mode, your brain like a balloon tethered with string, accompanying but not actually part of the body below. Walking for hours and miles becomes as automatic, as unremarkable, as breathing."

He called this "low-level ecstasy" and argued it was something modern life systematically suppresses. Modern life, after all, has a vested interest in keeping you agitated: an agitated person buys things, checks notifications, and worries about the future. A person in a state of low-level ecstasy is not shopping.

The hiker Mr. Brandybuck, on the morning of his Katahdin summit in September 2024, wrote:

"I continued forward through the haze, utterly alone. It was around 7 a.m., so maybe a handful of other hikers were starting the trail miles below. Past the junction at Thoreau Springs, I saw a shadow emerge from the mist and a hiker and I nodded as we passed. Anyone who's waking up hours before dawn to climb a mountain alone probably doesn't want to stop to chat."

In the mist, he thought about "how many centuries people have climbed up this mountain." He felt "their spirits just out of sight." A shirtless man in short shorts emerged from the Knife's Edge, took his photo, gave advice on a different trail down, and vanished. After 90 minutes on the summit, "after disease and heartbreaking beauty," he heaved his pack over his shoulder and began the journey home. There is something deeply appropriate about a mystical summit experience being interrupted by a shirtless man with advice.

## XIII. At the End

![The Appalachian Trail by the Numbers](/api/blog/images/appalachian_by_the_numbers.png)
*Key statistics that define the world's longest hiking-only footpath, from total miles to volunteer hours to average pack weight.*

The hiker who started with the 85-pound backpack and the laptop — who could barely walk the approach trail to Springer Mountain — made it to Harpers Ferry, the psychological halfway point. He left his laptop behind somewhere in Georgia, shed 60 pounds of gear, traded boots for trail runners. By the time he reached the ATC headquarters in Harpers Ferry, he looked like every other hiker in the photo archive: confident, fit, relaxed, and at times, exultant. He had discovered the great secret of travel: you do not need most of what you carry.

The archive contains more than 18,000 such images, Polaroids from 1979 onward, now scanned and searchable online. Sixteen volunteers spent 425 hours digitizing them. Even the record of the trail requires unpaid labor.

Every face in that archive tells the same story differently: of pain and boredom and beauty and community and solitude and the strange, addictive deprivation that Bryson called "the central feature of life on the Appalachian Trail." He meant that "the whole point of the experience is to remove yourself so thoroughly from the conveniences of everyday life that the most ordinary things — processed cheese, a can of pop gorgeously beaded with condensation — fill you with wonder and gratitude."

What the trail does is reduce you. To pain. To gratitude. To five million steps. To the single irreducible question that every hiker must answer in their own way: Why keep walking?

Alex Tucker, who thru-hiked in 2024, put it this way:

"Thru hiking is a dirty, hard, tiring, uncomfortable experience that is also colorful, rewarding, beautiful, exciting and absolutely something that I would do again."

"I fear that I didn't do the trail justice in this blog. There is beauty in that too. I have experienced an adventure so big, so grand, so unique and so strange that I can't accurately describe it. I don't think I will ever be able to. I don't think I ever want to be able to."

The Appalachian Trail, in its brutal totality, offers one thing that modern life cannot: the opportunity to be completely, undeniably, unequivocally done with something that mattered. And then, as the mist clears on Katahdin, to start over.

Every year, 3,000 people walk into the woods at Springer Mountain. About one in four will touch the sign at Katahdin. The others will carry something else out of those woods — something harder to name, something that does not require a certificate or a photo. The trail gives to each what each can carry. The rest it leaves on the forest floor.
