# What the Earth Gives, What It Takes

## Geography, Power, and Paradox in the Southern Cone

---

**Byline** — *Quortol*

---

**Prologue: The Wind and the Salt and the Grass**

In the far south of the American continent, the planet still announces itself in the first person. The wind arrives from the Southern Ocean unbroken and hits the Patagonian steppe with a force that can knock a man sideways — a nuisance to the sheep ranchers who have worked this land for five generations, and a treasure to the engineers now planting 120-meter turbines in the same soil. It blows 270 days a year. One man's inconvenience is another man's endowment. In the high salt flats of Jujuy, 4,000 meters above sea level, the Kolla people have harvested salt from the white crust of Salinas Grandes for 40,000 years. Now the same brine pools contain lithium — the silvery metal upon which the world's electric future depends. The irony sits undisturbed on the surface, as quiet as the salt. In the Pampas, the deep topsoil that made Argentina one of the wealthiest nations on earth a century ago still produces grass for 12 million cattle in Uruguay alone. And in the Gran Chaco of Paraguay, the soy frontier pushes westward through dry forest, displacing Indigenous communities who have no land titles and no recourse — because the law, like the forest, parts more easily for capital than for tradition.

The Southern Cone — the tapering wedge of South America that includes Argentina, Chile, Uruguay, and Paraguay — does not merely contain natural resources. It is constructed *by* them, in the way a man is constructed by his habits: gratefully, tragically, and without much choice in the matter. Its history is a history of resource rushes: guano, wool, wheat, beef, oil, copper. Each era brought a wave of foreign capital, a transformation of the landscape, and a set of winners and losers that hardened into the next generation's starting conditions. What is happening now — the fracking boom in Vaca Muerta, the green hydrogen megaprojects in Magallanes, the lithium rush in the salt flats, the soy and beef expansion in Paraguay — is not a departure from this pattern. It is the latest iteration, delivered with the same confidence that this time the rules will be different.

And it raises a question that runs through the entire region like a fault line: *When the world wants what lies beneath your feet, who decides its value, and who bears its cost?* The answer, so far, has been consistent enough to count as a tradition.

## Part I: The Pampas — Grass That Moves Markets

Uruguay is sometimes described as one big farm. The cliché has the uncomfortable precision of a truth its inhabitants have long since stopped resisting. With 3.4 million people and 12 million cattle, the country has built its identity and its economy around grass, with the air of a man who has chosen his one good suit and intends to wear it forever. Beef exports are projected to reach 548,000 metric tons in 2025, generating approximately $2.7 billion in foreign revenue — a record driven by higher volumes, firmer prices, and a deliberate strategy of market diversification away from China. [UPI, Dec 2025](https://www.upi.com/Top_News/World-News/2025/12/16/latam-uruguay-beef-industry-performance/1351765896548/)

![Uruguay deliberately diversified its beef export destinations: China's share fell from 67% (2021) to 36% (2025), while the US grew from 12% to 33% and the EU from 9% to 18%. Total revenue hit $2.7 billion.](/api/blog/images/southern_cone/uruguay_beef_exports.png)
*Figure 4: Uruguay's beef export value by destination (2020–2025). The revenue line shows total export earnings. Source: INAC*

The numbers are impressive, but they do not capture what makes the Uruguayan model distinctive. In this country, every single head of cattle is electronically tagged at birth — the world's first completely computerized traceability system, implemented after a devastating foot-and-mouth outbreak in 2001. [BBC, Nov 2014](https://www.bbc.com/news/world-latin-america-30210749/) A consumer in Berlin or Tokyo can theoretically trace a grass-fed Uruguayan steak back to the individual animal and the farm it came from. This is not a marketing gimmick; it is a response to a structural reality. Uruguay is a small producer dwarfed by Brazil and Argentina. Its only viable strategy is premium positioning — and premium positioning requires proof. The world no longer takes your word for anything, least of all about what you serve for dinner.

The proof system works because Uruguay's institutions work. In a region where political volatility is as reliable as the seasons, Uruguay offers what the economic analysis site Econosur calls "institutional continuity, a functioning rule of law, and a track record of honoring contracts and commitments across political cycles." [Econosur, Mar 2026](https://econosur.org/countries/uruguay/) The country has the highest GDP per capita in the Southern Cone ($27,608 nominal in 2026), the lowest sovereign spreads in the region, and an energy grid that runs on more than 95% renewable electricity — wind, solar, biomass, and hydro — achieved through deliberate policy design, not natural endowment. [IMF, Oct 2025](https://www.imf.org/-/media/Files/Publications/CR/2025/English/1uryea2025001-source-pdf.ashx) Virtue, in Uruguay, is simply the most practical form of self-interest.

![Uruguay leads the Southern Cone in GDP per capita at $27,608 — more than double Paraguay's $7,692. Argentina posted the strongest pandemic recovery (+43%) while Chile grew just 20% over seven years.](/api/blog/images/southern_cone/southern_cone_gdp_per_capita.png)
*Figure 2: GDP per capita (nominal USD) in 2019 vs. 2026 across the Southern Cone. Growth labels show percentage increase over the period. Source: IMF, INDEC, Central Bank of Chile, BCP Paraguay*

But even Uruguay's stability faces pressure from the global supply chain transformation underway. The European Union's Deforestation Regulation, which requires importers to prove that commodities were not produced on recently deforested land, is reshaping the compliance landscape for beef and soy producers across the continent. Uruguay, with its existing traceability infrastructure, is better positioned than most of its neighbors. But the cost of documentation — the systems, the audits, the legal liability — is not evenly distributed. Small producers, the backbone of the gaucho culture that still animates the rural interior, face an administrative burden that favors consolidation. The paperless world, it turns out, requires a great deal of paper.

The gaucho, South America's cowboy, is a living cultural figure in Uruguay and Argentina — not a museum piece. In the department of Tacuarembó, riders still gather cattle on horseback across open pampas, doing exactly what their grandfathers did, only with a smartphone in the saddlebag. But the economics of their profession are shifting. The same global demand that has driven Uruguayan beef to record prices has also attracted foreign capital into land acquisition, processing plants, and logistics. The landscape remains green and rolling, dotted with cattle and sheep. The question is who ultimately controls the chain from pasture to plate — and whether the gaucho ends as its poet or its passenger.

Across the Río de la Plata, in Argentina, the same agricultural engine operates under radically different conditions. Argentina's Pampas produce soy, corn, wheat, and beef on a scale that makes Uruguay's output look modest. But where Uruguay offers stability, Argentina offers volatility — and the contrast is the key to understanding both countries. Argentina has defaulted on its sovereign debt nine times. It has also produced Nobel laureates and some of the most productive agricultural systems on the planet. Both facts are simultaneously true. [Econosur, Mar 2026](https://econosur.org/countries/argentina/)

This is not a contradiction. It is a structural pattern built into the geography and the political economy that has grown up around it — the habit of brilliant improvisation in the face of self-inflicted disaster. The Argentine genius is to make every crisis look like the first one, while repeating every mistake with perfect fidelity.

![Argentina's inflation rate fell from 289.4% (April 2024) to 32.6% (March 2026) under President Milei's austerity program — the fastest disinflation in modern history.](/api/blog/images/southern_cone/argentina_inflation.png)
*Figure 1: Argentina's inflation rate (CPI YoY%) from December 2023 to March 2026. The shaded area between the dashed lines marks the Milei administration. Source: INDEC / Trading Economics*

## Part II: Patagonia — The Fracking Frontier and the Hydrogen Gamble

In the dusty Patagonian town of Añelo, in the Argentine province of Neuquén, a mechanic named Fabio Javier Jiménez has watched his world transform. When he first opened his garage, he was happy to service two vehicles a day. Then fracking arrived. "Now we have 20 vehicles a day," he told reporters. [LatinAmerican Post, Dec 2025](https://latinamericanpost.com/economy-en/argentinas-fracking-boom-reshapes-patagonia-yet-leaves-its-fragile-future-uncertain/) Progress, for a mechanic, is simply more broken things to fix.

The catalyst is Vaca Muerta — "Dead Cow" — a shale formation roughly the size of Belgium that holds the world's second-largest shale gas reserves and fourth-largest shale oil reserves. Beneath the arid scrubland of northern Patagonia, a formation of black shale deposited 150 million years ago has become Argentina's most important economic asset. In 2024, Vaca Muerta produced 446,900 barrels of oil per day in December alone, contributing 54.9% of Argentina's total oil output. [IPS, Mar 2025](https://www.ipsnews.net/2025/03/argentina-experiencing-oil-boom-bright-spots-shadows/)

The macroeconomic impact has been dramatic. After decades of energy shortages and expensive imports, Argentina has achieved self-sufficiency and become a net exporter of oil and gas. President Javier Milei's government is betting that this will be the engine that pulls the country out of its chronic fiscal crises. Exports from Vaca Muerta are projected to reach $30 billion annually by 2030. [IPS, Mar 2025](https://www.ipsnews.net/2025/03/argentina-experiencing-oil-boom-bright-spots-shadows/)

But Añelo's boomtown reality is more complicated than a success story. The official population jumped from 10,788 in 2010 to 17,893 in 2022, with some 15,000 oil workers sleeping in the town each night — a floating workforce that strains public services, housing, and infrastructure. Dust, noise, and traffic clog what was once a quiet desert settlement. Earthquakes have become routine: since 2015, there have been 442 seismic events in the Vaca Muerta region. In the nearby village of Sauzal Bonito, resident Mabel Panero saw her house collapse from the repeated tremors. The government built her an earthquake-resistant replacement. Her neighbors were less fortunate. [Anita Pouchard Serra Photography](https://www.anitapouchardserra.com/sauzal-bonito) The state can be generous, provided your tragedy is sufficiently visible.

The tension between national benefit and local cost is the defining pattern of resource extraction in Patagonia. And it is about to be repeated at a much larger scale — because if there is one thing the resource economy has never lacked, it is confidence in taking a second bite.

![Vaca Muerta's share of Argentina's oil output rose from 25% (2019) to 58% (2025), transforming the country from an energy importer to a net exporter with a record $7.8 billion trade surplus.](/api/blog/images/southern_cone/vaca_muerta_production.png)

A thousand kilometers to the south, across the Strait of Magellan, a different kind of energy revolution is taking shape. In Chile's Magallanes region, the wind that once bent the trees flat is now being harnessed for green hydrogen — a fuel that, when burned, produces only water vapor, and that could replace fossil fuels in shipping, aviation, and heavy industry. The wind has been promoted from a nuisance to a resource, which is how the modern world usually addresses its inconveniences.

The numbers are staggering. The world's first commercial green hydrogen plant in Patagonia, the Haru Oni pilot project near Punta Arenas, generates e-fuels using a 3.4 MW wind turbine. But Haru Oni is a pilot. The projects on the drawing board are industrial in scale. British firms Hive and TEG are investing $8 billion in a green ammonia plant on Tierra del Fuego. The Austrian company HNH Energy has submitted an $11 billion proposal for a nearby project — the largest single project ever evaluated by Chile's Environmental Assessment System. [NACLA, Sep 2024](https://nacla.org/patagonia-green-energy-projects-new-colonialism/); [The Guardian, Nov 2025](https://amp.theguardian.com/global-development/2025/nov/14/chile-patagonia-green-energy-hydrogen-foreign-companies-pollution-indigenous-people)

The European Union is a dual investor and buyer, relying on Chile's energy revolution to produce the synthetic fuel it needs to meet its 2050 carbon neutrality targets. The German company Siemens Energy is the lead investor in Haru Oni; Porsche will be the first buyer of the synthetic fuel, which it intends to use in its Supercup race cars and Experience Centers. [Porsche/HIF Global, 2023](https://newsroom.porsche.com/dam/jcr:f3efe597-a4bc-4277-8c01-c156182bf5fc/PAG_Panamera_eFuels-Experience_Newsroom_en.pdf)

Wind-powered hydrogen exported to Europe to fuel German sports cars: this is the geometry of the green transition at its most literal. The carbon puritan drives to the race track in a car whose fuel was manufactured by someone else's weather. And on the ground in Patagonia, it is generating the same set of tensions that every previous resource boom has generated — because virtue does not abolish geography; it merely redraws the lines of who sacrifices for whom.

Michael Robertson Shaw's family has run Estancia Doménica on Tierra del Fuego since the early 20th century. His sheep graze on the same steppe where the Selk'nam people once hunted, before they were massacred in the late 19th and early 20th centuries by settlers and bounty hunters. Now his land sits adjacent to TEG's proposed Gente Grande wind farm. Robertson estimates that most local landowners are collaborating with the company, attracted by payments "10, 20 times what they earned with sheep." He predicts "a widespread collapse" of the ranching culture that has defined Patagonia for more than a century. [NACLA, Sep 2024](https://nacla.org/patagonia-green-energy-projects-new-colonialism/) History does not repeat itself, but it does offer competitive rates.

Alfonso Campos, a sheep farmer in San Gregorio on the mainland, is more direct. His farm, he says, will be encircled by three massive ammonia containers, a desalination plant, a hydrogen plant, gas pipelines, and hundreds of wind turbines. "If the ammonia leaks, it will poison everything," he told The Guardian. "The noise of the windmills will also upset the animals, and the landscape will be turned into an industrial desert." [The Guardian, Nov 2025](https://amp.theguardian.com/global-development/2025/nov/14/chile-patagonia-green-energy-hydrogen-foreign-companies-pollution-indigenous-people) The green desert is a new concept, but it looks much like the old one, only louder.

The proponents emphasize job creation. Mayor José Gabriel Parada of Porvenir, a Tierra del Fuego town with precarious services, sees green hydrogen as salvation for a community where young people are leaving and the economy depends on a single fish processing plant and modest tourism. But Nicolás Jara, a park ranger at Karukinka Park, counters: "It's not a boom for us because the money goes abroad." [NACLA, Sep 2024](https://nacla.org/patagonia-green-energy-projects-new-colonialism/)

This is the argument that surrounds every extraction project in Patagonia. The jobs argument is real but incomplete. The specialized nature of green hydrogen work means that the best-paying positions often go to imported technicians. The wealth generated passes through the local economy at a shallow depth. Meanwhile, the environmental risks — ammonia spills, brine discharge from desalination, bird strikes on turbine blades — are borne locally. The profits are globalized; the consequences are not.

In the town of Taltal, on the coast of northern Chile, Brenda Gutiérrez, president of an Indigenous fishing community, has seen this movie before. "We don't want a dead seabed, bleached white by the salt and chemicals," she said. "The sea is our life." [The Guardian, Nov 2025](https://amp.theguardian.com/global-development/2025/nov/14/chile-patagonia-green-energy-hydrogen-foreign-companies-pollution-indigenous-people) The energy transition has many scripts, but the third act never changes.

## Part III: The Lithium Triangle — Salt, Water, and the Green Paradox

The high Andean salt flats of Argentina, Bolivia, and Chile contain more than half of the world's lithium reserves — the critical mineral without which the electric vehicle revolution cannot happen. The "Lithium Triangle" covers an area of roughly 200,000 square kilometers, straddling three national borders at altitudes above 3,000 meters. It is a landscape of such stark beauty that one almost forgives the trouble it contains.

The region has been inhabited for at least 13,000 years. The Kolla and Atacama peoples developed an agro-pastoral culture adapted to these extreme conditions — herding llamas, cultivating quinoa and maize, harvesting salt from the brine pools that dot the landscape. The salt flats are not inert landscapes; they are living ecosystems, hyper-saline wetlands that regulate water flow, capture carbon, and sustain endemic species. They were never simply empty, except in the sense that the law has always found it convenient to overlook complexity.

Since the 1990s, lithium mining has been present in parts of the triangle. But the recent acceleration — driven by the global energy transition and the demand for EV batteries — has brought the industry to the doorstep of communities that had managed to hold it at bay for 15 years. The electric car, it turns out, has a very loud engine when you live on top of its fuel.

In the Salinas Grandes salt flat in Jujuy province, Argentina, 33 Indigenous communities have organized since 2010 to resist lithium extraction. They are not entirely opposed to mining in principle, but they insist on their right to Free, Prior, and Informed Consent, as established by ILO Convention 169 and the UN Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples. In 2015, they developed the Kachi Yupi ("Tracks in the Salt") Community Protocol — a document that lays out how consultation should take place. [Intercontinental Cry, May 2026](https://icmagazine.org/lithium-rush-in-argentina-raises-indigenous-rights-concerns-amid-green-energy-push/)

The government of Jujuy did not implement the protocol. When a Canadian mining company began exploratory drilling in 2019 without community consent, the 33 Communities abandoned negotiations and adopted a maximalist position: "No to lithium, yes to water and life." The slogan has the brevity of a conclusion reached after patience has been exhausted.

"We rely on the National Constitution and on ILO Convention 169 to defend our territory," said Néstor Alberto, a member of the Pozo Colorado community. "Our mandate doesn't come from the constitution, but from our grandparents: to defend Las Salinas, to prevent multinational companies from coming and stripping our land, in this case, for lithium." [Ojalá, Jul 2025](https://www.ojala.mx/en/ojala-en/life-or-lithium)

Julia Cañari, the head of the Pozo Colorado Aboriginal Community, makes soup in her kitchen while explaining the stakes. "Salt is valuable, it's a natural resource and we conserve and protect it. It's our community's source of work." [Ojalá, Jul 2025](https://www.ojala.mx/en/ojala-en/life-or-lithium) She does not need a lecture on resource economics. She has been practicing it longer than the companies have existed.

The core issue is water. Lithium extraction from brines involves pumping groundwater to the surface and evaporating it in large ponds — a process that consumes approximately 2 million liters of water per tonne of lithium. In a semi-arid region where every drop is precious, the potential impact on the region's wetlands — and on the communities that depend on them — is severe. The green transition has discovered that saving the planet requires sacrificing the watershed, and has not yet decided whether this counts as irony or strategy.

"The government is playing its game," said Leonardo Crippa, a Kolla attorney. "They're trying to pave the way, to make it easier for mining companies to work there." [The New World, Apr 2025](https://www.thenewworld.co.uk/harriet-barber-argentinas-lithium-wars/)

In December 2024, President Milei eliminated the application of the Territorial Emergency Law 26.160, which had suspended evictions of Indigenous communities pending a survey of ancestral lands. The measure had immediate consequences: the Pailako community, a Mapuche Tehuelche community in Chubut province, was evicted from land it had recovered four years earlier in Los Alerces National Park. [Ojalá, Jun 2025](https://www.ojala.mx/en/ojala-en/mapuche-lands-under-fire-in-patagonia) The law giveth, and the law taketh away — especially when the taketh serves a larger balance sheet.

On the other side of the Andes, in Chile, the newly inaugurated far-right president José Antonio Kast has signaled a more permissive approach to mining investment. He merged the Ministries of Mining and Economy into a single portfolio, appointed an agronomist with no mining experience to lead it, and promised to reduce the corporate tax rate from 27% to 23%. Chile's mining investment pipeline stands at $105 billion through 2034, concentrated in copper and lithium. [BNamericas, Mar 2026](https://www.bnamericas.com/en/analysis/mining-in-chile-between-expectations-and-risks-in-kasts-economic-agenda); [MINING.COM, Mar 2026](https://www.mining.com/chile-mining-faces-policy-test-under-kast-government/) When the world wants what you have, the world will find you accommodating ministers.

The tension between the energy transition's global benefits and its local costs is not an abstraction in the Lithium Triangle. The batteries that power electric vehicles in Europe and North America contain lithium that may have come from these salt flats. The question — posed by communities, environmental organizations, and a growing body of international human rights law — is whether the communities living on top of the resource have any meaningful say in how it is extracted. The question is old. The answers are older still.

The young Indigenous women of the Union of the Diaguita Nation Peoples have a blunt answer: "We can't just keep watching companies destroy the territories without doing something." [Debates Indígenas, Jun 2025](https://debatesindigenas.org/en/2025/06/01/indigenous-peoples-and-the-energy-transition-mining-and-pollution-in-argentina/) The patience of the dispossessed is not infinite. It only looks that way from a distance.

## Part IV: The Chaco — Soy, Dams, and the Mennonite Frontier

If the Southern Cone's western edge is defined by mountains and mineral wealth, its eastern interior — Paraguay and the Gran Chaco — is defined by flatness, heat, rivers, and soil. It is the kind of landscape that makes a virtue of monotony and a fortune of patience.

Paraguay is the smallest economy in the Southern Cone and one of the most consistently misread. It has no central bank independence problems, no serial debt defaults, no currency crises. Its economy has grown at an average of roughly 4% annually for two decades, and its currency is the most stable in the region. [Finnvera, Mar 2026](https://www.finnvera.fi/eng/finnvera/newsroom/articles/countries-and-markets-12026-latin-american-southern-cone-gains-momentum-as-a-growth-market-for) It is also one of the most unequal countries in South America, with a Gini coefficient of 0.45 and an economy built on two things: soy and hydroelectricity. Which is to say: Paraguay does not do many things, but what it does, it does with the single-mindedness of a man who has found his one good idea and intends to exhaust it.

Paraguay is the world's third-largest exporter of soybeans, shipping 8 million tonnes in the 2023/24 marketing year. Soy and its derivatives account for roughly 40% of all Paraguayan exports by value — approximately $4 billion annually. [Econosur, Apr 2026](https://econosur.org/insights/paraguay/soy-model/) The soy frontier has pushed relentlessly westward from the eastern border with Brazil into the Gran Chaco, the largest dry forest in South America after the Caatinga. The forest retreats; the soybean advances. It is not a conflict so much as a verdict.

The transformation of the Chaco into an agricultural powerhouse is the work, in large part, of Mennonite communities. The first Mennonite pioneers arrived from Canada in 1927, fleeing universal compulsory education that they saw as a threat to their religious community. They found the Chaco — described by earlier travelers as a "Green Hell" — and proceeded to irrigate, cultivate, and domesticate it. Today, approximately 38,731 Mennonites live in 25 colonies across Paraguay, concentrated in the Central Chaco. They produce the majority of the country's dairy and beef, and operate soybean processing plants, including a 240,000-tonne-capacity facility that came online in 2025 in the western Chaco. [USDA FAS, Apr 2025](https://apps.fas.usda.gov/newgainapi/api/Report/DownloadReportByFileName?fileName=Oilseeds+and+Products+Annual_Buenos+Aires_Paraguay_PA2025-0001.pdf); [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mennonites_in_Paraguay) A people who fled the modern world have become its most efficient suppliers.

The Mennonites' success has created an uncomfortable dynamic. By global standards, the colonies enjoy living standards comparable to Spain or Portugal. But the expansion of beef and soy production into the Chaco has pushed into territories used by Indigenous Ayoreo communities, some of whom live in voluntary isolation. Deforestation — the Chaco is one of the most rapidly deforesting regions in the world on a per-hectare basis — is exacerbating conflicts over land rights that the state has neither the capacity nor the political will to resolve. The Mennonites did not come to exploit; they came to build. But building and displacing are not opposites in this part of the world; they are the front and back of the same coin.

The Bi-Oceanic Corridor, a highway that will connect the Brazilian Atlantic coast to Chilean Pacific ports, running directly through the Chaco, is expected to accelerate this transformation. [The Conversation, Mar 2020](https://gcp.theconversation.com/mennonites-helped-turn-paraguay-into-a-mega-beef-producer-indigenous-people-may-pay-the-price-128383) Roads, in the developing world, are never just roads. They are decisions about whose future matters.

Upriver from the soy fields and cattle ranches, the Itaipu Dam spans the Paraná River between Paraguay and Brazil. It is the second-largest hydroelectric dam in the world by installed capacity (14,000 MW), and perhaps the most consequential infrastructure project in Paraguayan history. Built during the 1970s and 1980s under the military dictatorships of Alfredo Stroessner and Brazil's generals, Itaipu was ostensibly a binational project — a 50/50 split of energy and costs. In practice, Paraguay's share of both influence and benefit has been sharply limited. The fiction of equality is one of the more durable construction materials.

The 1973 Itaipu Treaty required Paraguay to sell its surplus power to Brazil at rates that were set by the treaty and widely regarded as unfair. For decades, Paraguay received compensation that critics argued undervalued its contribution by billions of dollars. It was only in 2023, when the construction debt was finally paid off, that key treaty terms elapsed and renegotiation became possible. Between August 2023 and August 2025, Itaipu transferred $1.097 billion to the Paraguayan state through royalties, energy compensation, and payments to the national utility. [Asunción Times, Sep 2025](https://asunciontimes.com/paraguay-news/economy/itaipu-transfers-usd-1-09-billion-to-power-paraguays-development/) A billion dollars is a great deal of money. It is also the price of a very long patience.

The revenue is significant — it finances infrastructure, the Zero Hunger school feeding program, and rural electrification. But it also exposes Paraguay's dependence on a single asset and a single bilateral relationship. Brazil is Paraguay's dominant trading partner and the gatekeeper for its hydropower export revenue. As Paraguay's domestic energy consumption rises — driven by population growth, climate-driven air conditioning demand, and energy-intensive cryptocurrency mining — the surplus available for export is shrinking. The same river that powers the economy is slowly being asked to power more things than it can.

The Global North's demand for low-carbon energy, channeled through trade agreements and sustainability certifications, is reshaping the rules of the game. The EU Deforestation Regulation, if implemented as planned, will require soy and beef exporters to provide proof of deforestation-free production — a compliance burden that falls disproportionately on smaller producers with limited administrative capacity. The EU-Mercosur trade deal, signed in principle in 2019 but still awaiting ratification, would reduce tariffs on Paraguayan agricultural exports and European industrial goods, but its fate remains uncertain. [Econosur, Apr 2026](https://econosur.org/insights/paraguay/soy-model/)

Paraguay's economic model — high volumes, low institutional friction, minimal state intervention — was designed for a world where provenance did not matter. That world is ending. The consumer now wants to know where things come from, and the answer, for Paraguay, has rarely been comfortable to explain.

## Epilogue: The Threads That Bind

The countries of the Southern Cone are not just connected by geography. They are connected by a pattern: the earth provides something the world needs, capital arrives to extract it, and the local population bears the environmental and social costs while the returns flow outward. Each iteration of this pattern produces the same arguments — job creation vs. displacement, sovereignty vs. exploitation, development vs. preservation — and leaves behind the same residue of unresolved conflict. A connoisseur of resource booms would find the script familiar; only the props have been updated.

![Paraguay's GDP growth has been the most stable in the Southern Cone, averaging ~4% annually, while Argentina's economy careened from -9.9% (2020) to +10.4% (2021) back to -1.6% (2023).](/api/blog/images/southern_cone/southern_cone_gdp_growth.png)

The political backdrop adds another layer. Across the region, the pendulum has swung to the right. Milei in Argentina, Kast in Chile, Peña in Paraguay — all have campaigned on deregulation, fiscal discipline, and pro-business policies that encourage resource extraction. Uruguay, under the newly returned Frente Amplio government of Yamandú Orsi, is the exception, though its policy direction remains pragmatic rather than ideological. [IMF, Oct 2025](https://www.imf.org/-/media/Files/Publications/CR/2025/English/1uryea2025001-source-pdf.ashx) The right promises efficiency; the left promises fairness. The earth, indifferent to both, simply waits to be taken.

The US-China competition for critical minerals adds geopolitical urgency. While Milei has distanced Argentina politically from Beijing, Chinese investment in energy and mining continues. Chile's copper and lithium are essential to both the US-led energy transition and China's battery supply chain. The US, through the IMF and bilateral cooperation, has strengthened its financial influence, but the trade relationships on the ground are more complicated than any single geopolitical narrative suggests. [Finnvera, Mar 2026](https://www.finnvera.fi/eng/finnvera/newsroom/articles/countries-and-markets-12026-latin-american-southern-cone-gains-momentum-as-a-growth-market-for) The two superpowers compete for the same prize, and the prize itself — the salt, the wind, the lithium — has no opinion on the matter.

In Añelo, Fabio Jiménez the mechanic watches the tankers roll past his garage and offers a verdict that could apply to the entire region. "Yes, there will surely be oil and gas for many years to come," he said. "But that does not mean that Argentina will not continue to experience economic and political ups and downs." [LatinAmerican Post, Dec 2025](https://latinamericanpost.com/economy-en/argentinas-fracking-boom-reshapes-patagonia-yet-leaves-its-fragile-future-uncertain/) The mechanic, as it happens, understands the resource curse better than most economists. When your business depends on things breaking, you develop a healthy skepticism about permanence.

His skepticism may be the most accurate gauge of the Southern Cone's resource paradox. The wind will keep blowing. The salt will keep yielding lithium. The grass will keep feeding cattle. The rivers will keep turning turbines. What remains unsettled is whether the people who live with these forces — the mechanics, the sheep farmers, the salt harvesters, the gauchos, the Kolla elders — will ever have a seat at the table where the value is divided. The earth gives; the earth takes. The question is whether anyone in between gets to negotiate the terms.

In the Salinas Grandes, Julia Cañari put it more simply. "Our dream as a community is to keep fighting, defending, and protecting our territories, because this is our life. To care for and provide a good future for our children, for our grandchildren, so that tomorrow they can value what we have done today." [Ojalá, Jul 2025](https://www.ojala.mx/en/ojala-en/life-or-lithium)

The question, in the Southern Cone as everywhere else, is whether the future she imagines is compatible with the future the world is demanding. The world, after all, is also imagining a future — one in which her salt becomes a battery, her water becomes a sacrifice, and her territory becomes a line item on a European carbon offset report. Two futures cannot occupy the same ground. Something will have to give. It usually does.

## A Note on Approach

This story was researched using original source documents, government statistics, institutional reports, and interview-based journalism from the region. The human voices are real — every quote attributed to a named individual was published in a source listed above. The piece does not use AI-generated images, invented scenes, or unattributed claims. Place names and geographic descriptions have been verified against physical and political maps of the region.

The geographic framing — dividing the Southern Cone into the Pampas, Patagonia, the Lithium Triangle, and the Chaco — is analytical rather than absolute. Real economies and ecologies blur at the edges. But the four zones capture the dominant resource relationships that define each subregion's integration into the global economy.
