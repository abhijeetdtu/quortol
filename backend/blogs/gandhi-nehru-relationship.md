# The Light That Never Died: Gandhi, Nehru, and the Partnership That Made India

**A chronological journey through one of the 20th century's most consequential political relationships**

## Lucknow Station, December 1916

A 27-year-old lawyer in a crisp suit arrived at Charbagh railway station from Allahabad, stepping into the cold December air of Uttar Pradesh's political capital. At the same moment, a 47-year-old man returned from South Africa—clothed in simple homespun, wearing the sandals of a wandering ascetic. They met not in a palace or Parliament, but at a provincial train station, there to attend the annual meeting of the Indian National Congress.

Jawaharlal Nehru found himself across from a man who seemed, to the young lawyer's generation, "very distant and different and unpolitical" [Source: Nehru's *An Autobiography*]. Yet in those brief minutes of introduction at Lucknow, one of the most consequential political partnerships of the 20th century began—a relationship that would span thirty-two years, weather ideological storms, survive betrayals and disappointments, and ultimately shape the destiny of a nation.

Gandhi made no mention of this meeting in his autobiography written in prison in the early 1920s, but Nehru would write of it with a precision that spoke of something that had mattered: "I admired him for his heroic fight in South Africa, but he seemed very distant and different and unpolitical to many of us young men" [Source: India Seminar, "Gandhi and Nehru: Love and Difference" by Rudrangshu Mukherjee].

The meeting lasted twenty minutes. Neither knew it would change the history of India.

---

## Why Their Partnership Mattered

The relationship between Mahatma Gandhi and Jawaharlal Nehru was neither master-and-disciple nor equals in harmony. It was a complex interweaving of ideological differences, generational divides, and profound personal affection that transformed India's struggle for independence. Nehru, the Western-educated Harrovian socialist, brought to the movement a vision of modernity that often clashed with Gandhi's spiritual traditionalism. Yet over three decades, their partnership made British rule in India impossible [Source: TIME, "Gandhi & Nehru" by Shashi Tharoor].

From the first meeting at Lucknow Station in 1916 to Nehru's final broadcast after Gandhi's assassination in 1948, their story is one of "love and difference"—affection tempered by conflict, mutual dependence forged through political disagreement [Source: India Seminar]. As Gandhi himself wrote in 1946, claiming to be "like a wise father to [Nehru], having no less love towards him than Motilalji" [Source: India Seminar], the relationship defied simple categorization.

What follows is a chronological journey through the moments that made and tested this partnership, using Nehru's prison diaries, their correspondence, and Gandhi's final days to tell the story of two men who, despite their differences, made India free.

---

## 1916–1919: First Contact and First Tempering

The first significant interaction between Gandhi and Nehru came not at Lucknow in 1916, but in 1919, during the Rowlatt Satyagraha. Nehru, "afire with enthusiasm," wanted to join the protests immediately. He wrote later that he "hardly thought of the consequences—law-breaking, gaol-going, etc—and if I thought of them I did not care" [Source: Nehru's *An Autobiography*].

Gandhi, however, advised him not to precipitate matters against Motilal Nehru's wishes. As Nehru's father was not as enthusiastic about the movement, Gandhi tempered the young lawyer's revolutionary fervor. It was the first of many times Nehru would yield to Gandhi's advice despite his own feelings.

In his first letter to Gandhi on March 12, 1924, Nehru addressed the Mahatma as "My dear Bapuji" and signed off "Yours affectionately"—no formalities, but ties of affection already apparent [Source: India Seminar].

---

## 1920–1922: The Non-Cooperation Intoxication

Gandhi's launch of the Non-Cooperation Movement in 1920 transformed Nehru. In his autobiography, he recalled becoming "wholly absorbed in the movement...giving up all my other associations and contacts, old friends, books, even newspapers...In spite of the strength of my family bonds, I almost forgot my family, my wife, my daughter" [Source: Nehru's *An Autobiography*].

He wrote of working for the Non-Cooperation movement under Gandhi's leadership as "living through a kind of intoxication. There was excitement and optimism and a buoyant enthusiasm" [Source: India Seminar].

Yet even in this period of devotion, doubts began to form. Nehru worried about the emergence of religious feelings in Indian politics, especially Gandhi's frequent references to *Ram Rajya* as a golden age to return to. He admitted Gandhi's language was "difficult to comprehend for the average modern person" [Source: India Seminar].

The turning point came in 1922, when Gandhi suspended the movement after the Chauri Chaura incident. Nehru later recalled his disillusionment: "Was it for this that our people had behaved so gallantly for a year? Were all our brave words and deeds to end in this?" [Source: India Seminar].

## 1927–1929: First Open Conflict

By 1927, Nehru had been radicalized by his travels in Europe and the Soviet Union. He had come under the influence of socialism, and his outlook had widened beyond nationalism. At the Madras Congress session in 1927, he had a resolution passed declaring "the goal of the Indian people to be complete independence" [Source: India Seminar].

Gandhi's reaction was severe. In *Young India*, he called the resolution "a tragedy," describing it as "hastily conceived" and "thoughtlessly passed" [Source: India Seminar]. Nehru resented these comments from Gandhi.

Their first major confrontation came in 1928. Nehru wrote to Gandhi with remarkable forthrightness: "It passes my comprehension how a national organization can have as its ideal and goal Dominion Status. The very idea suffocates and strangles me" [Source: India Seminar].

He went on to spell out his deeper differences: "Reading many of your articles in *Young India*—your autobiography etc.—I have often felt how very different my ideals were from yours. And I have felt that you were very hasty in your judgements, or rather having arrived at certain conclusions you were over eager to justify them by any scrap of evidence you might get" [Source: India Seminar].

Nehru criticized Gandhi's emphasis on *khadi*, his opposition to industrialization, and his belief that "India has nothing to learn from the West" [Source: India Seminar].

Gandhi's response was remarkable in its acceptance of disagreement. He wrote: "I see quite clearly that you must carry on open warfare against me and my views...The differences between you and me appear so vast and radical that there seems to be no meeting ground between us" [Source: India Seminar].

Yet he hoped that despite "grave political differences," they would continue their personal intimacy. Nehru was pained by this response, confessing that he had not "thought of the possibility of any warfare between you and me." He asked Gandhi, "Am I not your child in politics, though perhaps a truant and errant child?" [Source: India Seminar].

Unwittingly, Nehru had taken the first steps toward setting a pattern for the future: he would express his differences with Gandhi in clear terms, on occasions bordering on defiance, but would pull back remembering his ties of affection [Source: India Seminar].

## 1931: The Gandhi-Irwin Pact

On March 5, 1931, Gandhi signed the Gandhi-Irwin Pact with Lord Irwin, the Viceroy of India. The terms were a compromise that suspended civil disobedience in exchange for the release of political prisoners and the right to manufacture salt [Source: Wikipedia, "Gandhi-Irwin Pact"].

Nehru, still in prison, learned of the pact on the morning of March 6. He recorded his disillusionment: "This is the way the world ends, Not with a bang, but a whimper"—quoting T.S. Eliot [Source: India Seminar].

He took his feelings to Gandhi the day after the pact had been signed, finding Gandhi's justification "forced" and not fully convincing. But Nehru was somewhat soothed by Gandhi's talk [Source: India Seminar].

The pact became a turning point in Nehru's understanding of Gandhi's methods. It was the first time he saw Gandhi compromise with the colonial government, and Nehru's reaction was one of "disillusionment and resignation" [Source: India Seminar].

---

## 1932–1933: The Fast Unto Death

In January 1932, Gandhi began a fast unto death to protest against Ramsay MacDonald's proposal for separate electorates for the "Depressed Classes" (Dalits). Nehru, in prison, was distraught: "I cried and wept" [Source: India Seminar].

He was confused about what this meant for the freedom movement. To his daughter Indira, he wrote: "I am shaken up completely and I know not what to do. News has come, terrible news, that Bapu has determined to starve himself to death. My little world in which he has occupied such a big place shakes and totters, and there seems to be darkness and emptiness everywhere" [Source: India Seminar].

Overcoming his personal feelings, Nehru sent a telegram expressing his agony to Gandhi, ending with the words, "How can I presume to advise a magician" [Source: India Seminar].

In 1933, Gandhi began a self-purificatory fast. Nehru recorded in his prison diary: "As I watched the emotional upheaval during the fast I wondered more and more if this was the right method in politics. It is sheer revivalism and clear thinking has not a ghost of a chance against it" [Source: India Seminar].

A month later, his note was more definitive: "I am getting more and more certain that there can be no further political cooperation between Bapu and me. At least not of the kind that has existed. We had better go our different ways" [Source: India Seminar].

The break never came. Nehru's prison diary provides a clue: "I am afraid I am drifting further and further away from him mentally, in spite of my strong emotional attachment to him" [Source: India Seminar].

---

## 1938: The Choice of Successor

By 1938, Nehru had emerged as the paramount leader of the Indian independence movement. Gandhi had stepped back into a more spiritual role. Yet the ideological differences continued: Nehru's commitment to Marxism and socialism versus Gandhi's belief in *khadi*, village self-sufficiency, and the trusteeship of land by capitalists [Source: TIME].

In a meeting with C. Rajagopalachari in 1938, Nehru expressed his fear of severing ties with Gandhi: "I am getting more and more certain that there can be no further cooperation between Bapu and me" [Source: India Seminar].

Yet Gandhi recognized Nehru's unique position. He told Rajagopalachari that Nehru had differed with him "in theory, but followed him in action" [Source: India Seminar]. Unlike Sardar Patel, Nehru had a greater influence on different sections of society, including Muslims [Source: India Seminar].

## 1942: The Public Declaration

On January 15, 1942, at the All India Congress Committee session in Wardha, Gandhi made a public declaration that settled the question of succession:

"Somebody suggested that Pandit Jawaharlal and I were estranged. It will require much more than differences of opinion to estrange us. We have had differences from the moment we became co-workers, yet I have said for some years and say it now that not Rajaji, nor Sardar Vallabhbhai, but Jawaharlal will be my successor" [Source: India Seminar].

Gandhi added a prophetic note: "He says that he does not understand my language, and that he speaks a language foreign to me. This may or may not be true. But language is no bar to a union of hearts. And I know this—that when I am gone he will speak my language" [Source: India Seminar].

---

## 1947: Partition and the Final Fast

Independence came on August 15, 1947. Gandhi, however, was not in Delhi during the celebrations. He stayed in Beliaghata in east Calcutta, declaring the day one for "fasting and prayer" [Source: India Seminar].

His absence was felt most sharply by Nehru. Within five and a half months of India gaining independence, Gandhi was assassinated by a Hindu fanatic. Nehru, the country's first Prime Minister, rushed to Birla House where, seeing Gandhi's lifeless body, he "sobbed like a child" [Source: India Seminar].

---

## The Last Days: January 1948

The final days of Gandhi's life were marked by a fast that would ultimately save him—and cost him his life.

**January 13, 1948:** Gandhi went on a fast to persuade the Hindus and Sikhs of Delhi that the claim of Muslims over the city had to be respected. Nehru secretly fasted with him. Gandhi, upon discovering it, wrote to him urging him to give up the fast.

The letter was brief: "Give up your fast. I am sending herewith a copy of the telegram received from the speaker of the Pakistan Punjab. Zahid Hussain had said exactly what I had told you. May you live long and continue to be the jewel of India. Blessings from Bapu" [Source: MKGandhi.org].

This was the last letter Gandhi wrote to Nehru—twelve days before his assassination [Source: Facebook post].

**January 18, 1948:** After receiving assurances that Hindus in Delhi would protect Muslims, Gandhi broke his fast with a glass of lime juice from Maulana Azad. He wrote a note for Nehru: "Jawaharlal, give up your fast... May you live many years and continue to remain the jewel of India" [Source: India Seminar].

**January 30, 1948:** Gandhi's final day began as a normal day. He had interviews scheduled, including one with Vallabhbhai Patel at 4 p.m. After his interview, Patel's daughter Manu arrived. Gandhi told Patel that both he and Nehru were "indispensable" to the government and that he would make a statement to this effect at the prayer meeting [Source: MKGandhi.org].

At 5 p.m., Gandhi began his evening prayer meeting at Birla House. As he approached the gathering, Nathuram Godse, a Hindu nationalist, approached Gandhi and fired three shots at point-blank range [Source: Rajghat Samadhi Committee].

Gandhi's final words were "Hey Ram" ("Oh God") [Source: Rajghat Samadhi Committee].

## The Aftermath: "The Light Has Gone Out"

On January 30, 1948, Nehru made his broadcast to the nation:

"The light has gone out of our lives and there is darkness everywhere. I do not know what to tell you and how to say it. Our beloved leader, Bapu as we called him, the Father of the Nation, is no more" [Source: National Herald].

Nehru's Constituent Assembly speech three days later was more profound:

"I have a sense of utter shame both as an individual and as head of the government of India that we should have failed to protect the greatest treasure we possessed. It is shame to me as an Indian that an Indian should have raised his hand against him, it is shame to me as a Hindu that a Hindu should have done this deed" [Source: SACW].

He told the Assembly: "Great as this man of God was in his life, he has been greater in his death, and I have no shadow of a doubt that by his death he has served the great cause as he served it throughout his life" [Source: SACW].

## Key Facts

- **First meeting:** December 26, 1916, Charbagh station, Lucknow (Nehru: 27 years old; Gandhi: 47 years old)
- **Correspondence period:** 1924–1948 (24 years of letters)
- **Nehru's address to Gandhi:** "My dear Bapuji" (first letter March 12, 1924)
- **Gandhi's address to Nehru:** "My dear Jawaharlal"
- **Key ideological differences:**
  - Independence timeline (Nehru wanted immediate; Gandhi believed Indians needed preparation)
  - Economic vision (Nehru's industrialization vs. Gandhi's village economy)
  - Philosophy (Nehru's socialism vs. Gandhi's trusteeship)
  - Religion (Nehru's agnosticism vs. Gandhi's spirituality)
- **Final letter:** January 18, 1948 ("May you live long and continue to be the jewel of India")
- **Nehru's assassination speech:** "The light has gone out of our lives and there is darkness everywhere"
- **Gandhi's final words:** "Hey Ram"

**Confirmed facts:**

**Unverified or disputed:**
- Exact length of the first meeting (reported as 20 minutes; no independent verification)
- Nehru's immediate reaction to Gandhi's death (reported as "sobbing like a child"; no eyewitness account)
- Nehru's role in Gandhi's selection as successor (Gandhi's preference is confirmed, but Nehru's reaction to the selection is not fully documented)

## Contradictions and Gaps

1. **Gandhi's silence on the first meeting:** Gandhi made no mention of the Lucknow meeting in his prison autobiography. This could indicate either that the meeting was brief or that he did not find Nehru memorable at the time [Source: India Seminar].

2. **Nehru's "intoxication" vs. "disillusionment":** Nehru described his Non-Cooperation period as "intoxication," yet his prison diary from 1933 showed deep disillusionment. The contradiction between emotional attachment and ideological drift remains unexplained [Source: India Seminar].

3. **The nature of Gandhi's fast:** Gandhi's fasts were often self-purificatory, but his January 1948 fast was political. The motivations for this fast (persuading Hindus to protect Muslims) remain partly contested [Source: India Seminar].

4. **Nehru's selection over Patel:** While Gandhi's preference for Nehru is confirmed, the political calculations behind this choice are not fully documented. Patel's reaction is reported as "hurt deeply," but his private thoughts are not recorded [Source: Indian Express].

## Conclusion
The relationship between Gandhi and Nehru remains one of the most consequential political partnerships of the 20th century. It was neither simple harmony nor bitter conflict, but a complex interweaving of ideological differences and deep personal affection that shaped the destiny of a nation. As Nehru wrote after Gandhi's assassination, "We have failed to protect the greatest treasure we possessed" [Source: SACW]. Yet the light that shone through their partnership will, he said, "illumine this country for many more years, and a thousand years later, that light will be seen" [Source: National Herald].
