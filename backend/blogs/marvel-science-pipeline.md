# The Laboratory of the Imagination: How Marvel's Fictional Science Became Real Research

**By Staff Journalist** | May 2026

Patent examiners are paid to take invention seriously. They are not, as a rule, paid to read comic books. But on March 13, 2019, the UK Intellectual Property Office granted patent GB2559971 to a British company called Gravity Industries Limited for a wearable flight system with propulsion assemblies worn on a user's body — a jet suit — and the line between those two activities blurred. During examination, the patent officer did something unusual: they searched the prior art and found a relevant disclosure — the suit worn by the fictional character Iron Man in Marvel Comics, which includes propulsion units on the hands. The office cited it. [Venner Shipley, Apr 2019]

It was not the first time a comic book had entered the official record of human invention. A *Beano* strip had been cited against a pet-entry signal system. A Donald Duck comic was raised against a method for raising sunken vessels. But the Iron Man citation was different. It was not a novelty. It was an acknowledgment that a fictional engineer from a 1963 comic book had, decades before any real prototype, described a technological system that a government patent office considered relevant to a real invention.

The Gravity Industries patent was ultimately granted because the real suit added a torso propulsion assembly that Iron Man's didn't have. But the question the examination raised — at what point does science fiction become prior art? — is the wrong question. The right one is: what has Marvel already helped build?

## I. The Patent Evidence

The patent record is the most concrete measure of Marvel's influence on engineering, if not the most abundant. It is sparse, but it is real.

In 1991, inventor Stephen E. Kimble filed US Patent No. 5,072,856 for a "toy web-shooting glove" — a device that expels string foam from the palm to simulate a spider's web. The patent explicitly states its purpose: "to create a toy that makes it possible for a player to act like a spider person by shooting webs from the palm of his or her hand." [Google Patents, US5072856A] Marvel eventually licensed the invention, and when the patent expired, a dispute over royalty payments reached the Supreme Court in *Kimble v. Marvel Entertainment* (2015). The case, which turned on whether patent royalties could extend beyond the patent term, produced a 6–3 opinion by Justice Alito that acknowledged the strange intersection: a real patent inspired by a fictional character, now part of American case law. [Patent Docs, Aug 2015]

In 2011, an inventor filed US patent application 20130145530A1 for an "Iron man suit" — a full body exoskeleton "constructed from a strong material, such as steel... capable of protecting its wearer from projectiles, such as bullets, and including a hydraulic mechanism and a power source." The application describes sensors to detect body movements, a computer to process them, and actuators to lift heavy objects. [Google Patents, US20130145530A1] The patent, filed nearly 50 years after Iron Man's first appearance in *Tales of Suspense* #39, was effectively a real-world attempt to build what Tony Stark had been wearing since 1963.

And in 2017, Disney filed a patent for a virtual reality system that simulates throwing and catching Captain America's shield. The design shows a motion sensor fastened to the forearm, a physical shield on a short rail, and sensors that simulate the "boomerang effect" from the comics. [Chip and Company, Jun 2017]

None of this amounts to a mass phenomenon. But as a category of evidence, it is significant: the patent system — that most serious of institutions, designed to separate genuine invention from mere aspiration — has repeatedly treated Marvel's fictional technology as a benchmark for reality.

## II. The University Pipeline

The more revealing story lives not in the patent office but in the classrooms and laboratories where Marvel's fictional science has been treated not as entertainment but as a pedagogical starting point — and, in some cases, as a research question worth answering.

In 2001, James Kakalios, a physics professor at the University of Minnesota, created a freshman seminar called "Everything I Know About Science I Learned from Reading Comic Books." It was a real physics class. It covered Newtonian mechanics, quantum theory, and solid-state physics. The only thing unusual was the textbook: Silver Age comic books. "This is a real physics class," Kakalios has said, "that covers topics from Isaac Newton to the transistor, but there's not an inclined plane or pulley in sight. Rather, ALL the examples come from superhero comic books." [physicsofsuperheroes.com]

The class drew media attention when the first *Spider-Man* film was released in 2002. Kakalios turned it into a book, *The Physics of Superheroes*, which *Discover* magazine named one of the best science books of 2005. It has since been translated into six languages. Kakalios went on to serve as a science consultant for Warner Bros.' *Watchmen* (2007); his short video on the science of that film won an Upper Midwest Regional Emmy and a WEBBY nomination. In 2014, the American Association for the Advancement of Science gave him its Public Engagement with Science Award. In 2016, the American Institute of Physics awarded him the Andrew Gemant Award for his work in communicating physics to the public. [Georgia Tech School of Physics, 2017]

Kakalios's approach — using superhero narratives to explain real physics — has since spread across American higher education. At UC Davis, chemical engineering professor Ricardo Castro created "EMS 2: Materials Marvels: The Science of Superheroes" in 2015, a freshman engineering course that asks students to analyze whether Captain America's vibranium alloy shield could actually absorb kinetic energy, and whether Iron Man's repulsor beam could deliver concussive force. The class began as a pilot with 78 students; by its second year, enrollment had nearly doubled to 150. [University of California, Jan 2016]

At Lawrence Technological University in Michigan, Dr. Sibrina Collins — executive director of the Marburger STEM Center — published a peer-reviewed chapter titled "Inorganic Chemistry: Vibranium and Marvel Studios' Black Panther" in the American Chemical Society's *Advances in Teaching Inorganic Chemistry* series. The chapter uses a single question — "Where would vibranium be located on the periodic table if it were real?" — to draw students into the logic of electron configuration, metallic bonding, and the properties of real elements like titanium, which approaches vibranium's fictional strength-to-weight ratio. [TechCentury, Nov 2020]

"Superhero science provides an opportunity to make key connections to real science content," Collins wrote. The chapter connects *Black Panther* to Mary Shelley's *Frankenstein* — generally regarded as the first science fiction novel — and to a documentary on women of color in science, creating a pedagogical bridge between pop culture, the history of science, and equity in STEM classrooms.

## III. The Research Programs

If the classroom is where Marvel's science is taught, the laboratory is where it is tested.

Suveen Mathaudhu, a materials scientist who has held appointments at UC Riverside, UC Davis, and the University of California system, has been open about his inspiration. "People don't often realize how many superheroes gain their powers through science," Mathaudhu told UC's news service in 2017. "Bruce Banner — a scientist. Peter Parker — a chemist who designed his own webs. Iron Man — an engineer who builds his own suits." [University of California, Nov 2017]

Mathaudhu's own research has focused on making lightweight metals stronger by refining grain structures to the nanoscale — below 100 nanometers, where "the physics change." The practical application has been automotive: lighter vehicles mean better fuel efficiency. The Ford F-150, America's best-selling pickup truck, shifted from steel to aluminum body construction, reducing weight by 700 pounds and increasing fuel economy — a change made possible in part by the kind of nanostructured metallurgy Mathaudhu studies. The inspiration, he says, came from a conversation about the weight of Thor's hammer and the composition of Captain America's shield.

"When you take the grain size below one hundred nanometers, the physics change," Mathaudhu explained. "The materials get very, very, very strong, and sometimes also have high formability." His group has also shown how nanostructured aluminum can maintain electrical conductivity while boosting mechanical strength — a finding that could allow cheaper, lighter aluminum cabling to replace heavy copper in vehicles.

In 2016, researchers at NC State University and the U.S. Army Engineer Research and Development Center announced a composite metal foam armor that stops bullets. The material uses a ceramic strike face, a Kevlar backplate, and a metal-foam interlayer that absorbs kinetic energy on impact. In tests, the armor produced an indentation depth of 8 millimeters — nearly six times better than the 44-millimeter maximum allowed by the National Institute of Justice standard. Multiple news outlets and the researchers themselves described it as "Captain America-style armor." [IPWatchdog, May 2016]

At MIT and Rice University, a separate team investigated block copolymers that self-assemble into alternating layers of glassy and rubbery polymers. When struck by a high-velocity projectile, the material melts into a homogeneous liquid that traps the bullet and absorbs its energy. Again, Captain America's shield was the reference point — a fictional object that absorbs kinetic energy without transmitting it to the wearer.

The Penn GRASP Lab (General Robotics, Automation, Sensing and Perception) at the University of Pennsylvania has produced a systematic analysis of Iron Man's suit, evaluating which components are feasible with current technology and which remain science fiction. Researchers Marc Miskin (who builds cell-sized robots) and James Pikul (who works on high-power-density batteries) concluded that a bulletproof exoskeleton is realistic, but sustained flight remains beyond current energy-density limits. "Iron Man was this awesome kind of role model, in terms of superhero characters, for this exact reason," Pikul noted. "He didn't have super powers, but he was really smart and clever, and he used his smarts in the right way." [GRASP Lab, Penn Today]

## IV. The Space Agency Partnership

If there is a single institutional relationship that captures the strange marriage of fiction and science, it is the one between Marvel and NASA.

In 2016, the Center for the Advancement of Science in Space (CASIS), which manages the ISS National Laboratory, partnered with Marvel to create a mission patch featuring Rocket Raccoon and Groot from *Guardians of the Galaxy*. The patch represented all ISS National Lab investigations conducted that year. It flew to the ISS on SpaceX's 10th Dragon cargo mission. [Space.com, May 2017]

Ken Shields, CASIS's director of operations and educational opportunities, explained the logic with admirable candor: "There are very few brands in the world who have as large an impact as Marvel."

In 2018, the partnership escalated. CASIS and Marvel launched the *Guardians of the Galaxy Space Station Challenge*, inviting American students aged 13 to 18 to propose microgravity experiments for the ISS. The contest was divided into two tracks: Team Rocket (materials science and engineering) and Team Groot (biology and regenerative science). Two winning proposals were built into actual ISS investigations, launching later that year. Winners worked with DreamUp, NanoRacks, and Space Tango to develop flight hardware — an experience that exposed teenagers to the full engineering pipeline of space station research. [ISS National Lab, Jan & May 2018]

In 2023, Microsoft joined the partnership. To promote *Guardians of the Galaxy Vol. 3*, Microsoft worked with Redwire Space to 3D print a Zune — the obsolete Microsoft music player that Star-Lord uses in the film — aboard the ISS Additive Manufacturing Facility. The campaign included a teacher's guide with lessons on in-space manufacturing and the "Azure Space: Diversity in STEM" learning challenge. "Space is critical to inspiring future generations," said Redwire chief growth officer Mike Gold. [Florida Today/STimes, May 2023]

And in 2014, before any of this, NASA's Jet Propulsion Laboratory provided scientific content for "Marvel's Avengers S.T.A.T.I.O.N." — an immersive educational exhibit at Discovery Times Square in New York. NASA supplied material on the electromagnetic spectrum, black holes as energy sources, aerospace materials for Iron Man's armor, and the *Eyes on Exoplanets* interactive display to contextualize Thor's cosmic setting. At the opening, NASA Associate Administrator John Grunsfeld spoke about the partnership's goal: to use the "superhero universe as a gateway to real science." [NASA JPL, Jun 2014]

## V. What the Data Says

The relationship between Marvel and real science is not merely anecdotal. It is increasingly measurable, and the data reveals both the scale and the complexity of the influence.

A 2002 paper by Alberich, Miro-Julia, and Rosselló — "Marvel Universe looks almost like a real social network" — analyzed the full character interaction network of Marvel Comics, finding 6,486 characters connected by 168,267 links. The study, published on arXiv and cited in dozens of subsequent network analysis papers, demonstrated that the Marvel Universe's structure is not random: it has most characteristics of real human collaboration networks, though with lower clustering (0.012 vs. typical real-world values). [arXiv:cond-mat/0202174]

In 2017, a team led by James Carney published an even larger analysis in the *Journal of Cognition and Culture*: 19,877 characters scraped from 72,611 comics. The study found that superhero narratives reliably embody four cognitive patterns — punitive prosociality, quasi-omniscience, kin-signaling, and minimal counterintuitiveness — confirming that "cognitive constraints exercise a selective effect on the transmission of popular culture." [Journal of Cognition and Culture, DOI: 10.1163/15685373-12340009]

The most targeted academic work on Marvel and science comes from Alessandro Muscio of the University of Foggia, who has published two major studies. In 2023, he analyzed 1,632 Marvel characters in *Technological Forecasting and Social Change*, finding that while science and technology are central to nearly every character's origin, the overall portrayal is ambiguous — science creates as many problems as it solves. [Elsevier, Tech Forecasting & Social Change, vol. 186, 2023]

His 2025 follow-up, co-authored with Matteo Farinella and published in *Technology in Society*, examined 313 scientist characters across DC and Marvel using statistical econometric methods. The finding: comic books, despite their potential as vehicles for science communication, tend to perpetuate the "mad scientist" stereotype. Scientists in superhero narratives are more likely than other characters to be morally ambiguous, emotionally unstable, or outright villainous. [Elsevier, Technology in Society, vol. 82, 2025, DOI: 10.1016/j.techsoc.2025.102895]

This is the shadow side of the Science Pipeline. For every engineering student inspired by Tony Stark, there may be a reader absorbing the implication that scientific genius is dangerous. The Muscio and Farinella study is a reminder that influence is not always positive.

## VI. The Classroom Data

At scale, the educational impact is measurable in enrollment numbers and market growth.

Kakalios's course has run continuously since 2001. Castro's "Materials Marvels" at UC Davis grew from 78 to 150 students in its second year. Collins's vibranium curriculum is used in K-12 and college classrooms across Michigan.

The broader comics and science-fiction reading audience — the pool from which future scientists are drawn — has been studied. A 2018 survey by Menadue and Jacups, published in *SAGE Open*, found that science fiction readers report more positive attitudes toward science than the general population, and that this correlation strengthens with deeper engagement in the genre. [SAGE Open, Jun 2018]

And the readership itself is growing and diversifying. In 2025, the North American comics market reached $2.2 billion — a new record. The direct market (comic shops) grew nearly 30% year-over-year, driven in significant part by Marvel's Ultimate Universe relaunch and the return of the X-Men franchise. ICv2's White Paper reported in October 2025 that "Gen Z has arrived in comic shops in record numbers." [ICv2, Oct 2025; Feb 2026]

Marvel's share of the direct market in Q1 2025 was 37.9% — nearly 10 points higher than the year before. [ICv2, Apr 2025] The top-selling comic of 2024 by both units and dollars was *Ultimate Spider-Man #1*. [ICv2, Jan 2025]

These are not enrollment figures in physics courses. But they are data points in the pipeline: young people, in record numbers, voluntarily engaging with narratives built on scientific premises.

## VII. The Prior Art of the Future

The UK patent officer who cited Iron Man as prior art did not, in the end, reject the Gravity Industries jet suit. The real invention added a torso propulsion unit that the fictional one lacked. But the episode reveals something significant: fiction functions as a kind of prior art for the imagination itself. It draws the boundaries of what we consider possible.

In that sense, Marvel has done something no other media franchise has achieved. Its fictional technology has been cited in real patents. Its characters have been used by NASA to design educational challenges that sent student experiments to the International Space Station. Its narrative premises — gamma radiation, vibranium, nanotech armor, quantum entanglement — have been adopted as teaching tools in university physics, materials science, and chemistry curricula across the United States. Its social network structure has been the subject of peer-reviewed network analysis. Its portrayal of scientists is significant enough to warrant academic study in technology policy journals.

The $32.5 billion at the global box office is the headline. [The-Numbers.com; Box Office Mojo] The 6,486-character network analyzed on arXiv, the 150 engineering students at UC Davis learning materials science through Thor's hammer, the two student experiments that flew to the ISS because of a comic book challenge, the patent examiner who searched the fictional prior art — those are the footnotes. And they may turn out to matter more.

When James Kakalios was asked why he thought a physics class based on comic books worked, he gave an answer that could serve as an epitaph for the whole enterprise. "Students never wonder when they will use this information in real life," he said. "They're too busy wondering if Spider-Man could really stop a falling elevator." [Penguin Random House]

The question, in both cases, is the same. The answer, increasingly, is that someone is trying to find out.
