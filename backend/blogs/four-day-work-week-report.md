# The Four-Day Work Week: A Quiet Revolution in How We Live and Work

*Published: May 5, 2026*

---

## The Lede

In Reykjavík, Iceland, in 2015, Arna Hrönn Aradóttir, a public-health project manager with five children, volunteered for an experiment that seemed almost radical by contemporary standards: her employer offered to cut her work week from 40 hours to 36, without reducing her pay. At the time, the concept was ridiculed by some economists and business leaders as utopian fantasy. Today, more than a decade later, 90% of Iceland's workers either work reduced hours or have gained the contractual right to request them. The experiment that began as a small trial with 2,500 workers has quietly reshaped a national labor market, providing evidence that challenges nearly a century of economic orthodoxy about working time.

## The Story So Far

The concept of a shorter work week isn't new. In 1930, economist John Maynard Keynes famously predicted that by now we would all be working around 15-hour weeks. Richard Nixon, Vice President in 1956, promised Americans a four-day workweek would come "in the not too distant future." Yet the 40-hour, 5-day week remained entrenched for over 90 years.

The modern movement began in earnest in 2015 when Iceland launched two large-scale trials involving 2,500 workers. Productivity remained the same or improved across most workplaces, while worker wellbeing "dramatically" increased. Following the trials, Icelandic unions negotiated shorter hours, and by 2021, 86% of Iceland's workforce had gained the right to reduced hours.

What makes this different from previous attempts? The emergence of the **100-80-100 model**: 100% pay for 80% of working hours while maintaining 100% output. Developed by Andrew Barnes and Charlotte Lockhart at New Zealand's Perpetual Guardian, this framework addresses the core skepticism about maintaining productivity. The trial resulted in a 20% increase in reported productivity, and Prime Minister Jacinda Ardern encouraged other businesses to try it.

## The Global Wave

Beginning in 2022, 4 Day Week Global launched coordinated global trials. By 2025, over 300 organizations had completed trials with more than 10,000 employees. The largest single trial, published in *Nature Human Behaviour* in July 2025, involved 2,896 employees across 141 organizations in six countries over six months.

The results have been consistent:

| **Outcome** | **Value** |
|-------------|-----------|
| Companies continuing after UK trial (2023) | 92% (56 of 61) |
| UK trial revenue increase vs. previous year | 35% average |
| UK trial attrition rate decrease | 57% |
| UK trial employee burnout reduction | 71% |
| Global trial organizations (Nature study) | 141 |
| Global trial employees (Nature study) | 2,896 |
| Iceland workers with shorter hours right | 86% of workforce |
| Iceland trial participants | 2,500 workers |

In the UK's July 2025 pilot of 17 companies and nearly 1,000 workers, **100% of companies permanently adopted the model**. Scotland's year-long public sector trial showed mental health improving by 18.4%, with staff work-life satisfaction rising from 4% to 84%.

## Why Now?

Several factors converged to create this moment:

1. **The pandemic catalyst** - Remote work and the "Great Resignation" gave employees greater leverage. Burnout rates spiked globally, making flexibility a competitive advantage for employers.

2. **Research-backed confidence** - Trials now span six countries and 300+ organizations. The *Nature Human Behaviour* study's rigorous design strengthens claims significantly.

3. **Government interest** - Spain announced a three-year pilot with employer subsidies; the UAE instituted a four-day week for government employees; Belgium gave employees the right to request shorter hours; Tokyo announced city staff would move to four-day weeks in late 2024.

4. **Economic growth proof** - Iceland's economy expanded by 5% in 2023, second only to Malta among wealthy EU economies according to the IMF. Labor productivity increased 1.5% annually over the last five years—the highest in Nordic countries.

## The Science

The *Nature Human Behaviour* study (Fan, Schor, Kelly & Gu, 2025) found:

- **Improvements in burnout, job satisfaction, mental health, and physical health** compared to 12 control companies
- **Three key factors mediate the relationship**: improved self-reported work ability, reduced sleep problems, and decreased fatigue
- **Individual-level reductions** in hours are correlated with greater improvements in wellbeing
- **Benefits persisted** at 12-month follow-up and, if anything, increased

## What It's Really About

While revenue and productivity are important, they're secondary to the fundamental purpose: **worker wellbeing**. Burnout, mental health, work-life balance, and family time are the real wins. Workers report having better work-life balance, more time for "life administration" tasks, and more time to invest in hobbies, exercise, and wellness.

In the UK 2025 pilot, 62% reported reduced burnout, 41% noticed improvements to their mental health, and 45% expressed greater satisfaction with their quality of life. Only 3% would want to go back to working their old hours.

## The Challenges

Not every role or industry can adopt this model. The evidence is limited for:

- **Blue-collar and manual workers** who have little downtime and limited capacity to increase productivity
- **Front-line roles** in retail, schools, hospitals, nursing homes where additional workers may be needed to cover dropped hours
- **Industries requiring 24/7 coverage** and continuous operations
- **Long-term sustainability** beyond 24 months remains to be seen

Some managers reported initial strain when organizing training or events became more complex. A 2024 systematic review identified scheduling problems, more intense performance monitoring, and risk of benefits fading over time as potential negative impacts.

## The Future

As of early 2026, Spain's negotiations on reducing the work week to 37.5 hours caused "a rift in the governing coalition," with the fate unknown. Australia's Greens party has proposed a National Institute for the Four Day Work Week and test cases through the Fair Work Commission. Belgium allows any employee to ask to work their allotted 38 hours across just four days.

## What This Means for You

If you're considering implementing a four-day week:

1. **Don't compress** - The 100-80-100 model means 32 hours (4x8), not 40 hours in 4 days (4x10)
2. **Redesign work** - Radically shorten meetings, use technology thoughtfully, build focused work periods
3. **Track deep work** - Use tools to verify actual output stays at 100% while reducing shallow tasks
4. **Start with a pilot** - 2 months of preparation, then 6-month trial with research support
5. **Maintain output** - The third "100" is the contract. If your output dips, the arrangement fails

## The Bottom Line

The four-day work week is transitioning from fringe innovation to mainstream consideration. It's not about working less. It's about working differently—eliminating the 2-3 hours of wasted time that most knowledge workers lose daily to useless meetings, poor technology, and distractions.

As Joe Ryle, campaign director of the 4 Day Week Foundation, said: "People are happier, businesses are thriving, and there's no turning back. We've proved it again and again: a four-day week works and should now be implemented more widely across the economy."
