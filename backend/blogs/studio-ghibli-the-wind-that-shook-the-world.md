# The Wind That Shook the World

## How Studio Ghibli remade animation, conquered culture, and now faces its own ending.

---

**By our correspondent**

On a warm afternoon in November 2022, a crowd gathered at the gates of a theme park in Nagakute, a suburb of Nagoya, to watch Goro Miyazaki cut a ribbon. His father, Hayao, then 81, was not there — a man who announces his retirement every few years does not attend ribbon-cuttings. The younger Miyazaki, a director in his own right, stood beside the governor of Aichi Prefecture and the producer Toshio Suzuki, and welcomed the first visitors to Ghibli Park — a 7.1-hectare recreation of the studio's animated worlds, built inside the grounds of the 2005 World Expo.

There were no roller coasters. No branded thrill rides. Visitors walked through the **Hill of Youth**, a painstakingly built replica of the antique shop from *Whisper of the Heart*; they climbed a stairway inside **Ghibli's Grand Warehouse**, past a life-size Catbus; they wandered into **Dondoko Forest**, where the weathered farmhouse from *My Neighbor Totoro* stood exactly as it appeared on screen, down to the soot sprites scattered in the shadows. The park operates on a reservation system so restrictive that tickets sell out within hours of release. It is, in every respect, a Studio Ghibli experience: uncommercial to the point of perversity, breathtakingly beautiful, and not easy to get into. The sort of place that demands devotion before it offers delight.

Studio Ghibli turned 40 in June 2025. By any measure, it has never been more powerful. Its films have grossed hundreds of millions of dollars worldwide. Two of them have won Academy Awards — *Spirited Away* in 2003 and *The Boy and the Heron* in 2024 — making Ghibli the only non-English language animation studio to claim the Oscar twice. [IndieWire](https://indiewire.com/awards/results/the-boy-and-the-heron-wins-best-animated-feature-oscar-1234960416) Its 22 films are streamed on Netflix across most of the planet, generating 39.1 million views in the second half of 2025 alone. [CBR](https://www.cbr.com/netflix-data-report-anime-warning/) Its characters appear on T-shirts in Mumbai, totes in London, phone cases in Sao Paulo. The studio's aesthetic — soft watercolor backgrounds, impossibly lush greenery, round-faced children with eyes full of wonder — has become a visual shorthand for nostalgia itself, so recognizable that OpenAI built a tool to replicate it, sparking a global controversy in March 2025. You know you have arrived when the machines come for your style.

But Ghibli's power has always been built on a paradox. It is a global phenomenon run by people who never wanted one. It is the most beloved animation studio in the world, and it is, in all likelihood, approaching its end. The two facts are not unrelated.

To understand what Ghibli was, what it became, and what it leaves behind, you have to start at the beginning — when two stubborn, brilliant men who had been making animation for decades decided to blow a new wind through the industry.

## I. The Founders

Studio Ghibli was not born in a boardroom. It was born from a magazine article — which is to say, from an editor who trusted his instincts and a publisher willing to indulge them.

In 1981, **Toshio Suzuki**, an editor at the anime magazine *Animage*, approached **Hayao Miyazaki** — then a relatively known but not yet famous animator — and proposed a feature story. Miyazaki, who had directed *The Castle of Cagliostro* two years earlier, was working on a manga he had been serializing in *Animage* called *Nausicaa of the Valley of the Wind*. Suzuki saw something in it. He pushed for a full adaptation. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Studio_Ghibli)

The 1984 film *Nausicaa of the Valley of the Wind* was a success. And Suzuki, who had brought together Miyazaki and his longtime collaborator **Isao Takahata** to produce it, saw an opportunity. He convinced the head of Tokuma Shoten to fund a new animation studio. On June 15, 1985, Studio Ghibli was founded.

The name came from Miyazaki. "Ghibli" is the Arabic word for the hot sirocco wind that blows across the Sahara. The Caproni Ca.309, an Italian reconnaissance plane used during World War II, was also nicknamed "Ghibli." (Miyazaki, who loved airplanes and Italy, got to honor both.) The intention was explicit: to blow a whirlwind through a stagnating Japanese animation industry. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Studio_Ghibli)

In a speech at the Annecy International Animation Film Festival in 1995, Suzuki put it plainly: "The idea was to dedicate full energy into each piece of work with sufficient budget and time, never compromising on the quality or content." [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Studio_Ghibli)

This was the founding principle, and it has never wavered. It also meant that every film was an existential bet. A studio whose motto is "never compromise" is a studio that lives perpetually on the edge of ruin.

The three founders could not have been more different. **Hayao Miyazaki** (born 1941) was the visionary: a man who drew his own storyboards, obsessed over every frame, and created fantasies that felt more real than reality. **Isao Takahata** (1935–2018) was the realist — a director who did not draw but whose films (*Grave of the Fireflies*, *Only Yesterday*, *The Tale of the Princess Kaguya*) pursued emotional truth with surgical precision. **Toshio Suzuki** (born 1948) was the producer, the strategist who kept the whole thing running, who once described his role as "making sure Miyazaki can make his films." [Dig Tokyo](https://digtokyo.jp/en/ct/040/)

Miyazaki himself acknowledged this dependency: "If it were not for Mr. Suzuki, there wouldn't have been Studio Ghibli." [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toshio_Suzuki)

The first official Ghibli release, *Castle in the Sky* (1986), was a swashbuckling adventure. Then came 1988, the year that defined the studio's range: Miyazaki's *My Neighbor Totoro*, a gentle, almost plotless story about two sisters and a forest spirit; and Takahata's *Grave of the Fireflies*, a devastating war drama about a brother and sister starving in the aftermath of the firebombing of Kobe. The two films were released as a double feature. The contrast was staggering — and deliberate. Ghibli would not be confined to a single tone or audience. A studio that could make you weep and wonder in the same afternoon was a studio that understood something essential about humanity.

Over the following decade, the studio built its reputation: *Kiki's Delivery Service* (1989), *Porco Rosso* (1992), Takahata's *Pom Poko* (1994), and *Whisper of the Heart* (1995). The films were successful in Japan, but not yet global phenomena. That changed in 1997.

## II. The Breakthrough

*Princess Mononoke* (1997) was a turning point. It was Miyazaki's most ambitious film — a medieval epic about the conflict between an industrial mining town and the gods of the forest. It was violent, morally complex, and featured no clear villain. (A film without a villain: how inconvenient for those who prefer their morality simple, and how liberating for everyone else.) In Japan, it became the highest-grossing film ever released at the time, earning ¥20.2 billion. [Nippon.com](https://www.nippon.com/en/japan-data/h01730/) Its U.S. release, distributed by Miramax with a script consultation by Neil Gaiman, introduced Ghibli to a wide American audience.

But it was *Spirited Away* (2001) that turned the studio into a legend. The story of a ten-year-old girl who must work in a bathhouse for spirits to save her transformed parents became a cultural event. It overtook *Titanic* as Japan's highest-grossing film of all time, a record it held for 20 years until *Demon Slayer: Mugen Train* surpassed it in 2021. It grossed more than $360 million worldwide across multiple re-releases. [Box Office Mojo](https://www.boxofficemojo.com/title/tt0245429/) And in 2003, it became the first — and until 2024, the only — non-English language animated film to win the Academy Award for Best Animated Feature. The industry had finally noticed that animation could be art; Ghibli had always known.

The Oscar was more than an award. It was a signal to the world that animation could be art — as though the world had not been receiving this signal from Ghibli for years. John Lasseter, then the chief creative officer of Pixar, had been a friend and admirer of Miyazaki's since 1981. He helped bring *Spirited Away* to American audiences. "Whenever we get stuck at Pixar or Disney, I put on a Miyazaki film sequence or two, just to get us inspired again," he told the Tokyo International Film Festival in 2014. [The Hollywood Reporter](https://www.hollywoodreporter.com/movies/movie-news/spirited-away-film-oscars-records-history-1235052088/)

Lasseter's relationship with Ghibli runs deep. He has described *The Castle of Cagliostro* as "the first animated film that I thought was made to entertain all ages." The influence runs both ways: *Toy Story*, *Up*, *Luca*, and countless Pixar films bear the imprint of Miyazaki's quiet moments, his celebration of stillness, his insistence that children's entertainment need not be frantic. Lasseter once said: "He takes time and celebrates the quiet moments in films and that is so the opposite of Hollywood movies." [Screendaily](https://www.screendaily.com/news/john-lasseter-talks-about-japanese-inspirations/5079059.article)

The debt extends beyond Pixar. Western directors from Wes Anderson to Guillermo del Toro have cited Miyazaki as an influence. Video games like *The Legend of Zelda: Breath of the Wild* and *Ni no Kuni* (the latter co-produced with Ghibli animators) have directly translated Ghibli's visual language into interactive media. The corrupted malice-blob in *Breath of the Wild* is unmistakably the demonic goo of *Princess Mononoke*. The game's green hills, ruined temples, and gentle exploration ethos are Ghibli rendered in polygonal form. [Zelda Dungeon](https://www.zeldadungeon.net/legendary-pictures-princess-mononokes-influence-on-breath-of-the-wild/)

## III. The Ghibli Difference

What makes a Ghibli film instantly recognizable? It is not merely the hand-drawn animation, though that is increasingly rare in an industry that has embraced CGI. It is the *weight* of things. Food that steams. Water that refracts light. Grass that bends underfoot. Miyazaki is famous for sending his animators to observe the real world — a visit to a vet to learn how dogs take medicine, which they translated into a dragon's movements in *Spirited Away*. [The Hindu](https://www.thehindu.com/entertainment/movies/studio-ghibli-40-years-hayao-miyazaki-handdrawn-animation-ai-render/article69747633.ece) In an industry that has decided reality is optional, Ghibli insists on getting it exactly right.

It is also the moral seriousness. Ghibli films do not offer simple villains. Lady Eboshi in *Princess Mononoke* destroys the forest, but she also employs former prostitutes and lepers, giving them dignity and purpose. The witch Yubaba in *Spirited Away* is greedy and authoritarian, but she is not evil. She runs a bathhouse, not a crime syndicate; her sins are those of a hotel manager with too much power and not enough sleep. Miyazaki once wrote in a memo regarding *Porco Rosso*: "We must treat every character respectfully. We must love their foolishness." [Vulture](https://www.vulture.com/article/ghibli-portrait-chatgpt-ai.html) It is a philosophy that would disarm most Hollywood screenwriters, who have been trained to love only their heroes.

And it is the heroines. From **Nausicaa** — a princess who studies the toxic jungle rather than fighting it, which is a level of ecological diplomacy most adults cannot manage — to **San** — the wolf-raised warrior of *Princess Mononoke* — to **Chihiro** — an ordinary, frightened girl who finds her courage through forced labor in a spirit bathhouse, which is really just a metaphor for growing up — Ghibli has produced a gallery of female protagonists that stands alone in animation. Nine of Miyazaki's thirteen feature films have female leads. [Academia.edu](https://www.academia.edu/37957539/Hayao_Miyazaki_and_Studio_Ghibli_Reimagining_the_Portrayal_of_Women_in_Japanese_Anime) They are not sexualized, not rescued, not waiting for princes. They work, they fight, they cry, they grow. They are, as one critic put it, "a far cry from the classic damsel in distress trope." [MovieWeb](https://movieweb.com/female-characters-hayao-miyazaki-films) They do not need a prince; they need a bath, a meal, and a reason to keep going.

"Miyazaki's heroines demand attention through an implicit desire for independence and a hell of a lot of girl power," wrote one analyst. [Film Daily](https://filmdaily.co/obsessions/studio-ghiblis-female-heroines/) In Japan, where the World Economic Forum's Global Gender Gap Report ranked the country 111th out of 144 nations in 2016, this is not a small thing. It is, in fact, the quietest kind of revolution: one that does not march but draws.

## IV. The Empire, Reluctantly Built

For decades, Ghibli resisted expansion. There was no merchandise strategy. The **Ghibli Museum**, which opened in Mitaka, Tokyo in 2001, was designed by Miyazaki himself — a whimsical, maze-like building with stained-glass windows, a rooftop robot from *Castle in the Sky*, and an exclusive short film theater that shows work available nowhere else. Tickets must be reserved months in advance. Photography inside is largely forbidden. It is, by design, an experience of scarcity. [Ghibli Museum](https://www.ghibli-museum.jp/en/) One does not merely visit the Ghibli Museum; one is invited, if one has been patient enough and planned far enough ahead.

**Ghibli Park**, which opened in phases from 2022 to 2024, follows the same philosophy. There are no thrill rides. The park is designed for walking, for looking, for being inside the worlds of the films. Its five areas — Ghibli's Grand Warehouse, Hill of Youth, Dondoko Forest, Mononoke Village, and Valley of Witches — are less theme park and more museum of atmosphere. The park attracted approximately 1.8 million visitors in its first full year. The economic ripple effect is estimated at ¥71 billion ($710 million) annually, 1.48 times initial projections. [Aichi Prefecture](https://www.pref.aichi.jp/press-release/ghiblipark-hakyuukouka.html) The studio that refused commerce has created an economic ecosystem that most corporations would sign over their firstborn to inherit.

The streaming deals were the biggest shift. In 2020, after years of resistance, Ghibli licensed its entire catalog to Netflix (outside the US and Japan) and to HBO Max (in North America). It was a reluctant embrace of the future, driven by producer Toshio Suzuki, who said: "We've listened to our fans and have made the definitive decision to stream our film catalog." [Netflix](https://about.netflix.com/en/news/netflix-releases-21-studio-ghibli-masterpieces-around-the-world) The translation, one suspects, was: "We have run out of ways to say no."

The effect was immediate. A whole new generation discovered Ghibli. By 2025, Studio Ghibli films collectively ranked third among all anime-related content on Netflix, behind only *Demon Slayer* and *Naruto*, with 39.1 million views in six months. [CBR](https://www.cbr.com/netflix-data-report-anime-warning/) The studio that built its reputation on scarcity became, against its better judgment, a streaming juggernaut.

Then came the most consequential business move of all. In October 2023, Studio Ghibli was acquired by **Nippon Television Holdings**, becoming a subsidiary of the Japanese broadcaster. Nippon TV took a 42.3% stake. Hiroyuki Fukuda, a senior NTV executive, became president. Suzuki became chairman. Miyazaki became Honorary Chairman. The move was driven by the plain arithmetic of age: Miyazaki was 82, Suzuki 75. The studio's succession planning — always a fraught topic — had run into the wall of reality. Goro Miyazaki, Hayao's son and a director in his own right, declined to take over leadership of the studio. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Studio_Ghibli) It is a rare son who understands that inheriting the family business is sometimes the cruellest thing a father can offer.

Toshio Suzuki, reflecting on the sale, told *The New York Times* that the whole purpose of Studio Ghibli was to make Miyazaki films. "Give me a break," he recalled saying when Miyazaki brought him yet another new film idea after his third retirement. But he could never say no for long. [CBR](https://www.cbr.com/studio-ghibli-hayao-miyazaki-oscars-new-video-message/) Some men are constitutionally incapable of disappointing genius, even when they know they should.

## V. The Man Who Will Not Stop

Hayao Miyazaki has retired four times. The first was after *Princess Mononoke* (1997). Then *Spirited Away* (2001). Then *The Wind Rises* (2013). Each retirement announcement was treated with skepticism; each time, he came back. One begins to suspect that Miyazaki's retirements are merely a form of narcolepsy — he announces them, falls asleep, and wakes up with a new film.

"I know I've mentioned I'm retiring many times in the past," he told a press conference in 2013. "I know that many of you might think, 'oh again.' This time is for real." [BBC](http://www.bbc.com/culture/article/20231208-the-boy-and-the-heron-inside-the-dark-heart-of-studio-ghibils-latest-animated-masterpiece)

It was not. In 2016, three years after that press conference, he told Toshio Suzuki he had an idea. "It was only three years after he announced his retirement," Suzuki later recalled. "At that time, I honestly believed that we wouldn't be making any more films together." [The Hollywood Reporter](https://www.hollywoodreporter.com/movies/movie-features/hayao-miyazaki-out-of-retirement-the-boy-and-heron-1235823099/)

Suzuki spent a weekend reading the storyboards. He knew it was good. He drove to the studio planning to say no. When he arrived, Miyazaki did something he had rarely done: he opened the door for Suzuki, asked if he wanted coffee. "Just seeing that, all of my objections just flew away," Suzuki said. It is the oldest trick in the book — politeness as disarmament — and it worked because Miyazaki had never bothered to use it before.

The film became *The Boy and the Heron* (2023), the most expensive Japanese animated film ever produced, seven years in the making. It is a strange, semi-autobiographical fantasy about a boy mourning his mother, set against the firebombing of Tokyo. It won the Golden Globe, the BAFTA, and the Academy Award for Best Animated Feature. At 83, Miyazaki became the oldest director ever to win the category. [Variety](https://variety.com/2024/awards/awards/oscars-hayao-miyazaki-the-boy-and-the-heron-1235937629/) The prize for persistence, one might call it, though the film deserved it on its own terms.

"Miyazaki is a generational talent but so much of the context around him allowed him to do what he did," says Justin Johnson, head of cinema programming at the British Film Institute. "There'll be no other Steven Spielberg, because what he created in the seven-year period from *Jaws* to *E.T.* can't be replicated. It's the same with Paul McCartney. When they go, that's the end of the spirit they captured and embodied." [The Observer](https://observer.co.uk/culture/film/article/where-is-studio-ghibli-going-next)

But Miyazaki is still here. Studio Ghibli vice president Junichi Nishioka told the BBC in 2023: "Even now there are new ideas that he talks about. He will have a pencil in his hand until the very day that he dies." [BBC](http://www.bbc.com/culture/article/20231208-the-boy-and-the-heron-inside-the-dark-heart-of-studio-ghibils-latest-animated-masterpiece) Which is to say: he will work until he cannot, and then he will work some more.

## VI. The Insult to Life

In 2016, a documentary crew filmed Miyazaki as a team of AI researchers demonstrated a computer-generated animation: a grotesque creature crawling with no legs, its head dragging its body. Miyazaki watched in silence. Then he spoke.

"Whoever creates this stuff has no idea what pain is whatsoever. I am utterly disgusted." He paused. "If you really want to make creepy stuff, you can go ahead and do it. I would never wish to incorporate this technology into my work at all. I strongly feel that this is an insult to life itself." [Fortune](https://fortune.com/2025/03/27/chatgpt-studio-ghibli-hayao-miyazaki-openai-altman-copyright-lawsuit)

Nearly a decade later, in March 2025, OpenAI released its GPT-4o image generator. Users quickly discovered it could produce images in the unmistakable style of Studio Ghibli — soft lighting, round faces, lush landscapes, the whole visual vocabulary. Within days, social media was flooded with "Ghiblified" memes: the September 11 attacks rendered as a Miyazaki scene, the arrest of a weeping immigrant woman turned into a Ghibli still, family photos transformed into nostalgic fantasy. The internet had found a way to do in seconds what took Miyazaki a lifetime, and it used this power primarily to make terrible things look innocent.

OpenAI CEO Sam Altman changed his X profile picture to a Ghibli-style portrait of himself. The White House official X account posted a Ghibli-fied image of a woman in ICE custody. The tool was so popular that Altman said it was "melting" OpenAI's servers. The company initially delayed rollout to free users. [Fast Company](https://www.fastcompany.com/91308222/openais-studio-ghibli-style-images-renew-the-debate-over-ai-and-copyright)

OpenAI's position was that it prevented generation "in the style of individual living artists" but permitted "broader studio styles." An OpenAI spokesperson told 404 Media: "Our goal is to give users as much creative freedom as possible." [Animation Magazine](https://www.animationmagazine.net/2025/03/an-insult-to-life-itself-openais-new-tech-fuels-ghibli-imitation-trend-raises-questions/) It is a distinction so fine as to be invisible: the studio style is, in this case, inseparable from the artists who created it. But that is the sort of subtlety that corporate policy is designed to ignore.

The irony was savage. The studio that spends years hand-drawing every frame — Miyazaki once said "my process is thinking, thinking, and thinking, thinking about my stories for a long time" — had been reduced to a filter. The man who called AI "an insult to life itself" saw his life's work turned into a Photoshop preset. It is the sort of cosmic joke that would make a lesser man weep, and perhaps Miyazaki did, though he has not said so.

The controversy became a flashpoint in the broader debate about AI and copyright. Artists pointed out that OpenAI had trained its model on a vast corpus of images, likely including Ghibli's copyrighted work, without permission or compensation. The artist Karla Ortiz, who is suing AI companies, called it "another clear example of how companies like OpenAI just do not care about the work of artists and the livelihoods of artists." [Malay Mail](https://www.malaymail.com/news/tech-gadgets/2025/03/28/openais-viral-ghibli-style-image-tool-under-fire-amid-lawsuits-and-miyazaki-condemnation/171147)

Studio Ghibli itself has not commented. Miyazaki, now 85, has not responded publicly. Perhaps he is too busy working. Or perhaps, as one critic suggested, the trend "burns out sooner than later" — the accelerationist tendencies inherent to the form. By April, the internet had largely moved on to generating Muppet-style images instead. [Vulture](https://www.vulture.com/article/ghibli-portrait-chatgpt-ai.html) The attention span of the internet is shorter than a mayfly's; but the damage, as always, lingers longer.

But the question lingers: what does it mean when the most distinctive visual style in animation history can be replicated by anyone with a text prompt? It means that we have traded mastery for convenience, and we have not yet decided whether we regret the exchange.

## VII. What Remains

Studio Ghibli at 41 is a paradox. It has never been more commercially successful; it has never been more fragile. Like a man who wins the lottery on the same day he receives a terminal diagnosis, it finds itself rich in everything except time.

The streaming deals have brought the films to hundreds of millions of new viewers. The park draws crowds from around the world. A stage adaptation of *My Neighbor Totoro* at London's Barbican Theatre became the fastest-selling show in the venue's history, outselling Benedict Cumberbatch in *Hamlet* and Cate Blanchett in *The Seagull*. [The Observer](https://observer.co.uk/culture/film/article/where-is-studio-ghibli-going-next) It is a remarkable achievement for a story about two sisters and a forest spirit that has no villain and no plot — which is to say, for a story that trusts its audience to be human.

And yet the core question remains unanswered: **What happens when Miyazaki stops?** And after Suzuki? The studio has no obvious successor. Goro Miyazaki has directed three films (*Tales from Earthsea*, *From Up on Poppy Hill*, *Earwig and the Witch*) to mixed reviews. Other Ghibli directors — Hiromasa Yonebayashi (*When Marnie Was There*), Yoshifumi Kondo (who died in 1998) — have not filled the void. The Nippon TV acquisition ensures financial stability, but it is a corporate solution to a creative problem. One does not solve the question of soul by hiring a management consultant.

Toshio Suzuki has said, "The whole purpose of Studio Ghibli is to make Miyazaki films." One imagines Nippon TV's executives have a slightly different view. They have signaled plans for more theatrical adaptations, touring exhibitions, expanded streaming. They are keeping the engine running. But the soul of the studio — the uncompromising, hand-drawn, morally serious filmmaking that *is* Ghibli — depends on people who are not getting younger.

On a spring afternoon in 2024, Miyazaki accepted his second Oscar without leaving Tokyo. Producer Toshio Suzuki sent a statement: "Both Hayao Miyazaki and I have aged a considerable amount. I am grateful to receive such an honor at my age, and taking this as a message to continue our work, I will devote myself to work harder in the future." [Variety](https://variety.com/2024/awards/awards/oscars-hayao-miyazaki-the-boy-and-the-heron-1235937629/)

Work harder. At 83 and 76, respectively. It is either the most inspiring or the most tragic sentence ever written about the twilight of a great studio. Perhaps both.

Perhaps the most Ghibli-like thing the studio could do is refuse a neat ending. Miyazaki might make one more film. He might not. The catalog will endure: *Totoro*, *Spirited Away*, *Princess Mononoke*, *Grave of the Fireflies*, *Kiki's Delivery Service*, *Howl's Moving Castle*, *The Boy and the Heron* — these films will be watched, and loved, and studied, as long as people watch animation. They will outlive their creators, as all great things must.

The wind that Miyazaki, Takahata, and Suzuki set out to blow through the anime industry in 1985 became something larger than they could have imagined. It shaped the childhoods of millions. It changed what animation could be. It produced images — a girl riding a dragon, a forest spirit glowing at dusk, a Catbus racing through the trees — that have entered the permanent visual vocabulary of human culture. We cannot unsee them, and we would not want to.

The wind is still blowing. For how much longer, nobody knows. Not even Miyazaki. Perhaps especially not Miyazaki — for the man who creates the wind is rarely the one who can predict where it will go.
