### Scene Setting: June 23, 1757, Plassey, Bengal
- Physical location: Mango orchard near Palashi village
- Forces: 3,000 British (mostly sepoys) vs. 50,000 Bengali army
- Outcome: British victory transforms Company from trader to ruler

- How did a merchant company, chartered 57 years earlier, come to rule one of the world's richest regions?
- Why Britain, not France or Portugal?

1. **Fiscal-Military Innovation**: Company's ability to extract revenue and fund perpetual armies

### CHAPTER 1: The Royal Charter (1600-1613)

#### Core Thesis (2,000 words)
The East India Company began as a modest trading venture, but its royal charter granted monopoly privileges that would become the legal foundation for sovereignty.

#### Key Events
**1600**: Elizabeth I grants EIC charter (30-year monopoly)
- Purpose: Compete with Portuguese spice trade
- Scope: Trade between Cape of Good Hope and Straits of Magellan
- Legal basis: Exclusive rights to English trade with "East Indies"

**1608**: First factory at Masulipatam (Captain Henry Finch)
- Location: Coromandel Coast (south India)
- Significance: First permanent trading post

**1612**: Sir Thomas Rogers defeats Portuguese at Swally (Suval)
- Battle: English naval victory off Surat coast
- Outcome: Portuguese naval dominance weakened
- Scholarly focus: G.J. Bryant on early naval strategy

**1613**: Jahangir grants permission to establish at Surat
- Firman (decree) from Mughal Emperor
- Right to build factory, trade freely
- First major Mughal-English diplomatic breakthrough

**1615**: Sir Thomas Roe's embassy to Mughal court
- Diplomatic mission: Secure broader trading rights
- Agra audience: Jahangir's court
- Outcome: Limited success, but established diplomatic channel

#### Narrative Structure
1. Opening: 1600 charter announcement, London merchants' excitement
2. First voyages: Challenges of Indian Ocean navigation
3. Early encounters: Portuguese rivalry, Mughal bureaucracy navigation
4. Roe's embassy: Diplomatic drama, Mughal grandeur
5. Closing: Company still a trader, but legal foundation laid

#### Primary Sources (when possible)
- EIC Court Minutes 1600 (charter grant)
- Sir Thomas Roe's Embassy accounts (1615)
- Contemporary travel accounts (early 1600s)

#### Scholarly Secondary Sources
- Robert Travers: Early Company ideology
- C.A. Bayly: Pre-colonial Indian state context
- Tirthankar Roy: Economic foundations of early trade
- John Richards: Mughal-English diplomatic relations

#### Writing Notes
- Emphasize Company's modest ambitions (trade only)
- Highlight Mughal bureaucracy's openness to foreign trade
- Note Portuguese decline as Company opportunity
- Avoid anachronistic imperial language (Company still trading)
